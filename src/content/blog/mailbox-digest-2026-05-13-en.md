# Middle East Energy Disruptions, Shipping Shifts, Brent Baseline, and Global Manufacturing Costs

Date anchor: this analysis uses the machine date `2026-05-13` as “today”; “recent” refers to information available through May 12/13, 2026.

## Prioritized Conclusions

1. **The Brent baseline has shifted from a “geopolitical risk premium” to a “real supply-loss baseline.”** EIA’s May STEO puts the 2026 Brent average at **$95/bbl** and expects Brent to stay near **$106/bbl** in May-June; April spot Brent averaged **$117/bbl** and reached **$138/bbl** on April 7. The key assumption is that the Strait of Hormuz remains “effectively closed” through late May, traffic starts to recover in June, and flows move back toward pre-conflict levels later in the year.[EIA, 2026-05-12](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press588.php)

2. **The supply shock is large enough to dominate demand softness.** EIA estimates that Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, UAE, Qatar, and Bahrain collectively shut in **10.5 mb/d** of crude output in April, while global inventories fall by an average **8.5 mb/d** in 2Q26.[EIA, 2026-05-12](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press588.php) The World Bank estimates global oil supply fell **10.1 mb/d** in March, sees a **3.7 mb/d** 2Q26 market deficit, and forecasts a 2026 Brent baseline of **$86/bbl**; if disruptions last longer or recovery is slower, the annual average could sit in a **$95-115/bbl** range.[World Bank, 2026-05-08](https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/opendata/strait-of-hormuz-disruption-sends-oil-prices-surging)

3. **The manufacturing-cost pass-through is not just oil; it is a four-channel shock across fuel, shipping, gas/power, and raw materials.** Freightos notes that the Hormuz closure directly disrupts about **20%** of global oil flows but only about **2-3%** of container volumes; therefore the main container impact is not volume loss but fuel and surcharges. Carriers announced **$150-400/FEU** emergency fuel surcharges and began pushing GRIs/surcharges on non-Gulf lanes.[Freightos, 2026-03-18](https://www.freightos.com/logistics-technology-insights/industry-updates/freightos-global-freight-outlook-march-2026/) AP also reports that the Hormuz closure is squeezing bunker-fuel supply; roughly **80%** of globally traded goods move by sea, with Asia and Singapore’s bunkering hub hit first.[AP News, 2026-05-12](https://apnews.com/article/middle-east-wars-energy-asia-shipping-bunker-fuel-db0ba1dbc0bd3ff2179a84118d0064d0)

4. **Manufacturing cost pressure is higher in Asia and Europe than in the United States.** EIA data show that after February 28, front-month TTF rose to **$14.80/MMBtu**, up **35%** from before the closure; JKM rose to **$16.02/MMBtu**, up **51%**; Henry Hub fell **9%** over the same period. More than **10 Bcf/d** of LNG supply, about **20%** of the global market, has been affected, and no known laden LNG carrier crossed Hormuz from March 1 to April 24.[EIA Today in Energy, 2026-04-29](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67604) This makes power, steam, chemicals, glass, steel, and semiconductor-input costs more sensitive in Asia and Europe.

5. **Red Sea / Bab el-Mandeb rerouting makes the cost shock stickier.** Freightos says Houthi threats pushed carriers that had been tentatively returning to the Red Sea back away from the Southern Red Sea.[Freightos, 2026-03-18](https://www.freightos.com/logistics-technology-insights/industry-updates/freightos-global-freight-outlook-march-2026/) Sustained Cape of Good Hope routing typically adds **10-14 days** to Asia-Europe voyages and raises fuel consumption by about **30-40%**; with oil elevated, that raises lead times, working-capital needs, and freight surcharges at the same time.[CXTMS, 2026-05-05](https://cxtms.com/blog/2026-05-05-red-sea-bab-el-mandeb-shipping-financial-toll-2026)
