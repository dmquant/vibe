# Beijing Summit Trade Intentions vs. Fed "Higher-for-Longer" Dollar Supply Expectations

**Author:** Global Macro Analyst
**Date:** 2026-05-15 (anchored)
**To:** Thematic Researcher · Morning Thematic Rotation Watch (run 5d0c2d80-2ccc-4e28-b0af-08708291b3ed)
**Confidence:** Medium-high (data aligned to summit-close day + April FOMC + April CPI / March PCE)

---

## 1. Bottom line (read this first)

**No.** The trade intentions announced at the Beijing summit (May 13–14, 2026) are **not** sufficient — over the next 1–2 FOMC cycles — to shift the Fed's dollar-supply expectations under the "higher-for-longer" framework. I put the probability of a material dovish shift in the Fed reaction function attributable to the summit at **<15%**. Three reasons, ranked by weight:

1. **Intentions ≠ enforceable agreements.** The summit produced a framework ("constructive U.S.–China relationship of strategic stability"), an aspirational ~$1 trillion Chinese purchase basket (Boeing, soybeans, beef, advanced semis), a rare-earth export-restriction pause, and modest tariff scale-backs — but **no enforcement mechanism**. Historical base rate: China completed only ~58% of its Phase One (Jan 2020) purchase commitments. Until there is a credible execution clause, the FOMC will not embed these into its baseline.
2. **The inflation shock dominates the reaction function; trade relief is noise.** March headline PCE printed 3.5% YoY (Feb: 2.8%), core PCE 3.2% (Feb: 2.9%), and April CPI 3.8% — the highest reading since May 2023 — against an Iran-war energy shock that is still building. Even if tariff relief delivers 10–25 bps of goods disinflation, the energy passthrough swamps it. Three regional Fed presidents dissented at the April 29 meeting against forward guidance that hinted at eventual cuts; Boston's Collins publicly aligned with them on May 7. The risk is asymmetrically toward a *resumed hike*, not toward easing.
3. **"Dollar supply" channels are nearly orthogonal to trade policy.** The Fed's dollar-supply toolkit (SOMA / QT, SRF, FIMA repo, standing FX swap lines) is triggered by **USD funding-market stress**, not by trade policy. A trade truce *reduces* funding-stress tail risk, which means the Fed needs *less* dollar supply — fully consistent with, not contrary to, "higher for longer."

**Actionable read for the Thematic Researcher:** Any thesis that chains "Summit → Fed pivots dovish → USD supply loosens → risk-on / EM re-rate" breaks at link #2. **Do not** use the Beijing summit as the primary catalyst for short-USD or long-EM thematic trades. If you want to keep the theme, anchor it to the **June CPI + July FOMC** print under an Iran-de-escalation scenario, not to trade intentions themselves.

# Utilities Analyst Handoff: Impact of Hyperscaler Behind-the-Meter Nuclear/SMR Self-Generation on Traditional Grid Utility Valuation

Date anchor: `date +%Y-%m-%d` = **2026-05-15**. All “current,” “near term,” and “to 2030” references below are anchored to that date.

## Conclusions First

1. **This is not a simple “utilities lose load” story. But the load still needs interconnection, backup, capacity, dispatch support, fault support, and T&D upgrades. Grid companies that lock those services into long-term minimum bills, standby charges, exit fees, and collateral requirements have a more defensible valuation case.

2. **Before 2030, SMRs are more of an option value and bargaining tool than a scaled grid bypass.** Google/Kairos is targeting a first reactor in 2030 and 500 MW by 2035; Amazon/X-energy is targeting more than 5 GW by 2039. Those timelines lag the fastest phase of data center load growth. IEA says the United States accounted for 45% of global data center electricity use in 2024 and that data centers contribute nearly half of U.S. electricity demand growth to 2030; EIA forecasts U.S. commercial-sector electricity sales growth of 2.2% in 2026 and 5.3% in 2027. Near-term demand growth still largely falls on the traditional grid, gas, renewables, nuclear restarts, and T&D investment.

3. **The largest winners are not all “traditional utilities,” but three specific groups:** first, owners of existing or restartable nuclear assets that can sign premium long-term contracts; second, T&D or integrated utilities in data center hot spots that have approved large-load tariff protection; third, RTOs/utilities that turn BTM generation into a paid “grid-as-platform” service. Conversely, companies capitalizing uncontracted data center queue MW without minimum charges or cost-allocation protection deserve a discount.

4. **Regulators are turning “grid-bypass arbitrage” into “paid use of grid capability.”** The Talen/AWS Susquehanna case shows FERC will not automatically allow large loads to move nuclear output out of wholesale markets and behind the meter. In November 2024, FERC rejected an ISA amendment that would have raised co-located load from 300 MW to 480 MW; in December 2025, it required PJM to create transparent co-location rules and service charges. AEP Ohio’s data center tariff, which requires large new data centers to pay for at least 85% of contracted capacity for up to 12 years, is the kind of protection clause that should matter in valuation models.
