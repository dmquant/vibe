## 2. Feasibility and Persistence of a Hormuz Blockade

### 2.1 The chokepoint, quantified

| Metric | Value | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Crude + condensate flow | ~20–21 mbpd | ~30% of seaborne crude, ~20% of global liquids supply (EIA, 2024 baseline) |
| LNG flow | ~20% of global | Concentrated in Qatar (QatarEnergy) |
| Narrowest point / shipping lanes | 33 km / ~3 km | Two 2-nm lanes inbound/outbound |
| Bypass pipeline capacity | ~6.5 mbpd | Saudi East-West + UAE ADCOP at full rate — < 1/3 of throughput |

The bypass capacity gap is the key fact: the world physically cannot reroute around a closed Hormuz, so even a partial disruption translates one-for-one into a binding supply constraint and a price response.

### 2.2 Incentive matrix

| Actor | Incentive to close | Binding constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Iran (IRGC) | Retaliation against sanctions or US/Israel strikes; geopolitical leverage | Loses its own 1.5–1.7 mbpd export channel; fiscal collapse risk; pressure from China |
| China | None | ~50% of crude imports come from the Gulf — the loudest opposing voice |
| US / allied navies | None | 5th Fleet + CTF-153 can restore traffic within ~30 days |
| GCC (Saudi, UAE) | None | Inadequate bypass capacity, fiscal pain |

### 2.3 Probability distribution (subjective, next 6 months)

- **Full closure ≥ 30 days:** 10–15% — only credible if Iranian homeland depth targets are struck directly
- **Intermittent disruption / partial blockade** (mines, seizures, GPS spoofing, insurer withdrawal): 30–40%
- **Low-intensity standoff, no material disruption:** 45–55%

Historical calibration: during the 1984–88 Tanker War, 451 vessels were attacked but **the strait never actually closed**. War-risk insurance premia jumped from ~0.025% to ~0.5% of hull value, but tankers kept transiting. The takeaway: **conflict reliably lifts oil and shipping/insurance costs; it rarely cuts physical flow.**

### 2.4 Early-warning indicators (for the data scientist's signal panel)

1. **VLCC War Risk premium** (Lloyd's JCC list) — > 0.5% of hull = high alert
2. **AIS signal-loss rate** at Gulf terminals (Kharg, Ras Tanura, Fujairah)
3. **OPEC+ spare capacity utilisation** — Saudi + UAE ~3.5 mbpd buffer is the cushion
4. **Brent–Dubai EFS** spread — widening prices Asian-flow risk
5. **US CSG deployment** — ≥ 1 carrier strike group in-theatre = elevated state

## 3. Inflation Pass-Through if Oil Sits Above $100

### 3.1 Empirical elasticities

Convergent estimates from IMF WEO 2023 ch.1, ECB Economic Bulletin 2023/4, BIS WP 1131, and Cleveland Fed (2022):

| Economy | Headline CPI peak response per +$10/bbl sustained shock (6–12m) | Core CPI response |
|---|---|---|
| US | +0.20 to +0.35 ppt | +0.05 to +0.10 ppt |
| Euro area | +0.30 to +0.50 ppt | +0.08 to +0.15 ppt |
| Japan | +0.25 to +0.45 ppt (+0.05–0.10 add'l from JPY weakness) | +0.07 to +0.12 ppt |
| China | +0.10 to +0.20 ppt (administered fuel pricing dampens) | +0.03 to +0.07 ppt |
| EM average | +0.30 to +0.60 ppt | +0.10 to +0.20 ppt |

Drivers of cross-country dispersion:
- **Energy weight in the CPI basket**: US ~7%, EZ ~10%, Japan ~7% — but Europe/Japan retail tax structure raises the effective elasticity
- **FX channel**: USD-priced oil hits non-USD economies harder; the 2026 prints of EUR/USD and JPY/USD set the net pass-through
- **Policy buffers**: a re-run of the 2022–23 European energy subsidies / price caps could absorb 30–50% of the headline shock

### 3.2 Central estimate for sustained $100+

Assume Brent jumps from a $72 baseline to a $100–110 range and holds for six months (ΔP ≈ +$30/bbl):

| Economy | Headline CPI uplift (ppt, 6–12m window) | Core uplift (ppt) |
|---|---|---|
| US | +0.6 to +1.0 | +0.15 to +0.30 |
| Euro area | +0.9 to +1.5 | +0.25 to +0.45 |
| Japan | +0.8 to +1.3 (incl. FX) | +0.20 to +0.35 |
| China | +0.3 to +0.6 | +0.10 to +0.20 |

**Tail scenario** (Brent peak $140, 3-month average $115, then mean-reverts): peak pass-through is ≈ 1.3–1.5× the table above, but the duration is shorter (3–4 months); the YoY annual impact ends up similar.

### 3.3 Second-round effects: reaction functions and re-anchoring risk

- **Fed**: core PCE remains the policy anchor. If the first three months show no clear monthly core uptick and 5y5y breakevens stay below 2.5%, Powell will lean on look-through. But six-plus months of $100+ would block the 2026H2 cuts and potentially re-open hike debate; OIS can re-price 25–50 bp quickly.
- **ECB**: more sensitive than Fed — higher energy basket weight plus collectively-bargained wage indexation channels (German IG Metall, France SMIC) create an automatic second-round mechanism. **Most exposed central bank.**
- **BoJ**: with 2% target now established, imported inflation + JPY weakness could accelerate normalisation — but the real-income hit creates a policy dilemma.
- **EM**: large net-importers (India, Turkey, Philippines) face FX/CA pressure that often forces hike cycles.

### 3.4 GDP and recession risk

Standard heuristic: each sustained +$10/bbl trims advanced-economy GDP by 0.10–0.20 ppt over a year. A $30 shock → -0.3 to -0.6 ppt: euro area and Japan flirt with technical-recession territory; the US has more cushion (shale + net energy exporter status), but consumer sentiment and services PMIs become the watch items.
