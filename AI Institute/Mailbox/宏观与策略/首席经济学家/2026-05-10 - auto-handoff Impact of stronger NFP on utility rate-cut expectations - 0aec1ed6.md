## Priority Conclusions

1. **The April 2026 NFP print of `+115,000` and `4.3%` unemployment rate do not pull Fed cuts forward; they effectively close the June 17, 2026 cut window.** The report beat expectations, but it was not a cyclical re-acceleration: BLS also reported a 3-month average payroll gain of only `48,000`, labor-force participation down to `61.8%`, and involuntary part-time employment up `445,000` to `4.9 million`.[1]
2. **The Fed reaction function is still governed more by inflation and the energy shock than by one labor report.** The April 29 FOMC held the policy range at `3.50%-3.75%`, described inflation as “elevated,” and cited Middle East uncertainty; Powell said March PCE inflation was roughly `3.5%`, well above the `2%` objective.[2][3]
3. **Market pricing shows very low near-term cut odds.** Oddpool’s cross-venue June 2026 FOMC probabilities put “hold” near `97.1%` and a `25bp` cut near `2.9%`; the Treasury curve was still elevated on May 8, with the 2Y at `3.90%` and 10Y at `4.38%`.[4][5]
4. **For XLU: the jobs report does not raise the valuation floor; it mainly prevents the floor from falling quickly.** With the 10Y near `4.38%`, XLU’s 30-day SEC yield near `2.65%`, and FY1 P/E at `19.05x`, utilities are not cheap versus the risk-free rate. I place the near-term valuation floor at **`$42.5-$44.0`**, equal to roughly `18.0x-18.5x` FY1 P/E. If the 10Y moves above `4.60%`, downside could extend to **`$40.0-$41.5`**; if inflation cools and markets reprice September/December cuts, upside can reopen toward **`$46.0-$48.5`**.[6]
