# 2026-05-11 | Card 02 | AI Compute Capex Re-acceleration and A/H Sector Rotation

- **Board**: 2e229809-f290-491d-bba7-3b06f10b0d3e · Card 02 / 08
- **Analyst**: Chief Strategist (chief-strategist)
- **Date (work-date, Asia/Singapore)**: 2026-05-11
- **Stance**: synthesize · **Confidence**: 0.67

As of **2026-05-11**, I synthesize Card 01's Jevons/mix-shift thesis and support a systematic A-share / Hong Kong allocation rotation, with one qualification: the rotation should not be framed as "AI hardware only." If 2026-2027 AI compute capex is re-accelerating by more than 15% YoY, the investable chain broadens into **industrials + TMT + utilities**, because power access, HVDC/UHV delivery, copper intensity, 800G/1.6T optics, and automation capacity become the binding constraints.

## 1. Bottom Line

The >15% YoY capex trigger is already surpassed in the global data now visible. TrendForce raised its 2026 capex forecast for the top nine CSPs to **US$830 billion**, with YoY growth lifted from **61% to 79%**; Microsoft and Google are each around **US$190 billion**, Meta raised guidance to **US$125-145 billion**, and Amazon has guided to roughly **US$200 billion** [1][2][3][4]. This is no longer a narrow semiconductor cycle. It is an infrastructure capex cycle with an AI demand driver.

For A/H strategy, I would run a dedicated "AI physical infrastructure" equity sleeve with this ranking:

| Rank | Line | Weight | Preferred A/H expression | Strategy view |
|---:|---|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Industrials: grid equipment, HVDC/UHV, transformers, switchgear, automation, copper-linked equipment | **40%** | A-share dominant; selective HK industrials/metals | Best earnings breadth and strongest China policy overlap |
| 2 | TMT: 800G/1.6T optics, CPO/silicon photonics, AI PCB, servers, cooling, domestic inference silicon | **35%** | A-share optics/PCB + HK cloud/internet AI monetization | Highest beta, but already more crowded |
| 3 | Utilities: power operators, regulated grid-adjacent assets, storage, gas/nuclear/renewables beneficiaries | **25%** | A-share power + HK yield utilities | More defensive; tariff and capex-cycle pass-through are slower |

Within a China equity portfolio, I would size this as a **12-18% thematic overweight** versus benchmark, initially split **75% A-share / 25% Hong Kong**. A-shares have the deeper listed supply chain in electrical equipment, optics, automation and copper; Hong Kong adds cloud/platform monetization and yield-oriented utilities.

## 2. Why This Is Systemic, Not Just Thematic

**First, capex scale has crossed the macro-relevant line.** Microsoft disclosed FY26 Q3 capex of **US$31.9 billion**, with roughly two-thirds in short-lived assets such as GPUs and CPUs, and expects roughly **US$190 billion** of calendar-year 2026 capex while still being capacity-constrained through at least 2026 [2]. Alphabet raised 2026 capex guidance to **US$180-190 billion** and said 2027 capex should "significantly increase" versus 2026, backed by Google Cloud backlog of **US$462 billion** and direct API use of more than **16 billion tokens per minute** [3]. Meta lifted 2026 capex to **US$125-145 billion**, citing higher component pricing and future-year data-center capacity [4]. Amazon says it is investing approximately **US$200 billion** in 2026 capex and that a substantial portion of AWS capex already has customer commitments [5].

**Second, the bottleneck is rotating into physical infrastructure.** IEA estimates data centers consumed **415 TWh** in 2024, or about **1.5%** of global electricity consumption, and sees that more than doubling to around **945 TWh by 2030**. It also estimates roughly **20%** of planned data-center projects could face delays unless grid risks are addressed, with transmission lines taking **4-8 years** in advanced economies and wait times for transformers and cables doubling in three years [6]. That is why the allocation must include power equipment and utilities, not only chips.

**Third, China's domestic cycle is already aligned with the same direction.** In 1Q26, China fixed-asset investment rose **1.7% YoY**, manufacturing investment rose **4.1%**, infrastructure investment rose **8.9%**, and investment in electricity/heat production and supply rose **9.2%** [7]. The April 2026 NBS manufacturing PMI was **50.3**, with production at **51.5**, purchase quantity at **51.1**, raw-material purchase prices at **63.7**, and producer prices at **55.1** [8]. This is exactly the macro tape in which an AI capex shock can push selected industrials from "theme" into earnings revision.

## 3. Sector Ordering

### 1) Industrials: 40%

This is the highest conviction line because the market often treats AI infrastructure as TMT, while the real scarcity is increasingly in **power delivery and factory capacity**. The core should be HVDC/UHV equipment, transformers, switchgear, intelligent substations, power electronics, liquid-cooling components, precision manufacturing, industrial control systems, and copper-linked electrical equipment.

China policy reinforces the trade. State Grid invested **RMB75.7 billion** in fixed assets in January-February 2026, up **80.6% YoY**, while advancing UHV transmission lines, pumped-storage stations and transmission/transformation projects [9]. Separately, China allocated **RMB185.1 billion** of ultra-long special treasury-bond funding for 2026 equipment upgrades, supporting more than **6,700** projects and driving more than **RMB380 billion** of total investment; equipment and tools investment rose **13.9% YoY** in 1Q26 [10].

Recommended sub-weights inside the 40% industrials line:

| Sub-sleeve | Weight | Rationale |
|---|---:|---|
| HVDC/UHV, transformers, switchgear, substations | 16% | Most direct power-bottleneck beneficiary |
| Power electronics, liquid cooling, data-center electrical systems | 8% | AI rack density lifts electrical and thermal intensity |
| Industrial automation, robotics, inspection and factory equipment | 8% | Second-order beneficiary as grid and optics suppliers expand capacity |
| Copper-linked electrical equipment and selected non-ferrous exposure | 8% | Commodity beta with equipment pass-through |

### 2) TMT: 35%

TMT keeps the strongest revenue beta, but the risk-adjusted ranking is second because valuation and positioning are already more advanced. The focus should be optical communication, silicon photonics/CPO, 800G/1.6T modules, AI PCB, high-speed connectors, servers, cooling, and domestic inference silicon rather than generic software.

TrendForce estimates the global shipment share of **800G and above** optical transceivers will rise from **19.5% in 2024** to **over 60% by 2026**, and says Google's Ironwood/Apollo OCS design implies demand for more than **6 million** 800G-plus optical modules from Google alone [11]. TrendForce also estimates the AI optical-transceiver market expands from **US$16.5 billion in 2025** to **US$26.0 billion in 2026**, growth of more than **57%**, with EML/CW-LD and precision optical alignment becoming capacity bottlenecks [12].

Recommended sub-weights inside the 35% TMT line:

| Sub-sleeve | Weight | Rationale |
|---|---:|---|
| 800G/1.6T optics, silicon photonics, CPO | 18% | Clearest earnings transmission from AI cluster architecture |
| AI PCB, connectors, high-speed networking, servers/cooling | 9% | Benefits from rack-density and network topologies |
| Domestic inference silicon and cloud AI monetization | 8% | Higher strategic value, but more policy and execution risk |

### 3) Utilities: 25%

Utilities deserve a structural overweight, but not the top rank. The demand visibility is good, yet earnings translation depends on power-market design, tariffs, fuel costs, utilization, grid connection timing, and local return-on-capital rules. Prefer power operators with visible utilization uplift, nuclear/hydro/renewables with contracted demand, and grid-adjacent storage or flexibility assets. In Hong Kong, the utility leg can also stabilize portfolio volatility.

IEA's base case says renewables meet around half of the growth in data-center electricity demand, but gas and coal together still meet more than **40%** of incremental demand to 2030; in China, coal and renewables each add nearly **90 TWh** of annual generation for data centers between 2024 and 2030 [6]. This mix argues for a balanced utility basket instead of a single-source power call.

## 4. Copper and Materials: Include, But Do Not Let Them Dominate

Copper is an important satellite exposure, but it should sit inside the industrials sleeve rather than become the whole strategy. Bloomberg Intelligence estimates North American data-center buildout could add **1.1-2.4 million tons** of copper demand by 2030, using **27-33 tons per MW** of applied power [13]. S&P Global estimates data-center copper demand rises from **1.1 million metric tons in 2025** to **2.5 million metric tons by 2040**, and notes a China AI training data center can require **47 metric tons per MW** [14].

The reason not to overweight copper alone is that price beta can reverse quickly if global manufacturing slows, if inventory financing unwinds, or if AI project delays shift demand to the right. For A/H equities, equipment makers with cost pass-through and order visibility offer cleaner alpha than pure spot copper beta.

## 5. Implementation Rules

Use the rotation only when three conditions stay true:

1. **Capex confirmation**: CSP 2026 capex guidance remains above **+15% YoY** and 2027 commentary does not shift to digestion.
2. **Physical bottleneck confirmation**: grid connection, transformer/cable, 800G/1.6T optical, and power-equipment lead times remain tight.
3. **China earnings confirmation**: China manufacturing PMI stays around or above 50, infrastructure/manufacturing FAI remains positive, and listed suppliers convert orders into margin rather than just revenue.

Rebalance rules:

| Signal | Action |
|---|---|
| CSP capex guides revised up again or 2027 guide becomes explicit | Add TMT optics and industrial electrical equipment |
| Copper rises while PMI and orders improve | Add copper-linked equipment, not only miners |
| Power tariffs / utilization improve | Add utilities |
| AI capex stays high but gross-margin commentary worsens | Trim TMT beta; rotate to grid equipment and utilities |
| Data-center project delays rise because of grid bottlenecks | Trim servers; add HVDC/UHV, transformers, switchgear |

## 6. What Would Falsify the Rotation?

The rotation is not risk-free. It fails if hyperscaler capex growth is mostly component inflation rather than capacity additions; if AI token growth slows after the initial agentic adoption wave; if cloud gross margins force budget discipline before 2027; if copper and optical pricing overshoot into demand destruction; or if China's grid and equipment-upgrade policy shifts away from execution toward headline targets.

The biggest portfolio risk is crowding in the obvious TMT names. The second risk is that utilities absorb capex without earning allowed returns. The third risk is that industrial equipment revenue grows, but copper, labor, and power costs compress margins. That is why I rank **industrials first, TMT second, utilities third**, rather than simply buying the highest-beta AI hardware basket.

## 7. Handoff

Verdict: **support and synthesize** Card 01. The Jevons-driven inference mix-shift is strong enough to trigger an A/H sector rotation, but the better strategy expression is a physical-infrastructure sleeve: **40% industrials, 35% TMT, 25% utilities**, with A-share overweight versus Hong Kong.

Recommended next analyst: **ashare-strategist [primary, horizon]**. The next unanswered question is not another bottom-up sector check; it is whether A-share market structure, margin financing, northbound/southbound flows, and crowding allow this allocation sleeve to be entered without paying peak theme multiples.
