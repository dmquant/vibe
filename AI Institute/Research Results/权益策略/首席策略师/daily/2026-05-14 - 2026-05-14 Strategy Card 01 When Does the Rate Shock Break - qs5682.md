# 2026-05-14 Strategy Card 01: When Does the Rate Shock Break U.S. Equity Greed?

As of the institute work-date 2026-05-14, I do not think the U.S. equity tape is pricing a hard landing. It is pricing a still-liquid nominal-growth regime in which inflation is inconvenient but not yet allowed to damage earnings, default risk, or passive equity demand. That makes the current setup more fragile than the small index move suggests: if the 10Y Treasury yield breaks the recent high, the first-order damage should be valuation and factor duration; credit becomes the second leg only if spreads start confirming that higher rates are becoming a refinancing and margin shock.

## Core Judgment

I support the concern embedded in the card question, but frame it as delayed transmission rather than hard-landing complacency. The April PPI shock was large enough to reprice the Fed path and long-end yields, yet equity volatility and credit spreads still look too calm for a fully priced recession.

## Why This Is Not Yet a Hard-Landing Price

The BLS release on 2026-05-13 showed April final-demand PPI rising 1.4% seasonally adjusted and 6.0% from a year earlier, with services up 1.2% and goods up 2.0%. Core PPI, excluding food and energy, rose 1.0% MoM and 5.2% YoY. That matters because a services-heavy producer-price shock is more persistent for margins and consumer pass-through than a pure oil headline.

The equity reaction was too contained to be called hard-landing pricing. CNN's market report noted the 10Y yield hit 4.49% before easing toward 4.46%, while the S&P 500 was only slightly lower around the report window. Separately, the VIX at 17.99 on the May 12 close and Fear & Greed still in "Greed" imply that investors are buying the idea that nominal earnings and AI-led index concentration can absorb higher rates.

The credit market is the cleanest evidence that hard landing is not priced. HY OAS around 2.75% is below its long-term average and still consistent with easy refinancing assumptions. If investors were already discounting a recession, spreads would be widening ahead of equities, not sitting near compressed levels.

## Transmission Map

1. Rates first hit valuation. At S&P 500 PE 32.04 and Shiller PE 42.32, the market has little margin for a higher real discount rate. If 10Y moves from the 4.46%-4.49% area toward 4.60%-4.75% without a matching rise in forward EPS, the equity risk premium compresses mechanically. That is a multiple problem before it is a default problem.

2. Credit breaks later, but faster once it starts. HY spreads are tight because nominal growth, cash balances, and private-credit demand still mask refinancing stress. The risk regime changes if HY OAS moves through 325-350 bp while the 10Y is making new highs. That would mean the market is no longer treating higher rates as "good growth" inflation; it is treating them as cash-flow pressure.

3. Volatility is the bridge. VIX below 20 says equity investors still see the shock as tradable. A move above 22-25, especially with HY spread widening and a stronger dollar, would mark the point where rate pressure is being transmitted into positioning and liquidity.

## If 10Y Breaks the Prior High: Valuation or Credit First?

Base case: valuation first. The most immediate casualty should be high-duration equities, crowded AI/platform winners, unprofitable growth, rate-sensitive real estate proxies, and long-cash-flow cyclicals. This is because the shock starts in the discount rate and the market is still rewarding earnings resilience.

Credit becomes the first casualty only under a different mix: a long-end yield breakout plus widening oil/freight costs, weaker labor data, and a HY OAS break above 350 bp. In that case, higher yields would no longer be a valuation input; they would become a refinancing and downgrade impulse. We are not there as of 2026-05-14.

## A-Share Strategy Implications

For A-shares, the U.S. rate shock is mainly a valuation, FX, and style-rotation problem rather than a direct earnings shock. A higher U.S. 10Y tends to pressure CNH, limit northbound risk appetite, and reduce the valuation tolerance for long-duration growth themes.

Allocation stance:

- Overweight dividend yield, utilities, telecom operators, state-owned enterprise value, high free-cash-flow industrial leaders, and select banks/insurers that benefit from a value and income rotation.
- Neutral domestic-demand consumption until U.S. rates either stabilize or China policy easing becomes strong enough to dominate external discount-rate pressure.
- Underweight crowded high-duration themes, especially names priced on 2027-2028 earnings narratives rather than current cash flow.
- Keep export beta selective. A stronger dollar may support translation for some exporters, but a U.S. rate shock combined with sticky producer inflation is negative for global demand visibility.
- Use any U.S.-rate-led A-share drawdown to add to domestic policy beneficiaries only when the CSI 300 / ChiNext growth spread confirms style stabilization.

## Watch Triggers

- 10Y Treasury yield above 4.60%: valuation compression phase.
- 10Y above 4.75% with VIX above 22: positioning stress phase.
- HY OAS above 325 bp: credit warning.
- HY OAS above 350 bp with weaker payrolls or retail sales: credit becomes the leading risk.
- DXY strength plus CNH depreciation: A-share foreign-flow and growth-style pressure.
