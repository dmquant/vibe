# Weekly A-Share Sector Allocation Report (2026-05-11)

## Core View: Shifting from "Policy Expectations" to "Earnings Validation", Focusing on Hard Tech and Resources

For the week of May 11, 2026, the A-share market enters a period of performance confirmation following the release of Q1 reports. As the core market logic shifts from "policy expectation-driven" to "corporate earnings-driven," we expect May to maintain its "Red May" momentum. Major institutions like Goldman Sachs have raised their CSI 300 targets to 5,300, supported by positive PPI growth and structural improvements in Return on Equity (ROE).

### Sector Allocation Recommendations

| Rating | Key Sectors | Core Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Overweight** | **AI Hardware & Computing**, **Energy & Resources**, **Hard Tech & Localization** | Upward revisions in capital expenditure by global cloud providers with high earnings visibility; supply-demand gaps supporting resource prices; catalysts from the "15th Five-Year Plan" and the Big Fund Phase III. |
| **Benchmark** | **High Dividend Assets**, **New Energy (Lithium/Storage)**, **Real Estate** | Maintaining a "dumbbell" strategy to hedge volatility with high dividends; capacity clearing in new energy nearing completion; ongoing policy support for real estate stability. |
| **Underweight** | **Traditional Consumption**, **General Software**, **Traditional Financials** | Flat consumption data and low capital interest; market preference shifting to hardware; software valuations facing mean reversion; financial sectors acting as defensive anchors but lacking upside elasticity. |

---

### Detailed Logic Analysis

#### 1. Overweight: High Growth and Certainty
*   **AI Hardware & Computing Infrastructure**: Optical modules (CPO), PCBs, memory chips, and AI servers. As the global LLM arms race intensifies, hyperscalers continue to revise their CapEx upwards. The export-oriented AI hardware chain in A-shares offers the highest earnings certainty among growth sectors.
*   **Energy Security & Resources**: Oil, Petrochemicals, Copper, Aluminum, and Basic Chemicals. Given geopolitical uncertainties and the global manufacturing recovery, the supply-demand gap for commodities is unlikely to close in the short term. Resources offer both earnings growth and anti-inflation properties.
*   **Hard Tech & Localization**: Semiconductor equipment/materials, commercial space, and advanced packaging. Driven by the commencement of the "15th Five-Year Plan" and the deployment of the Big Fund Phase III, the localization thesis is transitioning from expectation to large-scale production.

#### 2. Benchmark: Balanced Allocation and Tactical Recovery
*   **High Dividend Assets**: Serving as core holdings to hedge market volatility. In a low-interest-rate environment, their bond-like characteristics remain attractive for long-term investors.
*   **New Energy**: Lithium batteries and energy storage. Capacity clearing has entered the second half, with profitability in certain segments bottoming out. We recommend focusing on global leaders as a counterbalance to the AI hardware rotation.
*   **Real Estate**: Leading quality developers. Increased "de-stocking" policy efforts provide valuation recovery momentum, even though a full fundamental turnaround may take more time.

#### 3. Underweight: Weak Demand and Valuation Re-rating
*   **Traditional Consumption**: Spirits and essential consumer goods. Internal demand recovery remains sluggish. Market capital currently prefers manufacturing with "going global" capabilities over domestic-focused sectors lacking catalysts.
*   **General Software**: Application software and Internet platforms. Market appetite has shifted from "conceptual models" to "computing infrastructure." Pure software sectors lacking solid earnings face valuation pressure.
