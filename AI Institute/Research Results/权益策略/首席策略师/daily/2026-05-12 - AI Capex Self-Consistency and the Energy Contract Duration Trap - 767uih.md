# AI Capex Self-Consistency and the Energy Contract Duration Trap

As of 2026-05-12, I synthesize the initial line of reasoning into a tradable A-share framework: the FY26-FY28 AI capex debate is no longer about whether spending is large, but whether a $550-650 billion sustainable annual run-rate can be monetized at 55-70% compute utilization before depreciation, energy, and funding costs turn the cycle from a growth trade into a balance-sheet trade.

## Core Judgment

I support the setup, with one adjustment: the market is already seeing capex guidance above the $550-650 billion self-consistency band, so the tradable variable is not more capex. It is whether utilization and pricing can defend margins while the funding market absorbs the energy-contract and debt-duration tail.

The framework has four linked nodes:

| Node | Base Case | Bear-Tail Threshold | Trading Meaning |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| FY26-FY28 AI capex | $550-650 billion annualized self-consistent band | >$650 billion without utilization improvement | Upstream capex beta becomes crowded; require order visibility and cash conversion |
| Compute utilization | 55-70% | <60% for two quarters | Treat hardware and IDC capacity as overbuilt; favor grid and efficiency bottlenecks |
| FY27 capex growth | 10-18% | <10% | Rotate from capex suppliers to monetization, software, and power-flexibility winners |
| Gross margin erosion | 150-250 bp | >250 bp or widening guidance | De-rate high-capex AI platforms and suppliers with weak pass-through |
| Credit spreads | +75-125 bp in the AI funding complex | +125 bp or failed jumbo issuance | Move from equity beta to quality balance sheets and credit-hedged pairs |

## Evidence Anchor

The capex impulse is visible and current. Microsoft said on its April 2026 FY26 Q3 call that calendar-2026 capex should be roughly $190 billion, including about $25 billion from higher component pricing, and that capacity would remain constrained at least through 2026 [Microsoft FY26 Q3]. Meta raised 2026 capex guidance to $125-145 billion, citing component pricing and additional data-center costs [Meta Q1 2026]. Amazon said in its February 2026 Q4 release that it expected about $200 billion of 2026 capex across Amazon, largely tied to AI, chips, robotics, and satellites [Amazon Q4 2025]. Alphabet's April 2026 Q1 transcript raised 2026 capex guidance to $180-190 billion and explicitly said 2027 capex should increase significantly versus 2026 [Alphabet Q1 2026].

That evidence creates a tension with the mission's $550-650 billion FY26-FY28 range. The Big Four alone now point to roughly $695-725 billion of 2026 capex at the current guide range. I therefore treat $550-650 billion as the self-consistent sustainable band, while the current guidance is the stress zone that needs higher utilization, stronger AI revenue conversion, or lower unit cost to avoid margin and credit pressure.

Energy is the physical constraint and the duration trap. The IEA's 2026 Energy and AI update says global data-center electricity consumption rose 17% in 2025, AI-focused data centers rose 50%, and data-center electricity use is projected to roughly double from 485 TWh in 2025 to 950 TWh in 2030 [IEA 2026]. Deloitte's 2026 TMT outlook estimates AI data-center capex at $400-450 billion in 2026 and about $1 trillion in 2028, while noting that inference-optimized systems can still have very high rack power density [Deloitte 2026]. S&P Global Market Intelligence places 1 GW AI data-center capital cost at roughly $25-30 billion and says higher utilization and tokens per watt per dollar are core metrics [S&P Global 2026].

China is aligning computing capacity with energy rather than treating AI as a pure semiconductor story. On May 8, 2026, Chinese authorities announced an action plan to integrate AI and energy, with the stated goal of safer energy supply for computing infrastructure, greener computing power, and better coordination between computing and electricity [China Gov 2026]. State Grid has also indicated up to RMB 4 trillion of fixed-asset investment over 2026-2030, including new power-system and digital/AI infrastructure [SASAC 2026]. For A-shares, this makes grid equipment, UHV, thermal flexibility, storage, cooling, and power-management stocks more durable than simple AI-server beta.

Credit is the transmission channel from capex to equity style. Apollo estimates that more than $1.5 trillion of five-year hyperscaler capex has been announced and that $300-400 billion per year could be financed through debt markets [Apollo 2026]. Reuters reported on January 15, 2026 that Barclays expected U.S. corporate bond issuance to rise in 2026 partly because AI hyperscaler capex may require jumbo public deals, while BofA estimated the five major hyperscalers issued $121 billion of U.S. corporate bonds in 2025 versus a $28 billion annual average during 2020-2024 [Reuters 2026]. This is why a 75-125 bp spread-widening tail is not an afterthought. It is the valuation discount rate for the whole AI supply chain.

## The Tradable Model

Use a simple self-consistency score:

`AI self-consistency = capex discipline x utilization x pricing power x funding tolerance`

For a practical desk version, score each variable from 0 to 2:

| Variable | 0 Points | 1 Point | 2 Points |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capex discipline | FY27 growth >25% with no ROI disclosure | FY27 growth 10-25% | FY27 growth <10% but backlog quality and utilization improve |
| Utilization | <60% | 60-70% | >70% |
| Pricing power | AI unit prices fall faster than cost | mixed pass-through | tokens per watt per dollar improves and cloud margins hold |
| Funding tolerance | spreads +125 bp or issuance weak | spreads +75-125 bp | spreads contained below +75 bp |

Interpretation:

| Score | Regime | A-share Action |
|---:|---|---|
| 6-8 | Demand self-consistent | Own AI compute leaders, optical links, advanced PCBs, liquid cooling, high-quality applications |
| 3-5 | Bottleneck and margin squeeze | Barbell: grid/power equipment plus cash-generative software; hedge hardware beta |
| 0-2 | Capex cliff | Underweight IDC/server/optical-module momentum; own utilities, grid, high-dividend quality, and credit-safe balance sheets |

## Portfolio Construction

**Base book for May 2026:** overweight power-first AI infrastructure, neutral selective AI hardware, underweight long-duration IDC capacity.

1. Long China grid and power-equipment bottlenecks. Prioritize UHV, transformers, switchgear, grid automation, power electronics, flexible thermal retrofits, and storage integrators with confirmed orders. The logic is that power availability remains valuable in both the bull case and the overbuild case.

2. Long efficiency, not raw capacity. Liquid cooling, high-density power supplies, thermal management, advanced packaging materials, and energy-management software should outperform generic server assemblers if utilization is only 55-70%.

3. Own application monetization only when utilization is validated. AI software, vertical agents, cybersecurity, and enterprise workflow names need evidence of paid deployment, renewal, and gross-margin stability. They are not automatic beneficiaries of capex.

4. Underweight the energy-contract duration trap. Avoid data-center and IDC operators that sign long-duration power, land, or cooling obligations while customer contracts are short, cancellable, or price-resetting. If utilization slips below 60%, the fixed energy contract becomes an equity multiple problem.

5. Hedge credit-sensitive AI beta. If hyperscaler or data-center credit spreads widen 75-125 bp, reduce suppliers with stretched receivables, high customer concentration, or capex-led inventory. Prefer net-cash companies and suppliers with prepayment, milestone billing, or SOE/grid customers.

## Pair Trades

| Trade | Long | Short / Hedge | Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| Power bottleneck | Grid equipment, UHV, transformers, switchgear | Generic AI server assemblers | AI capex above $650 billion and utilization below 65% |
| Efficiency over capacity | Liquid cooling, power modules, advanced packaging materials | Merchant IDC and land-bank data centers | Gross-margin guide down 150-250 bp |
| Monetization over buildout | Cash-generative AI applications and cybersecurity | Optical/server momentum basket | FY27 capex growth guide <10% |
| Balance sheet quality | Net-cash suppliers with SOE/grid orders | Levered IDC or receivable-heavy suppliers | Credit spreads +75-125 bp |

## Catalysts and Risk Rules

Add exposure to the power-first basket when three conditions align: capex guidance remains above $650 billion, data-center power queues or equipment lead times lengthen, and cloud/platform gross-margin guidance deteriorates by 150 bp or more. This is the squeeze regime where the bottleneck suppliers get paid while generic AI beta is re-rated lower.

Rotate back into AI hardware beta only if utilization is above 70%, cloud AI revenue growth accelerates, and FY27 capex growth stays above 15% without spread widening. A 2027 capex growth guide below 10% is not automatically bearish for all AI assets; it is bearish for capacity suppliers and bullish for monetization, efficiency, and replacement-cycle discipline.

Stop-loss the bear-tail framework if gross margins expand despite higher depreciation, spreads stay within +75 bp, and management teams prove that tokens per watt per dollar is improving faster than capex intensity. In that case the cycle remains a growth trade rather than a duration trap.
