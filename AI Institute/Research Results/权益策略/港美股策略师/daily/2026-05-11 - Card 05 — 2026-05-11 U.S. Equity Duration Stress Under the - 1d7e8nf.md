# Card 05 — 2026-05-11 U.S. Equity Duration Stress Under the Hormuz Rates Shock

- **Work-date (Asia/Singapore):** 2026-05-11
- **Analyst:** offshore-strategist
- **Stance:** stress-test
- **Board:** 430a48d9-c3f6-4699-87e7-fbb5a64b20a9, Card 5/8
- **Mission:** If 5y real rates rise 25-40 bp and 2y breakevens rise another 30-40 bp, have current SPX ERP and Mag-7 valuations priced the rate regime? How should U.S. tech duration be allocated versus energy and defensive sectors under the Hormuz base case?

## Bottom line

As of **2026-05-11**, I support Card 04's stress direction and apply it to U.S. equities: **SPX and Mag-7 valuations have not fully priced the joint move of higher real rates and higher front-end inflation compensation**. The market is priced for earnings resilience and AI-led margin durability, not for an oil shock that keeps the Fed constrained while real discount rates move higher.

This is not a call to short all U.S. technology. The stronger call is a relative allocation change: **cut broad high-duration tech and unprofitable growth exposure, keep only cash-flow-compounding AI/platform leaders at valuation discipline, and rotate active risk toward energy cash-flow equities plus selected defensive quality**. For a U.S. equity sleeve, I would move from tech overweight to roughly neutral/underweight: **reduce U.S. tech/Mag-7 active weight by 4-6 pp**, add **3-4 pp to energy/infrastructure**, and add **2-3 pp to health care, staples, and regulated defensive quality**. Utilities are useful only selectively because they are defensive but still rate-sensitive.

## Starting valuation map

The latest valuation pack before the work-date is already thin on compensation. WisdomTree/FactSet/S&P put the **S&P 500 forward P/E at 21.5x** as of 2026-05-06, versus **17.9x for the S&P 480** and **28.1x for Mag-7**; Mag-7 ex Tesla was **26.8x**. The same pack put SPX ERP at **2.73%** as of 2026-05-05, far below its **5.14% historical median**, where ERP is defined as forward earnings yield less the 10y TIPS yield.

| Metric | Latest pre-work-date level | Stress interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| S&P 500 forward P/E | 21.5x | Forward earnings yield ≈ 4.65%; valuation already assumes smooth earnings delivery. |
| S&P 500 ERP | 2.73% | Thin versus 5.14% median; a 25-40 bp real-rate rise compresses it further unless prices fall or EPS is revised up. |
| Mag-7 forward P/E | 28.1x | Earnings yield ≈ 3.56%; real-rate cushion is narrow. |
| Mag-7 ex Tesla forward P/E | 26.8x | Better quality, but still dependent on durable AI/cloud earnings revisions. |
| S&P 480 forward P/E | 17.9x | Lower multiple, but not automatically safe if margins are oil-sensitive. |
| Public Treasury par real curve | 5y 1.40%, 10y 1.93% on 2026-05-08 | This public curve implies 5y BE around 2.61% from nominal-real arithmetic. Card 04's higher market-implied 5y real anchor around 2.10% makes the equity stress stricter. |

The important point is the direction, not the exact curve definition: **higher real rates and higher breakevens are both negative for duration equities unless nominal earnings estimates rise enough to offset the discount-rate hit**.

## What the 25-40 bp real-rate shock does to SPX

Using the conservative **21.5x** forward P/E anchor, SPX forward earnings yield is about **4.65%**. If the real-rate leg rises 25-40 bp with forward EPS unchanged:

| Test | Implied equity adjustment |
|---|---|
| Keep ERP unchanged at 2.73% | Forward earnings yield must rise to roughly 4.90-5.05%, taking P/E to about **20.4x-19.8x** and implying roughly **-5% to -8%** price downside before any EPS revision. |
| Require ERP to widen modestly to 3.0% | P/E falls toward roughly **19.4x-18.8x**, implying about **-10% to -13%** downside. |
| Require historical-median ERP | Not a base-case target, but it shows the cushion gap: historical median ERP of 5.14% would require a much lower multiple unless real yields collapse. |

The 2y breakeven shock matters because it prevents the usual equity-friendly response. If 2y BE rises another **30-40 bp**, the market cannot simply assume Fed cuts and lower real yields will rescue multiples. That is exactly the Card 04 channel: inflation compensation rises at the front end, while 5y real rates also reprice higher.

## Mag-7: quality is not the same as immunity

Mag-7 is not homogeneous. The valuation table shows why the trade should be trimmed, not indiscriminately liquidated:

| Name | Forward P/E | LT earnings growth estimate | Stress read |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| NVDA | 24.2x | 25.1% | Still the best PEG in the group; keep but size to volatility and supply-chain/geopolitical risk. |
| MSFT | 22.3x | 16.8% | Defensible cash-flow compounder; keep core weight, avoid adding on multiple alone. |
| META | 19.7x | 10.4% | Cheapest large platform; buyback support helps, but ad cyclicality is the oil-growth risk. |
| AMZN | 32.3x | 20.1% | Cloud/AI upside, but retail margins face energy and wage pass-through risk. |
| AAPL | 31.5x | 10.0% | Most vulnerable among mega-cap defensives on valuation-versus-growth. |
| GOOGL | 31.2x | 8.8% | Multiple looks demanding versus the stated long-term growth estimate. |
| TSLA | 186.8x | 22.6% | Highest-duration and most vulnerable to real-rate repricing plus consumer financing stress. |

At **28.1x** for Mag-7, the group has a **3.56%** forward earnings yield. Against a 10y TIPS yield near **1.93%**, that is only about **1.6 pp** of real-yield spread before growth. If real yields rise 25-40 bp, the spread falls toward **1.2-1.4 pp**. That is acceptable only if earnings revisions stay positive. It is not a margin of safety.

## Sector allocation under the Hormuz base case

For the Hormuz base case, the better U.S. equity expression is **long energy cash flows and defensive quality funded by high-duration tech beta**.

| Sleeve | Allocation action | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| U.S. tech / Mag-7 | Reduce active overweight by **4-6 pp**; keep NVDA/MSFT/META-style quality, trim TSLA/high-multiple software/long-duration consumer tech. | Duration math is unfavorable when 5y real rises and 2y BE rises together. Quality tech can still compound, but broad tech beta is no longer the cleanest risk-adjusted exposure. |
| Energy and infrastructure | Add **3-4 pp** via integrated majors, low-leverage E&Ps, midstream, LNG/export infrastructure, and energy services with pricing power. | IEA's Hormuz factsheet shows nearly **20 mb/d** of oil flows and almost **20%** of global LNG trade linked to the Strait; energy cash flows carry the right earnings beta. |
| Defensive quality | Add **2-3 pp** to health care, staples, and selected regulated utilities. | These reduce cyclicality and margin shock. Utilities should be selective because higher real yields pressure dividend-duration equities. |
| Broad cyclicals/import-sensitive industries | Underweight airlines, cruise, autos, chemicals, small-cap discretionary, and energy-intensive industrials. | These face both cost inflation and weaker real-income demand. |
| Hedges | Use SPX/QQQ put spreads and XLE call spreads rather than only cash. | The risk is a two-sided tape: oil/rates can hurt multiples quickly, while AI earnings can still squeeze outright shorts. |

## Trading expression

For an institutional U.S. equity book benchmarked to SPX:

1. Move U.S. technology from overweight to **neutral or 2-3 pp underweight** versus benchmark.
2. Fund **+3-4 pp energy** and **+2-3 pp defensive quality** from high-duration growth and import-sensitive cyclicals.
3. Keep a **quality barbell** inside tech: retain profitable AI/cloud/platform names with forward FCF visibility; cut long-duration, negative-FCF, and valuation-versus-growth outliers.
4. Add a 6-8 week hedge package: **QQQ or SPX 5-10% put spread**, financed partly with lower-delta downside, plus **XLE or integrated-major call spread** as the oil-beta offset.
5. Reassess if Brent falls below **USD 95** for five sessions, 5y real falls back by **25 bp**, or 2y BE fails to hold above the Card 04 stress band.

## Judgment versus prior cards

This card **supports and extends** the prior line. Card 01 argued Hormuz is underpriced outside spot oil; Card 02 shifted the hedge toward inflation assets; Card 03 cleaned up the FX expression; Card 04 identified the real-rate/breakeven mismatch. U.S. equities are the next weak link: **the current ERP is too low to absorb the rates shock without either price downside or a fresh earnings upgrade**.

The portfolio answer is not "sell America" or "short AI." It is to stop treating high-duration U.S. tech as the default safe asset when the shock is inflationary first. In the Hormuz base case, **energy cash flows and selected defensive quality deserve a higher active weight than broad tech duration** until either oil breaks lower or real yields stop rising.
