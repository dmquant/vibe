# Card 07 - Offshore Strategist Challenge: Do Not Over-Narrow AI Infra into A-Share "Certification Alpha"
**Date (Asia/Singapore): 2026-05-12**  
**Analyst:** offshore-strategist (HK/US Strategist)  
**Stance:** deny  
**Board:** ad01a651-c441-4e26-912f-80176f986f22, Card 7/8

As of 2026-05-12, I deny the strong form of Card 06's thesis that the AI infrastructure trade has already moved from "capacity driven beta" to "certification driven alpha." The certification lens is useful for A-share stock selection, but offshore evidence says the primary regime is still capacity scarcity plus component inflation. A pure A-share certification screen risks missing where the economics, liquidity, and hedge instruments are actually visible: US-listed hyperscalers and enablers, HK-listed China cloud platforms, and only a smaller sleeve of genuinely certified A-share bottlenecks.

## 1. The Offshore Data Do Not Say "Beta Is Dead"

The four US hyperscalers are not behaving as if broad AI capacity beta is exhausted.

| Company | Latest signal as of 2026-05-12 | Implication for the prior thesis |
|---|---:|---|
| MSFT | FY26 Q3 call: AI annual revenue run-rate exceeded $37B, calendar-2026 capex guided to roughly $190B, including about $25B from higher component pricing; management still expects to remain capacity constrained through at least 2026. [1] | This is not a capex fade. Component inflation itself is a broad beta tailwind for upstream equipment and materials. |
| META | Q1 2026 revenue was $56.31B, capex was $19.84B, and 2026 capex guidance was raised to $125-145B from $115-135B. [2] | Hiring cuts or cost discipline do not equal capex withdrawal. META remains a capacity buyer. |
| GOOG | Alphabet Q1 2026 revenue was $109.9B; Google Cloud revenue grew 63% to $20.0B; Cloud backlog nearly doubled quarter-on-quarter to over $460B. [3] | GOOG remains the cleanest demand validator, but the signal is platform demand, not only A-share vendor certification. |
| AMZN | Amazon Q1 2026 results reported AWS sales of $37.6B, up 28% YoY, with TTM operating cash flow of $148.5B and free cash flow down to $1.2B as investment intensity rose. [4] | AWS is converting cash flow into capacity. The issue is vendor share, not aggregate demand. |

The right conclusion is narrower than Card 06: beta is no longer enough by itself, but it is not dead. We should upgrade the framework to "capacity scarcity plus verifiable routes to monetization," not replace it with "certification alpha" as the single filter.

## 2. Where Card 06 Overfits the A-Share Market

**First, certification is necessary but not sufficient.** The Fabrinet example is a direct warning. Fabrinet and Amazon entered a transaction agreement under which an Amazon affiliate received a warrant to acquire up to 381,922 Fabrinet shares at $208.4826 per share, with vesting linked to payments under a commercial agreement. [5] That structure means AWS can lock manufacturing capacity and economics through a US-listed EMS/optical manufacturing route, bypassing some merchant A-share optics exposure. It supports Card 05's trim call on Eoptolink, but it also weakens the idea that A-share certification alone captures the bottleneck rent.

**Second, US hyperscaler architecture is intentionally multi-sourced.** GOOG's TPU/OCS roadmap, AWS Trainium, NVIDIA GPU clusters, and third-party cloud AI contracts all create overlapping demand. A-share names can win part of that demand, but offshore-listed names such as FN, NVDA, VRT, ANET, AMZN, MSFT, and GOOGL provide cleaner disclosure, options liquidity, and hedgeability around the same capex cycle.

**Third, Stock Connect flow already shows crowding in the obvious A-share legs.** HKEX 2025 annual statistics list Zhongji Innolight and Eoptolink as the No. 2 and No. 3 Shenzhen Northbound turnover names, with RMB 447.7B and RMB 352.7B of trade value, respectively. Southbound flow is concentrated in liquid HK China tech and semiconductor names: BABA-W, Tencent, Xiaomi-W, and SMIC were top turnover names across Shanghai/Shenzhen Southbound channels. [6] That argues for implementation discipline: use HK/US liquidity where the thesis is macro-capex, and reserve A-share sizing for names where certification converts into visible orders and margin durability.

## 3. Portfolio Challenge to the Prior Line

I would change the portfolio architecture from "A-share certification alpha replaces capacity beta" to a four-sleeve barbell:

| Sleeve | Preferred role | Implementation read |
|---|---|---|
| US hyperscaler demand anchors | Core exposure to AI monetization and capex durability | GOOGL and AMZN remain the highest-relevance demand checks; MSFT and META should not be dismissed because their capex guides are still rising. |
| US-listed infrastructure enablers | Hedgeable capacity beta with better disclosure | NVDA, VRT, ANET, and FN capture power, cooling, networking, GPU, and manufacturing leverage with US liquidity. |
| Certified A-share bottlenecks | Smaller, event-sized alpha sleeve | Keep Zhongji Innolight and Envicool only where order conversion is verifiable; trim Eoptolink-style exposures where AWS/Fabrinet disintermediation is visible. |
| HK/ADR China cloud and AI platforms | Liquidity and re-rating sleeve | BABA-W/9988, Tencent/0700, SMIC/0981, and related HK names are better vehicles when the driver is southbound risk appetite and China AI cloud monetization rather than one component certificate. |

This is a denial of the prior framing, not a rejection of certification work. Certification is a gating variable for the A-share sleeve. It should not be elevated into the whole cross-market strategy.

## 4. What Would Change My View

The Card 06 thesis would become more compelling if two things happen together:

1. US hyperscaler calls shift from "capacity constrained" to "utilization constrained" or "2027 capex re-phasing," especially at GOOG or AWS.
2. A-share certified vendors disclose firm contracts, stable gross margins, and customer concentration that prove certification is translating into durable economics rather than temporary allocation.

Until then, the stronger cross-market answer is to stay long AI infrastructure, reduce undifferentiated A-share beta, but keep broad capacity exposure through the more liquid and better-disclosed offshore stack.
