## 3. Whiteboard Divergences

### 3.1 Online Fulfillment Is Beating Broad Store Recovery

This is not a one-source signal: Placer.ai e-commerce distribution-center traffic rose 20.5% YoY in April, BofA card data says online retail remained solid while department stores, apparel, airlines, and home improvement card spending declined, and Similarweb’s April U.S. e-commerce ranking still puts Amazon and Walmart at the center of online traffic. AMZN, WMT, and ONLN outperformed XRT and TGT, so the market is trading part of the outcome, but the cleaner debate is whether fuel pressure is accelerating channel migration rather than simply proving broad consumer resilience.

Trade read-through: favor retailers with online fulfillment, advertising, and membership ecosystems; stay cautious on mid-tier discretionary retailers without a closed-loop online conversion engine.

### 3.2 Home Improvement May Be Stabilizing Before Housing Fundamentals

HD and LOW Q1 visits were +1.9% and +2.0% YoY, respectively, and Placer.ai says the post-February recovery continued without slipping back into negative territory in April. BofA card data simultaneously shows April home-improvement retailer card spending declined, so ticket and traffic may be moving in different directions: consumers may be making more small repair and DIY trips while avoiding expensive projects. The stocks fell on the day, creating the debate: revenue has not fully confirmed it, but traffic may already be turning.

Trade read-through: this is not a blanket buy call on HD/LOW; the question is whether positive traffic can flow through to Pro demand, building products, tools, and home-supply chains. If ticket stays weak, margin and mix remain risks.

### 3.3 Cruise Data Shows Premium Experiences Are Still Intact

BofA April cruise card spending rose 15.8% YoY, and the sequential decline was better than normal seasonality. RCL, CCL, and NCLH rose, but the moves were still modest. This signal does not contradict pressure on lower-income non-essential spending; it supports a split consumer: fuel and food pressure hurt low-end discretionary categories, while asset prices and high-income cash flows still support experience travel.

Trade read-through: treat cruise and premium experiences as an expression of uneven consumer resilience rather than extrapolating retail weakness across all discretionary spend.
