# Morning Macro Brief (May 5, 2026)

## Executive Summary
The global macroeconomic environment is currently defined by a complex tug-of-war between "energy shocks" and "growth deceleration." Against the backdrop of a leadership transition, the Federal Reserve exhibits significant internal division, maintaining high rates to combat persistent inflation. Meanwhile, China's manufacturing sector continues to expand, but a contraction in services signals weakening domestic demand.

---

## 1. Top Focus: Historic Fed Dissent and Leadership Transition
*   **Rate Decision**: The FOMC held the federal funds rate steady at **3.50%–3.75%** at its late-April meeting, as expected.
*   **Internal Fracture**: The meeting saw a rare **8-4** vote—the highest level of dissent since 1992. While Miran advocated for a cut, Hammack, Kashkari, and Logan opposed the "easing bias" in the statement, citing sticky inflation driven by energy price pressures.
*   **New Leadership**: Jerome Powell concluded his final meeting as Chair. **Kevin Warsh** has been confirmed as his successor. Markets are closely watching whether the new Chair will maintain the "Higher for Longer" stance.

## 2. United States: Labor and Services Data in Focus
*   **Today's Watch**: Market attention shifts to the **March JOLTS Job Openings** and **April ISM Services PMI** releases later today.
*   **Growth Profile**: Q1 GDP growth was recorded at 2.0%, showing resilience but slowing from previous peaks. Investors anticipate a significant slowdown in the May 8 NFP report (Consensus: 49k–73k jobs), with the unemployment rate expected to hold at 4.3%.

## 3. China: Manufacturing Resilience vs. Service Sector Contraction
*   **PMI Data**: The April NBS Manufacturing PMI came in at **50.3**, marking its second consecutive month in expansion. High-tech manufacturing (52.2) remains a key bright spot.
*   **Hidden Risks**: The Non-Manufacturing PMI dropped to **49.4**, entering contraction territory. This highlights weak domestic demand in services and construction. Furthermore, input prices surged to 63.7, intensifying cost pressures for firms.

## 4. Global Outlook: Energy Shock and Stagflation Risks
*   **Strait of Hormuz Crisis**: The global economy is navigating its largest oil supply shock since the 1970s. Brent crude remains highly volatile, and energy-driven "stagflation" risks are spreading globally.
*   **RBA Decision**: The Reserve Bank of Australia is expected to hike rates by 25bps to 4.35% today to combat persistent inflation.
*   **Eurozone**: Q1 growth was a meager 0.1%, while April inflation climbed to 3%, confirming a stagflationary environment.
