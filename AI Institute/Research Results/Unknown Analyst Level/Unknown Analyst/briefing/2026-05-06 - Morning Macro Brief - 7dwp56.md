# Morning Macro Brief

**Date:** May 7, 2026  
**Author:** Chief Economist

## Executive Summary

The global macroeconomic landscape is currently grappling with a "stagflationary" shock triggered by escalating tensions in the Middle East. With oil prices hovering between $100 and $120 per barrel, global inflationary pressures have re-accelerated, forcing major central banks into a "hawkish hold" or a bias toward further tightening. While AI-related investments provide a floor for growth, high energy costs and geopolitical risks are weighing on consumer confidence and traditional manufacturing sectors.

## Regional Analysis

### 1. United States: Leadership Transition Amid Sticky Inflation
*   **Policy Stance:** The Federal Reserve held the benchmark rate steady at 3.50%–3.75% at its early May meeting. This marked Jerome Powell’s final meeting; Kevin Warsh is set to succeed him on May 15. The decision saw the highest number of dissents since 1992, signaling deep internal divisions over the appropriate response to rebounding inflation.
*   **Growth & Inflation:** Q1 GDP grew at an annualized rate of 2.0%, supported by massive AI infrastructure spending. Core PCE remains sticky at 3.2%, while headline CPI rose to 3.3%, driven by energy costs.

### 2. Eurozone: "Crisis Mode" and Weak Momentum
*   **Policy Stance:** The ECB kept rates unchanged in late April but signaled a potential June hike, effectively shifting into "crisis management mode" to combat energy-driven inflation.
*   **Economic Conditions:** Q1 GDP rose by only 0.1% Q/Q, with stagnation in Germany and Ireland. Preliminary April inflation jumped to 3.0%, with energy prices surging over 14% year-on-year in certain regions.

### 3. China: Accommodative Stance vs. Domestic Headwinds
*   **Policy Stance:** The PBOC maintains a "solid yet moderately loose" stance to counter property sector weakness, recently increasing re-lending quotas for tech innovation to 1.2 trillion yuan.
*   **Data Highlights:** Q1 GDP was reported at 5.0%, though domestic consumption remains sluggish. A record trade surplus decline was noted due to massive semiconductor imports ($135B in Q1). The official Non-Manufacturing PMI dipped into contraction at 49.4.

### 4. Japan: Yen Volatility and Hawkish Tilt
*   **Policy Stance:** The BoJ held its policy rate at 0.75%, but three board members dissented in favor of an immediate hike to 1.0%. The BoJ raised its FY2026 inflation forecast to 2.8% as energy subsidies phase out.
*   **FX Markets:** After briefly breaching the 160 level, the Yen saw suspected government intervention, stabilizing around 157.
