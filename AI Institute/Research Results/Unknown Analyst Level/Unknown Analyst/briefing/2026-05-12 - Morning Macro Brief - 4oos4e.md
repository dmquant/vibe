# Morning Macro Brief

**Anchor date: 2026-05-13, based on shell `date +%Y-%m-%d`**  
**Chief conclusion: the marginal global macro question has shifted from “is growth breaking?” to “will the energy shock re-anchor inflation expectations higher?” U.S. and China activity remain resilient, but U.S. inflation, euro-area energy inflation, and China’s upstream price rebound all argue against a quick pivot to broad monetary easing.**

## Priority Conclusions

1. **United States: April CPI is this week’s key macro input, and it weakens the June-cut narrative.** U.S. CPI rose **0.6% m/m** and **3.8% y/y** in April; core CPI rose **0.4% m/m** and **2.8% y/y**. Energy increased **3.8% m/m** and shelter **0.6% m/m**, so the inflation impulse is not just a one-item commodity noise story.[BLS CPI](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_05122026.htm)

2. **Federal Reserve: policy is still restrictive, but the statement showed rising internal disagreement.** On April 29, the FOMC kept the federal funds target range at **3.50%–3.75%** and the interest rate on reserve balances at **3.65%**; one voter preferred a 25bp cut, while three voters supported holding rates but objected to including an easing bias in the statement. Baseline call: the June meeting is more likely to wait for more inflation and labor-market confirmation than to cut immediately.[Fed statement](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260429a.htm)

3. **Growth is not in a collapse regime.** U.S. real GDP grew **2.0% SAAR** in Q1, and real final sales to private domestic purchasers rose **2.5%**; April payrolls increased **115k**, with unemployment at **4.3%**. This is a “slower, but not weak enough to force central-bank rescue” mix.[BEA GDP](https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/gdp-advance-estimate-1st-quarter-2026), [BLS employment](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/empsit_05082026.htm)

4. **Europe is closer to a stagflation trade-off.** Euro-area GDP rose only **0.1% q/q** in Q1, while April HICP inflation increased to **3.0% y/y**, with energy at **10.9% y/y**. The ECB held the deposit facility rate at **2.00%**, main refinancing rate at **2.15%**, and marginal lending facility rate at **2.40%**; the Bank of England voted **8–1** to keep Bank Rate at **3.75%**, with the sole dissent favoring a hike to **4.00%**.[Eurostat GDP](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/en/web/products-euro-indicators/w/2-30042026-bp), [Eurostat HICP](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/en/web/products-euro-indicators/w/2-30042026-ap), [ECB](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/pr/date/2026/html/ecb.mp260430~81b7179e6f.en.html), [BoE](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy-summary-and-minutes/2026/april-2026)

5. **China: demand is healing moderately, but the upstream price shock must be separated into “reflation” versus “margin squeeze.”** China’s Q1 GDP rose **5.0% y/y** and **1.3% q/q**; the April manufacturing PMI was **50.3**, the non-manufacturing business activity index **49.4**, and the composite PMI output index **50.1**. April CPI was **1.2% y/y**, core CPI **1.2% y/y**, and PPI **2.8% y/y** and **1.7% m/m**. This supports nominal growth, but if final demand does not follow, faster upstream price gains will compress midstream and downstream margins.[NBS GDP](https://www.stats.gov.cn/sj/zxfb/202604/t20260417_1963336.html), [NBS PMI](https://www.stats.gov.cn/sj/zxfb/202604/t20260430_1963473.html), [NBS CPI](https://www.stats.gov.cn/sj/zxfbhjd/202605/t20260511_1963659.html), [NBS PPI](https://www.stats.gov.cn/sj/zxfbhjd/202605/t20260511_1963658.html)

## Policy View

**Federal Reserve:** The baseline is a hawkish pause. If May CPI stays above **0.3% m/m**, the probability of a June cut should fall further; a growth-rescue discussion only reopens if payrolls print below **100k** for two straight months and unemployment breaks above **4.5%**.

**ECB:** The current mix of weak growth and energy-led inflation is poor terrain for firm forward guidance. Unless energy prices retreat and pull HICP back toward **2.5%**, the wait-and-see period around a **2.00%** deposit rate can persist.

**Bank of England:** The BoE’s reaction function is more inflation-focused than markets may prefer. An 8–1 hold, with the dissent coming from a hike, keeps wage and energy second-round effects at the center of the policy debate.

**China:** Macro policy should remain tilted toward stabilizing growth, expanding domestic demand, and protecting margins. Positive PPI helps nominal revenue, but if property and services consumption do not improve in tandem, support needs to come more from fiscal and quasi-fiscal channels than from rate cuts alone.

## Market Implications

- **Rates:** The 10-year Treasury at **4.46%** and the 30-year at **5.03%** on 2026-05-12, based on the U.S. Treasury daily curve, show that the long end is not trading a rapid easing cycle. Duration assets need confirmation from inflation, not just slower growth.[U.S. Treasury XML](https://home.treasury.gov/resource-center/data-chart-center/interest-rates/pages/xml?data=daily_treasury_yield_curve&field_tdr_date_value_month=202605)
- **Dollar:** The dollar has near-term support from rate differentials and safe-haven demand; if the energy shock weighs on European growth, a dollar decline requires U.S. inflation to cool at the same time.
- **Commodities:** The World Bank forecasts a **24%** rise in energy prices in 2026 and a Brent average of **$86/bbl**; if geopolitical disruption persists, commodity pass-through into CPI should be treated as a main-scenario risk, not a tail risk.[World Bank CMO](https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2026/04/28/commodity-markets-outlook-april-2026-press-release)
- **China assets:** Better nominal pricing supports upstream cash flow, but midstream manufacturing and consumer margins depend on whether firms can pass costs through to final demand. The macro backdrop is closer to “structural reflation” than broad demand overheating.
