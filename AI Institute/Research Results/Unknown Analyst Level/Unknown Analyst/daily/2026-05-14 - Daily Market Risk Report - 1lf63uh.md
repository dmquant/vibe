## Main Risk Transmission Channels

### 1. Sticky inflation to rate repricing

U.S. CPI and PPI both point to persistent price pressure: April CPI rose +3.8% year over year, core CPI rose +3.6% year over year, and PPI final demand rose +3.7% year over year. If the market keeps pushing out rate-cut expectations, the dominant portfolio risk shifts from earnings downgrades to a higher valuation discount rate. The most sensitive exposures are long-duration technology, REITs, long-duration credit, and high-dividend bond substitutes.

**Risk judgment:** The equity rally should not be treated as evidence that macro risk has declined. If the 10Y Treasury yield rises by 50 bp while equity volatility rises, portfolios can suffer a joint stock-bond drawdown and risk-parity deleveraging.

### 2. Growth crowding and limited breadth

QQQ is outperforming SPY, while IWM is almost flat. The rally is still concentrated in quality growth and AI-linked mega-cap exposure. This structure can persist when earnings revisions are positive, but it is fragile under the combined shock of higher rates, multiple compression, and crowded positioning.

**Risk judgment:** Single-theme exposure should be measured by marginal VaR, not just nominal weight. If the top ten equity positions contribute more than 45% of total equity VaR, reduce exposure or hedge with Nasdaq put spreads.

### 3. Dollar, EM, and China exposure

FXI, ASHR, and EEM are rebounding even as UUP is higher. This suggests the China/EM trade is being driven more by local policy expectations and risk appetite than by easier dollar liquidity. If the dollar strengthens further, EM equities and local-currency bonds face both FX and valuation pressure.

**Risk judgment:** New EM risk budget should go first to positions with clear earnings or policy catalysts and should be paired with FX hedges. Avoid extrapolating a one-day rebound into a medium-term trend.

### 4. Commodities, energy, and geopolitical tails

USO is down 1.55%, which helps ease near-term inflation expectations. Oil, however, remains a convex tail risk. A supply shock that drives oil sharply higher would raise inflation expectations, pressure consumer margins, and delay easing expectations at the same time.

**Risk judgment:** Energy is not today’s dominant risk, but the oil-jump scenario should remain active, especially for airlines, chemicals, consumer exposure, and long-duration bond portfolios.
