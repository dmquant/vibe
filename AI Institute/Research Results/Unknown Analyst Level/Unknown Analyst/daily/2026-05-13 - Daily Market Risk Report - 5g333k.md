## Key Market Risk Factors

### 1. Inflation And Rates: From Waiting For Cuts To Guarding Against Re-Tightening

BLS data show U.S. April CPI rising from +3.3% YoY in March to +3.8% YoY, core CPI rising from +2.6% YoY to +2.8% YoY, and energy up +17.9% YoY. This is not just a gasoline-price noise event: food, shelter and core components are all moving higher, raising the tail probability of sticky inflation.[1]

**Risk-control judgment:**

- The common risk factor has risen for long-duration equities, unprofitable growth, long-duration credit and low-coupon bonds.
- Short-term correlations in a traditional 60/40 portfolio can deteriorate; VaR models should not keep assuming bonds will hedge equities reliably.
- If PPI confirms upstream price pressure, the equity risk premium should be revised higher rather than reduced simply because CPI has been released.

### 2. Energy And Geopolitics: Oil Is The Shared Pressure Point For Inflation, Margins And Liquidity

Reuters reported Brent at $106.32/bbl in the Asian morning; although it was down 1.4% on the day, oil has remained at or above $100/bbl since late February. This risk is not limited to energy equities; it spreads through transport, chemicals, consumer fuel spending and inflation expectations.[4]

**Second-order effects to monitor:**

- Margin downgrades in airlines, logistics, chemicals, discretionary consumption and low-margin manufacturing.
- Current-account and FX pressure in energy-importing economies.
- Higher liquidity needs from commodity margining and hedging programs.

The 2026-05-12 index decline was modest, but the structure was more vulnerable: Nasdaq fell more than the S&P 500, and the Russell 2000 fell more than both. At the ETF level, SPY was $738.18 (-0.15%), QQQ was $707.24 (-0.85%), IWM was $282.57 (-0.97%), and DIA was +0.16%.[3][7]

## Risk Controls And Positioning Guidance

1. **Risk budget:** Reduce target usage of the total portfolio intraday VaR limit to 70%-80% of normal until the PPI, retail-sales and Trump-Xi event window has passed.
2. **Equity exposure:** Cut net longs in high-valuation growth, unprofitable technology and levered small caps; retain relative exposure to quality cash flow and defensive sectors.
3. **Rates exposure:** Long-duration bonds should no longer be treated as an automatic hedge; duration exposure must be jointly stressed with inflation scenarios.
4. **Commodity exposure:** Energy longs can hedge inflation, but gap and policy-headline limits are required; gold positions need dollar and real-rate stop rules.
5. **Liquidity:** Put crowded single names, high-gamma option exposure and cross-market margin calls on the same liquidity sheet.
6. **FX:** Rebalance dollar liabilities, non-U.S. revenues and Asia-currency exposure; avoid passive expansion of FX VaR during dollar strength.
