# Global Macro Weekly Research Draft (Week 18, 2026)

**Coverage window: 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01. Core conclusion: U.S. growth did not stall, but the mix has shifted from “resilient growth” to “sticky inflation under an energy shock plus a low-hiring labor market”; risk assets were supported by Mag 7 earnings resilience, while safe-haven buying did not dominate.**

## Prioritized Conclusions

1. **Q1 GDP at 2.0% SAAR was not inventory-driven.** Personal consumption contributed **1.08 pct** and fixed investment contributed **1.08 pct**, a combined **2.16 pct**, well above the **0.40 pct** inventory contribution; however, consumption growth slowed from **1.9%** in Q4 to **1.6%**, with services doing the heavy lifting.
2. **The labor market is “low hiring, low firing,” not confirmed recessionary contraction.** March NFP was **+178k** and unemployment was **4.3%**, but the January-March three-month average was only **+68k**; the latest JOLTS data are still for February, with openings at **6.882 million** and the hiring rate at **3.1%**, the lowest since April 2020.
3. **Core PCE has not delivered three consecutive upside surprises, but policy constraints have tightened.** March core PCE was **0.3% MoM / 3.2% YoY**, with YoY matching the **3.2%** forecast; the Q1 GDP report showed core PCE prices running at **4.3%** annualized, leading the Fed to hold 3.50%-3.75% with four dissents.
4. **AI CapEx did not trigger a broad tech valuation reset because revenue delivery still offsets the spending concern.** Alphabet Cloud was **$20.028B, +63% YoY**, Amazon AWS was **$37.587B, +28% YoY**, and Microsoft Azure was **+40% YoY**; Meta’s 2026 CapEx guide of **$125B-$145B** remains the most sensitive valuation pressure point.
5. **There was no synchronized USD and gold safe-haven bid before the long weekend.** LPL/Bloomberg weekly data show the S&P 500 **+0.95%**, Gold **-1.77%**, Oil **+8.19%**, and a weaker U.S. dollar; that is a risk-on tape with an energy shock, not broad safe-haven accumulation.

## Question 1: Growth Momentum Shift

### Method
I used the BEA Q1 GDP advance estimate released on 2026-04-30 and the BEA historical comparison table to decompose contribution points from PCE, fixed investment, inventories, net exports, and government spending; I compared Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 annualized growth rates.

### Findings

| Indicator | Q1 2026 | Q4 2025 | Change/contribution | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Real GDP | 2.0% SAAR | 0.5% SAAR | +1.5 pct | Growth rebounded but missed Reuters consensus of 2.3% |
| PCE growth | 1.6% | 1.9% | 1.08 pct contribution | Consumption still supports growth, but slowed |
| Services consumption contribution | 1.11 pct | 0.37 pct | +0.74 pct | Services drove consumption resilience |
| Fixed investment contribution | 1.08 pct | 0.26 pct | +0.82 pct | AI/equipment/IP investment was material |
| Inventory contribution | 0.40 pct | 0.14 pct | +0.26 pct | Helpful, but not the main driver |
| Net exports contribution | -1.30 pct | -4.68 pct | +3.38 pct | Imports still dragged, but less than in Q4 |
| Government contribution | 0.73 pct | -0.99 pct | +1.72 pct | Government rebound was an important add |

Interpretation: Q1 growth was not dependent on inventory rebuilding, as inventories accounted for only about one-fifth of headline GDP growth. The key supports were fixed investment and services consumption, showing that private domestic demand still had resilience. The weak points were goods consumption at **-0.03 pct** and residential investment at **-0.31 pct**, both consistent with rate sensitivity. Growth quality was better than an inventory-led rebound, but less broad-based than the 2.0% headline suggests.

### Evidence
- BEA, **GDP (Advance Estimate), 1st Quarter 2026**, 2026-04-30: Real GDP **2.0%**, Q4 2025 **0.5%**, final sales to private domestic purchasers **2.5%**; source: https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/gdp-advance-estimate-1st-quarter-2026
- BEA historical comparison table, **hist1q26-adv.pdf**: PCE **1.08 pct**, fixed investment **1.08 pct**, inventories **0.40 pct**, net exports **-1.30 pct**, government **0.73 pct**; source: https://www.bea.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/hist1q26-adv.pdf
- Reuters via Investing.com: economists polled by Reuters expected **2.3%** GDP growth; source: https://www.investing.com/news/economic-indicators/us-growth-picks-up-in-the-first-quarter-4649371

### Caveats
Advance GDP will be revised in the second estimate on 2026-05-28; inventories, trade, and government spending are often revision-prone. BEA also noted that IEEPA tariff refunds are treated as capital transfers and do not affect Q1 GDP, but any lagged impact on business behavior remains difficult to quantify.

## Question 2: Labor Market Inflection Point

### Method
I checked the official BLS release calendar and found that the planned 2026-05-01 April NFP and 2026-04-28 March JOLTS releases did not occur; April NFP is scheduled for 2026-05-08 and March JOLTS for 2026-05-05. The analysis therefore uses the latest official data available within the week: the March Employment Situation and February JOLTS.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest value | Previous/comparison | Numeric signal | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| March NFP | +178k | February -133k | 311k rebound | One-month rebound, not proof of reacceleration |
| 3-month NFP average | +68k | January-March | Low vs most of 2025 | Hiring momentum is weak |
| 6-month NFP average | +14.8k | October-March | Near-stagnant | Dragged by October and February declines |
| Unemployment rate | 4.3% | February 4.4% | Broadly stable | Not a recessionary jump yet |
| Long-term unemployed | 1.8 million | +322k YoY | 25.4% of unemployed | Duration of unemployment is worsening |
| JOLTS job openings | 6.882 million | January 7.240 million | -358k MoM | Labor demand cooled |
| JOLTS hires | 4.849 million | January 5.347 million | -498k MoM | Hiring rate 3.1%, lowest since 2020-04 |
| Layoffs/discharges | 1.7 million | unchanged | Layoffs did not broaden | Low hiring, not high firing |

Interpretation: The cleanest description is weak hiring with no layoff spiral. NFP rebounded in March, but BLS explicitly said payroll employment had changed little on net over the prior 12 months. The JOLTS hiring rate falling to the lowest since April 2020 matters more than openings alone. The labor market has not crossed into classic recessionary contraction because unemployment and layoffs have not jumped together.

### Evidence

## Question 3: Inflation Stickiness and Policy Constraints

### Method
I used BEA Personal Income and Outlays, BEA GDP price indexes, BLS CPI, and the Fed FOMC statement; I compared actual readings with available market expectations and focused specifically on whether a sequence of upside surprises exists.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest value | Forecast/previous | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| March headline PCE | 0.7% MoM / 3.5% YoY | February 0.4% / 2.8% | Energy lifted the headline |
| March core PCE | 0.3% MoM / 3.2% YoY | YoY forecast 3.2%; February 3.0% | In line, not a third consecutive upside surprise |
| Q1 PCE price index | 4.5% SAAR | Q4 2.9% | Quarterly inflation reaccelerated |
| Q1 core PCE price index | 4.3% SAAR | Q4 2.7% | Fed constraints rose materially |
| March CPI | 0.9% MoM / 3.3% YoY | February 0.3% / 2.4% | Gasoline was the main driver |
| March core CPI | 0.2% MoM / 2.6% YoY | core YoY below 2.7% consensus | Core CPI did not break higher |
| FOMC policy rate | 3.50%-3.75% | unchanged | 1 voter favored a cut; 3 opposed the easing bias |

Interpretation: It is not accurate to say core PCE has produced three consecutive upside surprises. January core PCE at **0.4% MoM / 3.1% YoY** matched expectations, February core PCE at **3.0% YoY** matched expectations, and March at **3.2% YoY** also matched expectations. The issue is the level, not the surprise: core PCE YoY is back at **3.2%** and Q1 annualized core PCE is **4.3%**, making Fed easing difficult. There is not enough evidence that the next meeting will focus on “re-hiking”; the more defensible implication is a weaker or contested easing bias.

### Evidence
- BEA Personal Income and Outlays, March 2026: PCE **0.7% MoM / 3.5% YoY**, core PCE **0.3% MoM / 3.2% YoY**, real PCE **0.2% MoM**; source: https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/personal-income-and-outlays-march-2026
- BEA GDP Q1 price indexes: PCE price index **4.5%**, core PCE **4.3%** SAAR; source: https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/gdp-advance-estimate-1st-quarter-2026
- BLS CPI March 2026: all-items CPI **0.9% MoM / 3.3% YoY**, gasoline **+21.2% MoM**; source: https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.htm
- Fed FOMC statement, 2026-04-29: policy rate **3-1/2 to 3-3/4 percent**, inflation elevated partly due to global energy prices, voting details; source: https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260429a.htm
- Fed implementation note: IORB **3.65%**, ON RRP **3.5%**, repo **3.75%**, bill purchases to maintain ample reserves; source: https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260429a1.htm

### Caveats
PCE expectation references come from public calendar/vendor pages rather than a Bloomberg terminal. Energy pass-through is nonlinear; if oil prices fall back, headline PCE can cool quickly, but if transportation, airline, chemical, and inflation-expectation channels broaden, core components may rise with a lag.

## Question 5: Geopolitical Safe-Haven Flows

### Method
I tested whether the cross-asset tape from 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01 showed a safe-haven pattern: USD, gold, and Treasuries rising while risk assets fell. I used LPL/Bloomberg weekly performance, AP closing data, Fed H.4.1 liquidity data, and holiday/central-bank context in Europe and Japan.

### Findings

| Asset/indicator | Weekly performance or level | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| S&P 500 | +0.95%; AP Friday close **7,230.12** | Risk assets made new highs |
| Nasdaq Composite | +1.24%; AP Friday close **25,114.44** | Tech earnings supported the tape |
| Nasdaq-100 | +1.49% 5-day change; close **27,710.36** | AI/cloud supported growth stocks |
| US10Y | Apr 24 **4.31%** to Apr 30 **4.40%**, +9bp | Bonds sold off; not classic Treasury safe-haven buying |
| Gold | -1.77% | No pre-holiday gold scramble |
| Oil | +8.19% | Geopolitical shock concentrated in energy |
| USD | LPL: U.S. dollar weakened; DXY futures around **98.01** on May 1 | No USD safe-haven bid |
| Fed reserves | Reserve balances **$2.919T**, +$16.8B WoW; ON RRP **$318.0B**, -$9.9B WoW | Liquidity did not show acute dollar scarcity |

Interpretation: Safe-haven buying was not the main story. Gold fell, the dollar weakened, and equities rose, the opposite of a USD-plus-gold precautionary bid. Geopolitical risk entered asset prices mainly through oil and inflation expectations. May Day closures thinned Europe/China trading, and Japan saw yen strength tied to intervention expectations, but that was a local FX story rather than a global safe-haven regime.

### Evidence
- LPL Weekly Market Performance, 2026-05-01: S&P 500 **+0.95%**, Nasdaq Composite **+1.24%**, Gold **-1.77%**, Oil **+8.19%**, U.S. dollar weakened; source: https://www.lpl.com/research/blog/weekly-market-performance-may-1-2026.html
- AP market close, 2026-05-01: S&P 500 **7,230.12**, Nasdaq Composite **25,114.44**, weekly S&P **+0.9%**; source: https://apnews.com/article/ee0fd67a1f14608ef46165f6dd693592
- Fed H.4.1, 2026-04-30: reserve balances **$2,918,599 million**, reverse repos **$317,998 million**, TGA **$981,929 million**; source: https://www.federalreserve.gov/Releases/H41/Current/

### Caveats
Exact DXY and XAU/USD weekly closes depend on the platform and futures/spot convention; this draft uses public weekly reports and verifiable closing quotes. May Day holidays reduced price discovery in parts of Europe and China.
