# Weekly FX Research: 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01

## Priority Conclusions

1. **The dollar story was not one-way strength; it was hawkish-rate support offset by a sharp yen shock into month-end.** The FOMC kept the federal funds target range at **3.50%–3.75%** and emphasized elevated inflation and energy-driven uncertainty. The calibrated DXY estimate rose to **99.20 on April 29**, then fell to **98.37 on April 30** as USD/JPY dropped from **160.439** to **156.619**.
2. **China’s macro signal supports RMB stability, but not a clean appreciation call.** China’s official manufacturing PMI was **50.3** in April, its second month in expansion, while non-manufacturing PMI fell to **49.4**, pointing to weak services and domestic demand. USD/CNY spot was almost unchanged for the week, from **6.8298** to **6.8282**, while the daily fixing weakened from **6.8579** to **6.8628**.
3. **The Labour Day holiday did not trigger a large CNH selloff; offshore RMB briefly traded stronger than onshore.** On April 30, USD/CNH was **6.8312** versus USD/CNY at **6.8332**, putting offshore RMB about **20 pips** stronger. On May 1, with onshore markets closed, the CNH-CNY gap moved back to about **+20 pips**, showing that holiday liquidity amplified two-way noise rather than a one-way depreciation move.
4. **The yen has entered the intervention-sensitive zone, raising carry-trade risk.** USD/JPY reached an April 30 intraday high of **160.725** and closed at **156.619**, with a **3.30%** high-low range. JPY appreciated **1.45%** against USD over the week, the strongest G10 performance in this sample.
5. **Euro area inflation did not strengthen the June rate-cut narrative; it weakened it.** Eurostat’s April HICP flash estimate rose to **3.0% y/y** from **2.6%** in March, led by energy at **10.9%**. EUR/USD was broadly flat over the week, as energy inflation support and growth-risk concerns offset each other.

## Research Basis and Data Notes

The coverage window is **2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01**, using New York or London daily closes where available. RMB fixings are the PBOC-authorized CFETS central parity rates. USD/CNY and G10 spot closes primarily use PoundSterlingLive historical tables. USD/CNH uses public historical tables and market snapshots, with April 30 at **6.8312** and May 1 at **6.8302**. Daily DXY values are reconstructed from the official DXY weights using EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD, USD/CAD, USD/SEK, and USD/CHF, then calibrated to the public April 30 month-end point of **98.37**. This is suitable for visualization and directional analysis, not as a substitute for ICE settlement data.

## Question 1: How did the Fed’s hawkish/dovish tilt reshape the dollar?

### Method

I tracked the April 29, 2026 FOMC statement, Powell’s press conference, calibrated DXY closes, 10Y UST yields, and major FX daily closes from April 27 to May 1. The weekly comparison uses April 24 close to May 1 close, with a separate focus on FOMC day, April 29, and the post-FOMC session, April 30.

### Findings

| Indicator | 2026-04-24 | 2026-04-29 | 2026-04-30 | 2026-05-01 | Weekly change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Fed funds target range | 3.50%–3.75% | 3.50%–3.75% | 3.50%–3.75% | 3.50%–3.75% | 0 bp |
| DXY calibrated estimate | 98.75 | 99.20 | 98.37 | 98.49 | -0.26% |
| 10Y UST | 4.33% | 4.42% | 4.40% | 4.39% | +6 bps |
| EUR/USD | 1.1721 | 1.1675 | 1.1732 | 1.1721 | 0.00% |
| USD/JPY | 159.370 | 160.439 | 156.619 | 157.065 | -1.45% |

The FOMC message was hawkish in rates terms: the Committee held **3.50%–3.75%** and retained data-dependent language on the timing and extent of future adjustments, while dissents showed disagreement over keeping an easing bias in the statement. The dollar initially benefited from higher Treasury yields and Powell’s emphasis on inflation risk, lifting the calibrated DXY estimate to **99.20** on April 29. The move reversed on April 30 when yen strength pulled USD/JPY sharply lower, dragging DXY back to **98.37**. The week therefore did not produce a clean “higher for longer equals stronger dollar” outcome; yen intervention risk and month-end rebalancing diluted the Fed impulse.

## Question 2: Did China’s April PMI support RMB stability?

### Method

I compared China’s official manufacturing, non-manufacturing, and composite PMI readings month on month, then cross-checked USD/CNY fixing, USD/CNY spot, and USD/CNH behavior from April 27 to May 1. PMI interpretation uses 50 as the expansion/contraction threshold.

### Findings

| Indicator | 2026-03 | 2026-04 | MoM change | Assessment |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Official manufacturing PMI | 50.4 | 50.3 | -0.1 | Second month in expansion |
| Official non-manufacturing PMI | 50.1 | 49.4 | -0.7 | Back in contraction |
| Composite PMI output index | 50.5 | 50.1 | -0.4 | Still expanding, slower momentum |
| USD/CNY spot | 6.9032 (Mar 31) | 6.8332 (Apr 30) | -1.01% | RMB strengthened in April |
| USD/CNY fixing | 6.8579 (Apr 27) | 6.8628 (Apr 30) | +49 pips | Slightly weaker fixing |

Manufacturing PMI at **50.3** is enough to support an RMB-stability argument, especially as production and export-linked activity remained resilient. But the drop in non-manufacturing PMI to **49.4** shows that domestic demand and services activity were still soft ahead of Labour Day. RMB spot strengthened over April, but the fixing did not keep reinforcing that move during the report week, suggesting the policy preference was stability rather than a one-way appreciation signal. The conclusion is that PMI supports RMB stabilization, not a confirmed new appreciation trend.

### Caveats

PMI is a diffusion index: it measures direction, not output volume. April was affected by Labour Day timing, export front-loading, and energy-price shocks. USD/CNY spot data in some April sessions had thin liquidity; the PoundSterlingLive series is better for directional analysis than execution-grade pricing.

## Question 3: How large was the CNH holiday effect?

### Method

I compared USD/CNH and USD/CNY closes around China’s Labour Day holiday and calculated the CNH-CNY gap as USD/CNH minus USD/CNY in pips. I also checked one-week RMB option implied volatility from month-start to month-end to see whether the market paid up for short-dated holiday hedges.

### Findings

| Date | USD/CNY close | USD/CNH close | CNH-CNY gap (pips) | Note |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 2026-04-24 | 6.8363 | 6.8500 | +137 | Offshore RMB weaker |
| 2026-04-27 | 6.8298 | 6.8420 | +122 | Pre-holiday risk premium remained |
| 2026-04-28 | 6.8375 | 6.8480 | +105 | Gap narrowed |
| 2026-04-29 | 6.8375 | 6.8468 | +93 | Orderly through FOMC risk |
| 2026-04-30 | 6.8332 | 6.8312 | -20 | Offshore RMB turned stronger |
| 2026-05-01 | 6.8282 | 6.8302 | +20 | Onshore closed, gap mild |

The holiday effect did not appear as a large CNH depreciation. Instead, offshore RMB briefly traded stronger than onshore on April 30, with the gap narrowing from **+137 pips** on April 24 to **-20 pips**. ChinaMoney’s April market data show USD/CNY ATM 1W implied volatility fell from **5.4000%** at month-start to **4.1500%** at month-end, so the options market did not demand a larger short-tenor holiday premium. The main holiday risk is that, with onshore markets closed, CNH becomes more sensitive to the dollar, yen, and global risk sentiment.

Free public USD/CNH data sources differ by timezone and closing convention. The May 1 USD/CNY point is an offshore/international vendor quote, not an official onshore trading close. The volatility series is USD/CNY ATM rather than a full USD/CNH surface; it is a useful proxy for RMB short-tenor volatility demand but not a perfect offshore option measure.

## Question 4: Has yen weakness hit the carry-trade stop-out zone?

### Method

I reviewed USD/JPY intraday highs/lows, closes, and weekly change, then placed that move against U.S. and Japan policy-rate differentials and 10Y yield context. The aim is to judge whether the carry return still compensates for tail-risk volatility.

### Findings

| Indicator | Value |
|---|---:|
| USD/JPY April 30 high | 160.725 |
| USD/JPY April 30 low | 155.569 |
| USD/JPY April 30 close | 156.619 |
| April 30 intraday range | 3.30% |
| USD/JPY change, Apr 24 to May 1 | -1.45% |
| BoJ policy rate upper, April 24 market table | 0.75% |
| Fed target upper | 3.75% |
| U.S.-Japan policy spread, upper-bound basis | 300 bps |

The yen is now in the intervention-sensitive zone: USD/JPY broke **160** but failed to hold it, then posted a more than **5 yen** high-low swing on April 30. The U.S.-Japan policy-rate spread is still about **300 bps**, so carry remains positive, but a single reversal of this size can erase months of carry income. JPY led G10 currencies from April 30 into May 1, showing that the market was pricing either intervention risk or a squeeze after official warnings. The stop-out line is not a single spot level; it is the combination of **above 160 + higher volatility + official warning risk**.

## Question 5: Did euro area inflation reinforce June rate-cut expectations?

### Method

I used Eurostat’s April HICP flash estimate, comparing headline and component inflation with March, then combined that with the ECB’s April 30 rate decision and EUR/USD price action.

### Findings

| Indicator | 2026-03 | 2026-04 | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Euro area HICP | 2.6% y/y | 3.0% y/y | +0.4 pp |
| Energy | 5.1% y/y | 10.9% y/y | +5.8 pp |
| Services | 3.2% y/y | 3.0% y/y | -0.2 pp |
| Food, alcohol & tobacco | 2.4% y/y | 2.5% y/y | +0.1 pp |
| Non-energy industrial goods | 0.5% y/y | 0.8% y/y | +0.3 pp |
| ECB main refinancing rate | 2.15% | 2.15% | 0 bp |
| ECB deposit facility rate | 2.00% | 2.00% | 0 bp |

The rise in April inflation from **2.6%** to **3.0%** does not support a June rate-cut narrative. Energy at **10.9%** was the main driver, but services inflation was still **3.0%**, making it difficult for the ECB to dismiss second-round risks entirely. EUR/USD rose from **1.1675** to **1.1732** on April 30, consistent with support from higher inflation and an ECB hold. The real euro constraint is the growth damage from an energy shock, not a clearer rate-cut path.

### Caveats

The April HICP reading is a flash estimate and may be revised. The ECB may treat energy as a supply shock rather than demand overheating, but persistence would reduce policy tolerance. This report does not use the full OIS curve; it infers policy pressure from inflation, the rate decision, and spot FX reaction.
