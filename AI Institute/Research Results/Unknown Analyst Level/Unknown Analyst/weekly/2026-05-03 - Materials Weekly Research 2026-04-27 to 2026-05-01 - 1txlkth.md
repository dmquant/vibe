## Question 1: Was the iron ore rally confirmed by steel fundamentals?

### Method

I compared `TIO=F` closing prices on 2026-04-24 and 2026-05-01, then checked finished-steel demand through Mysteel's change in five major steel-product retail inventories from 2026-04-24 to 2026-04-30. I also used the SteelOrbis China steel page and GMK Center's April iron ore coverage for industry context.

### Findings

| Indicator | 2026-04-24 | 2026-05-01 / 2026-04-30 | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| `TIO=F` iron ore futures close (USD/t) | 107.10 | 107.86 | +0.71% |
| `TIO=F` weekly high | 107.10 | 107.86 (2026-05-01) | New weekly high |
| Mysteel five major steel-product retail inventory (million tonnes) | 20.911 (implied) | 20.31 (2026-04-30) | -0.6011 million tonnes, -2.9% |
| `SLX` steel ETF close | 101.56 | 103.45 | +1.86% |

Iron ore rose only modestly, but the direction matched ongoing finished-steel destocking. Mysteel reported that inventories across 132 cities fell by 601,100 tonnes to 20.31 million tonnes from 2026-04-24 to 2026-04-30, indicating that seasonal demand or pre-holiday restocking was still absorbing supply. Steel equities also outperformed: `SLX` rose +1.86% while `XLB` fell -1.10%, showing a preference for the steel chain over broad materials. The constraint is that `TIO=F` was up only +0.33% from April 1 to May 1, so the move looks more like range repair than a trend breakout.

### Caveats

The near-term conclusion is high-level consolidation and pullback, not a fresh acceleration higher.

Lithium equities were strong, indicating that the market is trading bottom confirmation and recovery in battery-material prices. The conclusion is that a lithium price floor is partially confirmed, but the exact weekly spot increase cannot be quantified from public data.
