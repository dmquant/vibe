## Question 2: Stagflation Risk

### Method
I used BEA’s Q1 GDP advance estimate and March Personal Income and Outlays release, comparing the last six quarters of real GDP and core PCE price growth. The test is whether growth is persistently below potential while inflation reaccelerates.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest value | Prior/comparison | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Q1 2026 real GDP | `2.0%` SAAR | Q4 2025 was `0.5%` | Growth recovered; this is not recessionary low growth |
| Q1 2026 core PCE prices | `4.3%` SAAR | Q4 2025 was `2.7%` | Core inflation reaccelerated sharply |
| March PCE prices | `0.7%` m/m, `3.5%` y/y | February was `0.4%` m/m | Energy lifted headline inflation |
| March core PCE prices | `0.3%` m/m, `3.2%` y/y | February was `0.4%` m/m, `3.0%` y/y | Core year-over-year inflation moved up |
| March real PCE | `0.2%` m/m | February was `0.3%` | Real consumption slowed but did not contract |

Q1 GDP growth of `2.0%` and real final sales to private domestic purchasers of `2.5%` show that domestic demand still has resilience, so the evidence for stagflation is incomplete. The problem is the price side: Q1 core PCE prices at `4.3%` SAAR and March core PCE at `3.2%` y/y both moved in the wrong direction, meaning stronger growth did not create room for cuts. March headline PCE at `0.7%` m/m was heavily energy-driven, but core PCE above 3% shows this is not just oil noise. The base case is a soft landing with renewed inflation pressure, not confirmed stagflation.

## Question 3: Cost Pass-Through Pressure

### Method
I used the April 2026 ISM Manufacturing PMI and the Dallas Fed Texas Manufacturing Outlook Survey, comparing PMI, new orders, employment, prices paid, raw-material prices, and finished-goods prices. The pass-through framework is: ISM prices first pressure PPI and goods chains, then core PCE goods and services need to confirm.

### Findings

| Indicator | April 2026 | March 2026 | Change | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| ISM Manufacturing PMI | `52.7` | `52.7` | `0.0` | Manufacturing kept expanding |
| ISM New Orders | `54.1` | `53.5` | `+0.6` | Demand improved marginally |
| ISM Employment | `46.4` | `48.7` | `-2.3` | Factory employment remained in contraction |
| ISM Prices Index | `84.6` | `78.3` | `+6.3` | Cost pressure intensified |
| Dallas Fed raw materials prices | `37.0` | `32.7` | `+4.3` | Regional input prices rose |
| Dallas Fed finished goods prices | `27.6` | Not listed | `+9.0` points | Highest since July 2022 |

The ISM Prices Index at `84.6`, up `25.6` points over three months, is the week’s clearest cost-push signal. The PMI holding at `52.7` shows expansion did not break, and new orders at `54.1` suggest firms still have some ability to pass costs through. The limitation is that core PCE is services-heavy, while ISM manufacturing prices map more directly to goods, raw materials, and PPI. The conclusion is that upside risk is higher for headline CPI/PCE and PPI over the next two months; a second core PCE rebound is more likely, but it needs confirmation from services prices or wages.

### Evidence

### Caveats
The ISM Prices Index is a diffusion index, not a price-growth rate. The Dallas Fed survey is regional. Neither can be mechanically converted into core PCE.

## Question 4: Labor Cost Trend

### Method
I used BLS Employment Cost Index data for Q1 2026, reviewing total compensation, wages, benefits, and real wages across civilian workers, private industry, and state and local government. The key question is whether labor costs are strong enough to form a wage-price spiral.

### Findings

| Category | Total compensation, 3-month SA | Wages, 3-month SA | Benefits, 3-month SA | Total compensation, 12-month NSA | Wages, 12-month NSA | Benefits, 12-month NSA |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Civilian workers | `0.9%` | `0.8%` | `1.2%` | `3.4%` | `3.4%` | `3.6%` |
| Private industry | `0.9%` | `0.7%` | `1.3%` | `3.4%` | `3.4%` | `3.6%` |
| State and local government | `1.0%` | `1.0%` | `1.2%` | `3.5%` | `3.4%` | `3.6%` |
| Private health benefits | Not listed | Not listed | Not listed | Not listed | Not listed | `5.7%` |

ECI shows labor costs are not out of control, but they remain sticky. Private industry wages rose only `0.7%` over three months, so there is no obvious wage reacceleration; however, benefits at `1.3%` and health benefits at `5.7%` y/y still raise total employer costs. Inflation-adjusted private wages and salaries rose only `0.1%` y/y, so wages are not strong enough by themselves to drive demand-led inflation. The Fed should read this as “stay patient,” not “hike now” or “cut with confidence.”

### Evidence
- BLS, `Employment Cost Index - March 2026`, 2026-04-30: https://www.bls.gov/news.release/eci.nr0.htm
- BLS, `ECI Home`, latest numbers: https://www.bls.gov/eci/

### Caveats
ECI is quarterly and lagged, and the March 2026 release included new seasonal adjustment factors. Benefit costs are sensitive to industry mix and insurance pricing.
