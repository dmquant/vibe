# Weekly Market Sentiment Research: April 27 to May 1, 2026

## Priority Conclusions

1. **The AI narrative still supported risk appetite, but it was not the market’s only driver.** QQQ rose **1.55%** for the week and XLK rose **1.03%**; ETF.com recorded April 30 net inflows of **$2.410B, $1.052B, $0.709B, and $0.614B** into VGT, QQQ, IGV, and SMH, respectively. Still, XLE gained **3.48%** for the week, ahead of XLK, showing that oil and energy risk remained an important competing theme.
2. **“Higher for Longer” was largely priced into the front end of the rate path.** The official FOMC target range was **3.50%-3.75%**, not the **5.25%-5.50%** stated in the plan; a CME FedWatch snapshot showed the probability of no change by September 2026 rising from **92.7%** to **96.1%**, while the probability of a 25 bp cut fell from **7.2%** to **3.8%**.
3. **There was no synchronized safe-haven bid in gold and the dollar.** GLD fell **2.32%** and UUP fell **0.25%** for the week; their daily return correlation was **-0.89**, not a joint defensive move. The defensive signal was narrower: SKEW rose from **139.08** to **141.38**, while VIX fell from **18.71** to **16.99**.
4. **Retail investors turned cautious while institutional/configuration flows still bought risk, especially core indexes and monetizable AI infrastructure exposure.** AAII-related reporting showed the bull-bear spread falling from **+11.6%** to **-1.6%**, with Bulls at **38.1%** and Bears at **39.7%**; ETF.com recorded April 30 net inflows of **$7.414B** into U.S. equity ETFs and **$11.395B** into all ETFs.
5. **The planned May 1 April NFP event did not exist.** The BLS stated that the April Employment Situation would be released at **8:30 a.m. ET on 2026-05-08**; the relevant official labor data available during the week were the March jobs report and the April 30 weekly jobless claims data.

## Data and Method Overview

The coverage window is **2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01**, with most comparisons measured from the **2026-04-24 close to the 2026-05-01 close**. Prices and sector ETF performance use StockAnalysis historical closes; VIX, VIX9D, VIX3M, VIX6M, and SKEW use official Cboe historical CSV files; Put/Call uses YCharts and Cboe daily statistics; flows use ETF.com daily/weekly ETF flows and ICI/First Trust summaries; macro inputs use the Federal Reserve, BLS, and a CME FedWatch snapshot. Raw EPFR sector flows and complete daily CNN Fear & Greed history were not available from open sources, so this report substitutes public ETF flow data and verifiable Fear & Greed snapshots.

## Question 2: Is “Higher for Longer” Fully Priced In?

### Method

I used the official Federal Reserve FOMC statement to verify the policy rate and vote split; a CME FedWatch snapshot to measure the change in cut probabilities after the decision; and the BLS release calendar to verify whether an April NFP release occurred on May 1. The comparison basis is the pre- and post-FOMC probability shift.

### Findings

| Indicator | Pre-Decision / Plan Value | Post-Decision / Verified Value | Sentiment Meaning |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Fed funds target range | Plan stated 5.25%-5.50% | Officially 3.50%-3.75% | The plan’s rate assumption was wrong |
| FOMC vote | N/A | 8 for, 4 against | Large split, with both dovish and hawkish objections |
| June no-change probability | 98.8% | 98.6% | No cut was almost fully priced |
| July no-change probability | 94.6% | 96.5% | Cut expectations moved further out |
| September no-change probability | 92.7% | 96.1% | The market trusted the Fed more than a weak-labor narrative |
| September 25 bp cut probability | 7.2% | 3.8% | Cut odds were roughly halved |
| April NFP release date | Plan stated 2026-05-01 | Officially 2026-05-08 | There was no April NFP on May 1 |

The front-end “Higher for Longer” regime was essentially accepted by markets. The April 29 FOMC statement kept the range at **3.50%-3.75%**, and three dissenters opposed the easing bias rather than demanding a cut, which reduced confidence in a rapid 2026 easing cycle. The alleged May 1 weak NFP release did not occur, so there was no official April payrolls shock to offset the FOMC. Stocks still rose, which suggests that investors treated “higher for longer” as a background condition while earnings and flows drove the short-term tape.

## Question 4: Are Retail and Institutional Positions Diverging?

### Method

For retail sentiment, I used AAII-related reporting on Bulls, Bears, and the bull-bear spread; for institutional/configuration flows, I used ETF.com daily/weekly flows, ICI/First Trust flow summaries, and FINRA margin debt. Raw EPFR data was not publicly downloadable, so public ETF flows are used as the substitute.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest Value | Prior / Comparison | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| AAII Bulls | 38.1% | 46.0% | Retail optimism cooled |
| AAII Bears | 39.7% | 34.4% | Retail concern increased |
| AAII bull-bear spread | -1.6% | +11.6% | Retail turned notably cautious |
| April 30 total ETF net inflow | $11.395B | N/A | Institutional/configuration capital returned |
| April 30 U.S. equity ETF net inflow | $7.414B | N/A | Risk assets were bought |
| Week ended April 24 U.S.-listed ETF net inflow | $36.8B | YTD near $600B | Weekly flows remained strong |
| Week ended April 22 ICI ETF net issuance | $47.90B | N/A | Aggregate ETF demand was strong |
| FINRA margin debt | $1.22T | +39% YoY | Leveraged risk appetite was elevated |

Retail and institutional behavior did diverge. The AAII-related bull-bear spread fell from **+11.6%** to **-1.6%**, showing retail investors became more cautious at index highs; ETF flows, however, still showed buying of U.S. equities, QQQ, VGT, and SMH. That said, institutional money was not indiscriminately chasing high beta: XLK saw **-$504.91M** of outflows on April 28, and ARKK saw **-$2.861B** of outflows on April 29, showing that volatile thematic capital remained quick to exit. The cleanest conclusion is “retail cautious, configuration flows still buying core indexes and AI infrastructure.”
