# Agriculture Weekly Research Report (2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01)

## Priority Conclusions

This week’s agricultural pricing story was not “delayed U.S. planting plus a La Niña return.” It was “fast U.S. corn/soybean planting, elevated spring wheat and winter wheat weather risk, and a broader weather/geopolitical risk premium in grains.” CBOT July corn rose from $4.63 1/2/bu on 2026-04-24 to $4.80 1/4/bu on 2026-05-01, up 3.6% for the week; July soybeans gained 2.1%; July CBOT wheat gained 3.4%. Chinese hog prices showed a short pre-holiday rebound, but the hog-to-corn ratio was still only 4.03, well below the usual breakeven zone. The sow herd decline to 39.04 million head supports a bottoming-cycle argument, but it does not yet prove a supply-demand reversal. The food-security premium should be framed as weather and trade-policy tail risk rather than an immediate low-stock story: 2025/26 global wheat stocks-to-use is about 34.5%, and global rice stocks-to-use is about 35.6%, neither of which is a defensive-low inventory setting.

## Question 1: Planting-Window Challenge

### Method

I used the USDA Crop Progress report released on 2026-04-27, covering conditions through 2026-04-26, and compared current progress, prior-week progress, the 2025 same-period number where available, and the five-year average for corn, soybeans, and spring wheat. I cross-checked USDA figures through Successful Farming and Reuters summaries. The replanting-pressure assessment is based on whether progress is behind the five-year average and whether a large share remains unplanted by late May.

### Findings

Corn and soybeans were not delayed; both were ahead of the five-year average, so late-May replanting pressure is not the central risk at this point. The more relevant weather risk is wheat: spring wheat planting was 3 pct behind average, with limited fieldwork days in North Dakota, while winter wheat good/excellent ratings were only 30%, far below 49% a year earlier. Grain price strength therefore came more from wheat quality risk, global weather concerns, and geopolitical energy risk than from a failed U.S. corn/soybean planting start. If early-May Midwest rains materially reduce fieldwork windows, corn’s early cushion could shrink, but the current data do not justify cutting U.S. corn or soybean trend yield assumptions.

Late-April hog prices did show resilience, but the move was mainly driven by slaughterhouse stocking, shifting supply rhythm, and sentiment repair after a long price decline. The sow herd at 39.04 million head, down about 1.5% quarter on quarter and 3.3% year on year, supports the view that capacity is being reduced, but the decline is not large enough to confirm an above-expectation contraction. A hog-to-corn ratio of 4.03 still points to losses, which should restrain restocking and help later supply. The conclusion is that bottoming signals are stronger, but April data have not fully confirmed a cycle reversal.

Summer-crop margins in 2026 are only marginally better than 2025, not structurally repaired. After this week’s futures rally, corn moved near the full-cost breakeven range, while soybeans had a better cushion. But energy, fertilizer, and interest costs are still rising, and ERS expects 2026 production expenses to grow 1.0% in nominal terms. Fertilizer export controls and Middle East energy disruption increase volatility in nitrogen and phosphate prices, so selling-price improvement can be absorbed by higher input costs. The cleaner conclusion is that soybean margin elasticity is better than corn, while corn still needs strong yields or policy payments to stabilize returns on cash-rented ground.

## Question 4: Food-Security Premium

### Method

I calculated stocks-to-use from USDA April 2026 WASDE global wheat and rice tables: stocks-to-use = ending stocks / total domestic use. I then combined the inventory picture with Indian rice policy, Brazil safrinha moisture conditions, NOAA ENSO probabilities, and this week’s price action to assess the source of the risk premium.

### Findings

| Commodity | 2025/26 production | Domestic use | Ending stocks | Stocks-to-use | YoY stock change | Judgment |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Global wheat | 844.15 MMT | 820.12 MMT | 283.12 MMT | 34.5% | +23.35 MMT | Not low-stock; price driven by weather risk |
| Global wheat excluding China | 704.08 MMT | 672.12 MMT | 158.28 MMT | 23.5% | +26.96 MMT | Tradable stocks are tighter, but not extreme |
| Global rice | 541.35 MMT | 540.57 MMT | 192.31 MMT | 35.6% | +1.00 MMT | Ample total stocks, concentrated inventories |
| Global rice excluding China | 395.02 MMT | 393.44 MMT | 87.31 MMT | 22.2% | N/A | Trade side is sensitive to Indian policy |
| India rice ending stocks | N/A | 127.00 MMT | 49.00 MMT | 38.6% | N/A | Export policy has room to loosen |

Global wheat and rice stocks-to-use have not reached defensive historical lows. Wheat rallied because U.S. winter wheat ratings were weak, spring wheat planting was slow, Australia and Asia faced weather stress, and energy/geopolitical risk lifted the broader risk premium. Rice is a “high total stocks, concentrated tradable stocks” market: Indian inventories and export policy have a large influence on FOB prices, and Indian 5% broken parboiled rice at $337-$344/tonne in late April suggests price pressure has eased. A food-security premium should remain, but it is more like option value than a shortage directly triggered by current stocks.
