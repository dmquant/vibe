# Weekly Bond Research: April 27-May 1, 2026

> The upstream file `01_weekly_plan.md` was missing from the workspace. This report reconstructs the research agenda from the prior-step summary: conflicting U.S. macro data, the BoJ/JGB policy shift, China credit recovery, and the implications for rates, curves, duration, and credit allocation.

## Priority Conclusions

1. **Treasuries traded as an inflation and term-premium repricing, not as a growth-scare rally.** From April 24 to May 1, 2026, 2Y/5Y/10Y/30Y Treasury yields rose `+10/+10/+8/+6 bps`, with the 10Y ending at `4.39%`. Q1 GDP rebounded to `2.0%` from `0.5%` in Q4, while the March PCE price index rose `0.7%` m/m, core PCE rose `0.3%` m/m, and the ISM Prices Index jumped to `84.6`, leaving little room for a sustained long-duration rally.
2. **The curve remained in a bear-flattening regime, so duration should not be added aggressively.** 2s10s narrowed from `53 bps` to `51 bps`, and 5s30s narrowed from `99 bps` to `95 bps`. The front end and belly reacted more to “growth is not weak plus inflation is sticky”; new duration should be concentrated inside 5Y, with 10Y+ duration underweight or hedged.
3. **The BoJ held at 0.75%, but the 6-3 vote shows hawkish pressure is now inside the reaction function.** Three board members favored lifting the overnight call-rate target to `1.0%`; the JGB 10Y rose from `2.435%` to `2.515%`, and the 30Y from `3.655%` to `3.730%`. Supply-demand risk in Japan’s super-long sector remains elevated.
4. **China credit is showing a seasonal rebound, not a full releveraging cycle.** March new RMB loans were `RMB2.99 trillion` and aggregate financing flow was `RMB5.23 trillion`, sharply above February, but Q1 new loans of `RMB8.6 trillion` were below `RMB9.78 trillion` in Q1 2025, and outstanding aggregate financing growth slowed to `7.9%` y/y from `8.2%` in February. Credit spreads can be supported by liquidity, but the evidence for a broad fundamental expansion is still insufficient.
5. **Credit risk pricing remains friendly but not cheap.** ICE BofA US HY OAS tightened from `2.86%` to `2.83%`, while US IG OAS edged from `0.80%` to `0.81%`. The better risk-adjusted trade is high-quality carry and short-to-intermediate credit, not chasing spread compression in lower-rated long-duration credit.

## Research Question 3: Is China’s Credit Recovery Strong Enough to Support Bond Risk Appetite?

### Method

I used PBOC-related reporting and market data to track March credit, outstanding aggregate financing, and aggregate financing flow, comparing February, March, and the Q1 2025 benchmark. Because full April credit data had not been released by May 1, this section focuses on March and Q1 data.

### Findings

| New RMB loans | RMB0.90 tn | RMB2.99 tn | +RMB2.09 tn |

| 5Y+ LPR | 3.5% | 3.5% | 0 bps |
| China 10Y government bond yield | 1.8064% (April 16, ChinaBond spot) | 1.7473%/1.75% (April 30, ChinaBond/MacroMicro) | about -6 bps |

Interpretation: March credit rebounded sharply from February, but that is consistent with quarter-end and post-Lunar New Year loan timing rather than definitive evidence of a broad credit cycle restart. Outstanding aggregate financing growth slowed from `8.2%` to `7.9%`, and Q1 new loans were below the same period in 2025, signaling still-cautious real-economy financing demand. The LPR was left unchanged for the 11th consecutive month, pointing to liquidity support and targeted easing rather than broad rate-cut stimulus. For bond allocation, China rates still offer a low-yield anchor, while credit exposure should favor high-certainty SOE/LGFV-type issuers; property-chain and weaker private credits remain vulnerable.

## Research Question 4: What Are the Duration, Curve, and Credit Allocation Conclusions?

### Method

I combined UST, JGB, China rates, and US IG/HY OAS to form portfolio guidance across duration risk, curve shape, credit compensation, and policy-event risk. Credit spreads use FRED/ICE BofA daily series: `BAMLC0A0CM` and `BAMLH0A0HYM2`.

### Findings

| Allocation Dimension | Numeric Basis | Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| Treasury duration | UST 10Y +8 bps to 4.39%, 30Y +6 bps to 4.97% | Neutral to underweight long duration |
| Treasury curve | 2s10s -2 bps, 5s30s -4 bps | Favor 2Y-5Y carry in bear flattening |
| JGB risk | JGB 10Y +8 bps, 40Y +11 bps; BoJ 6-3 | Avoid unhedged super-long JGB exposure |
| China rates | China 10Y about 1.75%, LPR unchanged | Low-vol rates carry; credit requires issuer selection |
| US IG | OAS 0.80% to 0.81% | Carry is holdable, spread compensation is limited |
| US HY | OAS 2.86% to 2.83% | Do not chase lower-rated risk; prefer BB/B short-to-intermediate tenors |

Interpretation: The main portfolio lesson is not to treat the rate rise as an automatic long-duration buying opportunity, because inflation and policy uncertainty remain active. The 2Y-5Y Treasury sector offers better risk-adjusted carry, while 10Y+ exposure is more vulnerable to term premium, fiscal supply, and overseas demand. JGB curve moves show that the global long-end supply-demand anchor is weakening, and dollar investors should monitor Japanese reallocation risk. In credit, spreads are not wide; expected return is more about coupon carry than spread tightening.

### Evidence

- U.S. Treasury Daily Treasury Par Yield Curve Rates, April 24-May 1, 2026.
- Japan Bond Trading Co. JGB Rates, April 24-April 30, 2026.
- FRED/ICE BofA: `BAMLC0A0CM`, April 24 `0.80%`, April 30 `0.81%`; `BAMLH0A0HYM2`, April 24 `2.86%`, April 30 `2.83%`.
- BoJ Statement and JGB purchase schedule, April 28 and April 30, 2026.

### Caveats

OAS is index-level data and does not represent new-issue concessions or single-name liquidity. Duration guidance does not incorporate client liability duration, tax treatment, or benchmark constraints; implementation should be recalibrated to the specific portfolio mandate.
