# Week 18 2026 Market Sentiment Research

Coverage window: 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01. Bottom line: this was not a “sticky inflation breaks risk appetite” week. It was a “inflation anxiety remains, but earnings and the AI capex narrative still dominate” week. The S&P 500 rose 0.9% on the week to 7,230.12, VIX fell from 18.71 to 16.99, and the AAII bull-bear spread dropped from +11.6 to -1.6, meaning retail investors turned more cautious while price action, volatility, and sector leadership still leaned risk-on.

## Question 1: Inflation Persistence vs. Rate-Cut Expectations

**Method**: I used the BEA Personal Income and Outlays release published on 2026-04-30 for March 2026, comparing February and March headline/core PCE monthly and yearly rates. I then cross-checked S&P 500 action and available Fear & Greed references to assess whether “greed” was materially damaged.

| Indicator | February 2026 | March 2026 | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| PCE price index MoM | +0.4% | +0.7% | +0.3pct |
| Core PCE MoM | +0.4% | +0.3% | -0.1pct |
| PCE price index YoY | +2.8% | +3.5% | +0.7pct |
| Core PCE YoY | +3.0% | +3.2% | +0.2pct |
| Real PCE MoM | +0.3% | +0.2% | -0.1pct |
| CNN Fear & Greed reference | 63.8 prior | 63.4 on 04-30 | -0.4 points |

**Findings**: PCE clearly showed that disinflation had stalled, especially with headline YoY jumping from 2.8% to 3.5% and core YoY rising from 3.0% to 3.2%. The core monthly rate eased from 0.4% to 0.3%, however, which kept markets from treating the report as runaway inflation. The S&P 500 rose 1.0% on 04-30 to 7,209.01, showing that earnings and AI investment expectations offset macro pressure. The public CNN Fear & Greed reference was still in greed territory at 63.4 on 04-30 rather than neutral; “greed” was dented, not reversed.

**Evidence**: BEA reported March PCE +0.7% MoM, core +0.3% MoM, PCE +3.5% YoY, and core +3.2% YoY. The Mortgage Reports cited CNN Fear & Greed slipping from 63.8 to 63.4 on 04-30. AP/Washington Post recorded the S&P 500 rising 73.06 points to 7,209.01 on 04-30.

**Caveats**: CNN does not provide a complete public bulk download of daily Fear & Greed history, so this report uses public secondary references. Different vendors may publish proprietary “Fear & Greed” models, which should not be treated as the same time series.

## Question 2: Liquidity Vacuum Risk

**Method**: I used the official CBOE VIX historical CSV for 04-24 through 05-01 and AP daily S&P 500 closes. I compared the 05-01 VIX move against 04-30, the weekly VIX change, and the intraweek closing high.

| Date | S&P 500 close | Daily change | VIX close | VIX daily change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 2026-04-24 | 7,165.08 | +0.80% | 18.71 | - |
| 2026-04-27 | 7,173.91 | +0.12% | 18.02 | -0.69 |
| 2026-04-28 | 7,138.80 | -0.49% | 17.83 | -0.19 |
| 2026-04-29 | 7,135.95 | -0.04% | 18.81 | +0.98 |
| 2026-04-30 | 7,209.01 | +1.02% | 16.89 | -1.92 |
| 2026-05-01 | 7,230.12 | +0.29% | 16.99 | +0.10 |

**Findings**: Despite May Day closures across Asia, Hong Kong, and much of Europe, VIX only rose from 16.89 to 16.99 on 05-01, a 0.10-point increase, which is not an abnormal spike. The real intraweek stress point was 04-29, when VIX rose from 17.83 to 18.81 while the S&P 500 slipped 0.04%, matching oil and rate anxiety. After the PCE release on 04-30, VIX fell sharply to 16.89, showing renewed volatility selling. For the full week, VIX declined from 18.71 to 16.99, meaning risk premia compressed rather than expanded.

**Evidence**: CBOE VIX History CSV; AP 04-27, 04-28, 04-29, and 05-01 daily index closes; Washington Post/AP 04-30 daily index close.

**Caveats**: VIX measures U.S. equity option-implied volatility, not global cash-market liquidity directly. U.S. markets were open on 05-01, so the low-liquidity thesis mainly concerns reduced cross-market participation rather than a U.S. exchange holiday.

## Question 3: Retail vs. Institutional

**Method**: I compared the AAII 04-30 weekly survey with CFTC Traders in Financial Futures data for 2026-04-28, using CME S&P 500 Consolidated and NASDAQ-100 Consolidated positioning. The goal was to compare retail opinion surveys with institutional futures exposure.

| Indicator | Latest | Previous | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| AAII Bullish | 38.1% | 46.0% | -7.9pct |
| AAII Neutral | 22.2% | 19.5% | +2.7pct |
| AAII Bearish | 39.7% | 34.4% | +5.3pct |
| AAII Bull-Bear Spread | -1.6pct | +11.6pct | -13.2pct |
| S&P 500 Asset Manager net | +998,208 | +1,019,328 | -21,120 |
| S&P 500 Leveraged Money net | -400,613 | -405,927 | +5,314 |
| NASDAQ-100 Asset Manager net | +80,004 | +85,805 | -5,801 |
| NASDAQ-100 Leveraged Money net | -55,663 | -54,238 | -1,425 |

**Findings**: AAII retail sentiment moved quickly from clearly bullish to mildly bearish, with the bull-bear spread falling 13.2 percentage points in one week. CFTC data showed Asset Managers still nearly one million contracts net long S&P 500 futures, although less long than the prior week; Leveraged Money remained heavily net short, though slightly less short in S&P 500. The divergence is therefore not “retail euphoric while institutions exit.” It is “retail cautious, long-only institutions still long, leveraged money still hedged,” a defensive-uptrend structure typical of high-level markets.

**Evidence**: Investrade’s 04-30 note cited AAII Bullish 38.1%, Neutral 22.2%, Bearish 39.7%, and Bull-Bear Spread -1.6. CFTC `fut_fin_txt_2026.zip`, report date 2026-04-28.

**Caveats**: AAII is an opinion survey, not a position measure. CFTC futures categories cover only futures exposures and do not represent the full universe of cash equities, ETFs, options, or OTC positions.

## Question 4: AI Sector Sentiment Divergence

**Method**: I used LPL’s 05-01 S&P 500 sector weekly returns and cross-checked AP/Schaeffers/Investrade reporting for 04-28, 04-30, and 05-01 earnings and options-market context. The question was whether capital rotated out of high-valuation technology into defensive value, or instead rotated within the AI complex.

| Sector | Weekly return | One month | YTD |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Communication Services | +4.42% | +16.34% | +9.87% |
| Energy | +3.53% | -0.65% | +31.05% |
| Financials | +1.16% | +5.31% | -5.02% |
| Consumer Staples | +1.08% | +3.38% | +10.00% |
| Real Estate | +0.96% | +7.88% | +10.38% |
| Health Care | +0.96% | -1.59% | -6.05% |
| Utilities | +0.89% | +1.08% | +9.16% |
| Consumer Discretionary | +0.80% | +11.72% | +2.21% |
| Industrials | +0.55% | +5.47% | +11.83% |
| Information Technology | +0.22% | +17.89% | +8.20% |
| Materials | -1.51% | +1.60% | +12.33% |

**Findings**: The AI trade did not disappear, but it shifted from a single hardware/chip chase into internal rotation across cloud, platform, advertising, and Apple earnings confirmation. AP noted on 04-28 that Nvidia and Broadcom were major market drags, showing profit-taking in valuation-sensitive AI and chip names. Alphabet then helped push indexes to records on 04-30, and Apple supported the Nasdaq on 05-01 after earnings. Communication Services led the week at +4.42% while Information Technology gained only +0.22%, implying capital stayed in AI beneficiaries but changed vehicles. Energy’s +3.53% was more about oil shock pricing than a classic defensive rotation.

**Evidence**: LPL 05-01 sector weekly returns; AP 04-28 reporting that Nvidia and Broadcom weighed on the market; Washington Post/AP 04-30 reporting Alphabet-led strength; Schaeffers 05-01 reporting Apple’s post-earnings strength and a Cboe equity put/call ratio of 0.48.

**Caveats**: Sector returns are index-level data and cannot separate active mutual funds, ETFs, hedge funds, and retail orders. Large technology companies are spread across Information Technology, Communication Services, and Consumer Discretionary, so XLK alone understates AI narrative strength.
