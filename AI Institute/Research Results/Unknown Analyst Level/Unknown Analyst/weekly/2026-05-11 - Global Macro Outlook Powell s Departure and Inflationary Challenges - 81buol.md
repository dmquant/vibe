# Global Macro Outlook: Powell's Departure and Inflationary Challenges
**Date:** May 11, 2026
**Author:** Chief Economist

## Executive Summary
This week marks a significant turning point for global markets. Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell is scheduled to conclude his term on May 15, with market attention shifting toward the policy stance of his expected successor, Kevin Warsh. Persistent geopolitical tensions have kept Brent crude prices elevated between $100 and $105/bbl, intensifying inflationary pressures in the US and Europe. Upcoming CPI data from both the US and China will serve as critical benchmarks for the "higher-for-longer" narrative and the trajectory of China's economic recovery.

---

## 1. US Macro: Inflation Stickiness & Fed Leadership Transition
*   **The End of the Powell Era:** Jerome Powell will officially step down this Friday. Markets anticipate Kevin Warsh to take the helm, potentially signaling a more hawkish shift or a renewed focus on monetary aggregates.
*   **CPI Test:** US CPI data scheduled for May 12 is forecasted at 3.6%–3.7% YoY. While core inflation shows signs of moderation, the energy-driven surge in headline CPI keeps it well above targets, likely delaying any pivot to rate cuts.
*   **Consumer Resilience:** Retail Sales are expected to grow by 1.2% MoM, indicating that the US consumer remains resilient despite sustained price pressures.

## 2. China Economy: Demand Recovery & Tech Expansion
*   **Price Normalization:** China’s April CPI (released today) is expected within the 0.8%–1.0% range, suggesting a gradual improvement in domestic demand and easing deflationary concerns.
*   **Thematic Drivers:** The widespread adoption of DeepSeek V4 is enhancing total factor productivity in the manufacturing sector. As global AI infrastructure spending accelerates, the international expansion of Chinese AI models is becoming a new engine for service trade growth.

## 3. Geopolitics & Commodities
*   **Middle East Tensions:** Ongoing conflict continues to threaten oil supply chains. High crude prices represent a "second-round shock" to global inflation.
*   **Energy Costs in Europe:** Germany’s CPI is forecasted to tick up to 3.0%, underscoring the severe energy-driven inflationary pressures still facing the Eurozone.
