# Industrials Weekly Research Draft

**Coverage window:** 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01  
**Conclusion first:** Global manufacturing has recovered from the 2025 trough, but this is not a clean, low-risk synchronized expansion; aerospace bottlenecks remain engine-led; defense budgets are strong, but the April 27 public contract flow did not verify a single `$50B` direct award to LMT/RTX; the automation AI premium is strongest in electrification, data centers, and industrial software rather than a broad surge in traditional robot orders.

## Question 1: Has Global Manufacturing Bottomed?

### Method

I used April 2026 PMI releases and order data available publicly as of 2026-05-04, comparing April versus March. China uses the NBS official manufacturing PMI; the US uses ISM Manufacturing PMI plus New Orders, Inventories, Customers' Inventories, and Prices; Europe and Japan use S&P Global/HCOB or au Jibun Bank/S&P Global measures; US hard-order data uses Census durable goods and Trading Economics' Census-based core capital goods summary.

### Findings

| Indicator | 2026-04 or latest month | Previous | Change | Direction |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| China NBS Manufacturing PMI | `50.3` | `50.4` | `-0.1` | Expanding, slower |
| China NBS New Orders | `50.6` | `51.6` | `-1.0` | Expanding, slower |
| China NBS Production | `51.5` | `51.4` | `+0.1` | Slightly better |
| US ISM Manufacturing PMI | `52.7` | `52.7` | `0.0` | Fourth straight expansion month |
| US ISM New Orders | `54.1` | `53.5` | `+0.6` | Orders improving |
| US ISM Inventories | `49.0` | `47.1` | `+1.9` | Still contracting, slower drawdown |
| US ISM Customers' Inventories | `39.1` | `40.1` | `-1.0` | Still “too low” |
| US ISM Prices | `84.6` | `78.3` | `+6.3` | Cost pressure accelerating |
| Eurozone flash Manufacturing PMI | `52.2` | `51.6` | `+0.6` | 47-month high |
| Japan Manufacturing PMI | `55.1` | `51.6` | `+3.5` | Highest since January 2022 |
| US Durable Goods Orders, Mar-26 | `+0.8% m/m` | `-1.2% m/m` | `+2.0 ppts` | Rebound |
| US Core Capital Goods Orders ex-aircraft, Mar-26 | `+3.3% m/m` | `+1.6% m/m` | `+1.7 ppts` | Fastest in nearly six years |

The answer is “yes, the trough is behind us, but the expansion quality is mixed.” The US ISM mix of orders, production, and very low customer inventories supports a restocking cycle, while the `+3.3%` core capital goods print shows business equipment demand is not just a defense story. China remains above 50, but new orders fell from `51.6` to `50.6`, suggesting export resilience and front-loaded purchasing are stronger than domestic demand. Japan and the Eurozone improved sharply, but S&P Global commentary points to energy, geopolitical risks, and supply-chain front-loading, which may overstate underlying end-demand strength.

### Caveats

PMI is a diffusion index, not an order-dollar measure; April readings were affected by Middle East conflict, oil prices, and tariff-related front-loading. ASEAN country-level April PMI values could not be obtained in a complete and consistent free public source, so this report uses S&P Global's February “record ASEAN upturn” only as background, not as chart data.

## Question 2: Is the Defense “Super Cycle” Already Priced?

### Method

I separated budgets, contracts, and market pricing. Budget inputs come from the FY26 DoD briefing, MDA budget reporting, and the House Defense Appropriations summary. Contract flow focuses on public April 27 week disclosures tied to Space-Based Interceptor, Golden Dome, and hypersonic systems. Valuation and performance use PortfoliosLab YTD total returns and available current P/E figures, with the caveat that page timestamps differ.

### Findings

| Item | Number | Direct exposure |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| FY26 DoD total | `$961.6B` | Broad defense demand baseline |
| Golden Dome, FY26 President's Budget | `$25B` | Missile defense, space sensors, C2, interceptors |
| Long-range weapons including hypersonic munitions | `$6.5B` | LMT, RTX, NOC, rocket motors/munitions chain |
| Hypersonic weapons | `> $3.9B` | ARRW, LRHW, testing and production |
| MDA FY26 ask including supplement | `$13.2B` | NGI, GMD, sensors, Golden Dome interfaces |
| House FY26 Golden Dome support | `~$13.4B` | `$9.6B` MDA + `$3.8B` Space Force |
| House FY26 hypersonic test / weapons | `$4.5B` | Hypersonic test infrastructure, testing, weapons systems |
| SSC SBI OTA awards | up to `$3.2B`, `20` OTAs, `12` companies | LMT, NOC, Raytheon, GD Mission Systems, SpaceX, Anduril, others |
| Castelion Blackbeard/F/A-18 contract | `$105M` | Non-traditional contractor; evidence of low-cost hypersonic competition |

| RTX | `-3.89%` | SPY `+5.97%` | `-9.86 ppts` | RTX/LMT page 2026-04-29; SPY 2026-05-02 |
| NOC | `+1.18%` | SPY `+5.97%` | `-4.79 ppts` | RTX/NOC page 2026-04-25; SPY 2026-05-02 |

The market has not indiscriminately priced a “super cycle” into every defense prime. LMT is only modestly ahead of SPY in the available YTD data, while RTX, NOC, and GD lag SPY; this contrasts with the strong budget backdrop and shows investors are distinguishing program exposure, execution risk, valuation, and non-traditional competition. The key public April 27 increment was the Space Force SBI prototype OTA pool and Castelion's `$105M` Navy hypersonic award, not a single `$50B` LMT/RTX award. The super-cycle direction is real, but it is a multi-year budget funnel rather than backlog that was fully booked this week.

## Question 3: Is Aerospace Supply-Chain Recovery Matching Delivery Targets?

### Method

I used Airbus Q1 2026 official results, Boeing Q1 delivery announcements, and GE Aerospace's Q1 maintenance network update to calculate Q1 completion, remaining required monthly deliveries, and the disclosed engine/MRO bottlenecks.

### Findings

| Company | Q1 2026 deliveries | 2026 target | Q1 completion | Q2-Q4 remaining | Required Q2-Q4 monthly average |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Airbus | `114` | `870` | `13.1%` | `756` | `84.0` |
| Boeing | `143` | No explicit public annual target | N/A | N/A | N/A |

| Airbus Q1 detail | 2026 Q1 | 2025 Q1 | YoY |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Commercial aircraft deliveries | `114` | `136` | `-16.2%` |
| A220 | `19` | N/A | N/A |
| A320 Family | `81` | N/A | N/A |
| A330 | `3` | N/A | N/A |
| A350 | `11` | N/A | N/A |
| Commercial aircraft backlog | `9,037` | N/A | N/A |

Airbus' full-year target is aggressive relative to its Q1 pace: Q1 averaged only `38` deliveries per month, while the remaining nine months require roughly `84` per month. Management kept the `870` delivery target but also identified Pratt & Whitney as the key A320 Family ramp-up pacer, with A320 Family expected to reach `70-75` per month by end-2027 and stabilize at `75` thereafter. GE Aerospace did not disclose a new April 28 LEAP TAT figure, but it added Iberia as the seventh Premier MRO and expanded Delta TechOps' LEAP-1A/1B capabilities. That proves maintenance capacity is expanding, but it does not prove TAT is already sufficient to support OEM delivery targets.

## Question 4: Can the Robotics and Automation “AI Premium” Last?

### Method

I compared ABB Q1 2026 orders, book-to-bill, segment orders, gross margin, and EBITA data; added Fanuc FY2026 revenue and net margin; and checked whether companies disclosed AI-driven order share.

### Findings

| Company / segment | Orders or revenue | Growth | Margin | AI-related disclosure |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| ABB group orders | `$11.3B` | Comparable `+24%` | Operational EBITA margin `23.5%`, `+320 bps` | Data centers strongest, orders up triple digits in management commentary |
| ABB Electrification orders | `$6.647B` | Comparable `+44%` | Gross margin `40.1%`, `-270 bps`; EBITA margin `24.0%` | Clearest beneficiary of AI data centers and grid investment |
| ABB Automation orders | `$2.464B` | Comparable `+5%` | Gross margin `37.0%`, `-240 bps`; EBITA margin `14.7%`, `+50 bps` | Automation Extended adds advanced analytics / AI, but no order-share disclosure |
| Fanuc FY2026 Q4 revenue | `¥234.5B` | N/A | TTM net margin `19.4%` vs `18.5%` | AI order share not disclosed |
| Fanuc TTM revenue / EPS | `¥857.8B` / `¥178.47` | EPS growth `12.9%` | Net margin improved | Robotics recovery appears cyclical; AI contribution is not quantifiable |

The AI premium can persist, but it should be segmented. ABB's data supports an “AI infrastructure electrification” thesis rather than a “robot orders are exploding” thesis: Electrification orders rose `+44%` comparable, while Automation rose only `+5%`, and both areas faced gross-margin pressure from mix, FX/commodities, and price-cost factors. Fanuc's margin improvement points to better automation demand and cost control, but public materials do not disclose AI project share in new orders. The cleaner investment exposure is electrical equipment, data-center power, industrial software, and high-end controls rather than treating all robot companies as AI beneficiaries.
