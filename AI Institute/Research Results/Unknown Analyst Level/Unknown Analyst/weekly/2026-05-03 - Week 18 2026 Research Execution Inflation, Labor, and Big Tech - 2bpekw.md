## Question 1: Inflation Path

### Method
I used BEA's March PCE data released on 2026-04-30, compared Jan-Mar headline/core MoM, YoY, and the three-month compounded annualized pace, then checked the Treasury curve, SPY/QQQ, and VIX to judge whether markets had exhausted tolerance for "Higher for Longer."

### Findings

| Indicator | Jan 2026 | Feb 2026 | Mar 2026 | Conclusion |
| :--- | ---: | ---: | ---: | :--- |
| PCE price index MoM | +0.3% | +0.4% | +0.7% | Headline reaccelerated as food/energy pressure entered the aggregate index. |
| PCE price index YoY | +2.8% | +2.8% | +3.5% | Year-over-year inflation was well above the 2% target. |
| Core PCE MoM | +0.4% | +0.4% | +0.3% | The monthly pace eased but remained too hot. |
| Core PCE YoY | +3.1% | +3.0% | +3.2% | Core YoY inflation turned back up. |
| Core PCE 3-month annualized |  |  | about +4.49% | Not compatible with the 2% target. |
| 10Y Treasury |  |  | 4.39% on May 1 | Up 4bp on the week; bonds modestly repriced. |
| VIX |  |  | 16.99 on May 1 | Down 9.19% on the week; no risk-appetite break. |

March core PCE at +0.3% MoM and +3.2% YoY is enough to show inflation stickiness has not cleared; the more important statistic is the Jan-Mar annualized core pace of about 4.49%, far above the Fed's 2% target. The bond-market response was a mild front-end-led selloff: 2Y rose from 3.78% to 3.88%, 10Y rose from 4.35% to 4.39%, and the 2s10s spread narrowed from +57bp to +51bp. Equities did not show an intolerance break: SPY rose +0.94% on the week, QQQ rose +1.55%, and VIX fell. The conclusion is that markets are still absorbing "Higher for Longer," but a renewed rate-cut narrative needs either softer April inflation or a materially weaker May 8 labor report.

### Evidence
BEA's March PCE release reported PCE prices +0.7% MoM, core PCE +0.3% MoM, PCE +3.5% YoY, and core PCE +3.2% YoY. Treasury curve data came from U.S. Treasury Daily Treasury Rates; equities and VIX came from Yahoo Finance historical OHLCV.

### Caveats
PCE is monthly and subject to revision; a three-month annualized pace is sensitive to one-month energy shocks. Yahoo Finance volume is not a substitute for exchange depth, quoted spreads, or futures-market liquidity. Market "tolerance" is inferred from price action, not directly observed.

## Question 2: Labor Market

### Method
I first verified the BLS release calendar, then used the latest official Employment Situation release, March 2026, to answer the wage and unemployment questions; industry employment comes from BLS Table B-1. The planned "May 1 April NFP" cannot be executed because BLS lists the April 2026 release for 2026-05-08.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest official month | Value | Answer to the question |
| :--- | :--- | ---: | :--- |
| Total nonfarm payrolls | Mar 2026 | +178k | Payroll growth recovered, but the combined Jan-Feb revision was -7k, so the trend is not overheated. |
| Unemployment rate | Mar 2026 | 4.3% | Still low, though not as extremely tight as the 2022-2023 period. |
| Average hourly earnings | Mar 2026 | +0.2% MoM / +3.5% YoY | Monthly wage growth slowed; YoY wage growth remains elevated. |
| Average workweek | Mar 2026 | 34.2 hours | The workweek fell 0.1 hour, pointing to softer labor demand at the margin. |
| April 2026 Employment Situation | Scheduled release | 2026-05-08 08:30 ET | There was no NFP release on May 1. |

There is evidence of slower wage growth at the margin: average hourly earnings rose +0.2% MoM in March, below the +0.3%/+0.4% rhythm often associated with overheated wage pressure. The +3.5% YoY pace is still above a fully comfortable zone for a 2% inflation objective. The unemployment rate of 4.3% "changed little" and remains low, but long-term unemployment was 1.8 million and up 322k over the year, so labor-market quality is less strong than the headline rate alone suggests. The key data-governance conclusion is that May 1 should not be described as NFP day, and any "post-NFP May 1 volatility" interpretation is data misuse. Actual April payrolls, unemployment, and AHE cannot be filled until the May 8 release.

### Evidence
The BLS March Employment Situation reported payrolls +178k, unemployment 4.3%, average hourly earnings of $37.38, +0.2% MoM and +3.5% YoY, and a 34.2-hour workweek; it also stated that the April Employment Situation is scheduled for 2026-05-08. BLS Table B-1 provides industry employment changes.

### Caveats
March data cannot substitute for April data; BLS initial estimates may be revised. There is no authoritative free and stable source for industry-level consensus, so the visualization should be changed from "actual vs consensus" to "latest official industry contribution."

## Question 3: Earnings Quality

### Method
For AMZN and AAPL, I used official earnings materials and separated revenue, EPS, operating cash flow, free cash flow, Capex/PPE purchases, and identifiable AI revenue or product evidence; post-earnings share reaction uses the first full trading day close-to-close return. EPS and revenue surprises use third-party consensus estimates only for the scatter plot, not as company-reported fundamental evidence.

### Findings

| Company | Release date | Revenue/EPS | Cash flow and Capex | AI monetization evidence | First-day post-earnings price reaction |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | ---: |
| AMZN | 2026-04-29 after close | Net sales $181.5B (+17% YoY); EPS $2.78 | TTM operating cash flow $148.5B (+30%); TTM FCF $1.2B (-95%); TTM net PPE purchases $147.299B (+67%) | AWS sales $37.6B (+28%); AWS operating income $14.2B; Trainium/Bedrock disclosures show AI demand entering AWS revenue, but a $16.8B pre-tax gain on Anthropic investments lifted net income. | AMZN +0.77% on Apr 30 vs Apr 29 |
| AAPL | 2026-04-30 after close | Net sales $111.184B (+16.6% YoY); EPS $2.01 (+21.8% YoY) | Six-month operating cash flow $82.627B (+53.3%); PPE payments $4.344B (-27.7%) | Services $30.976B (+16.3%) and iPhone $56.994B (+21.7%); no separate AI revenue disclosure, while R&D +33.6% is more an investment signal. | AAPL +3.24% on May 1 vs Apr 30 |

AMZN is the strongest sample for "AI investment turning into revenue": AWS grew +28%, AWS operating income reached $14.2B, and Bedrock/Trainium customer disclosures provide real demand signals. The quality issue is that improvement is not one-directional: TTM FCF fell from $25.9B to $1.2B, and the company explicitly linked the decline to higher AI-related PPE investment; net income also includes a $16.8B pre-tax gain on Anthropic investments that should not be extrapolated. AAPL's quality is cleaner: revenue, EPS, Services revenue, and operating cash flow were all strong, while PPE spending fell. But AAPL did not disclose a quantified AI revenue line, so the strong report should not be attributed directly to AI monetization. Market reaction supports the distinction: AAPL's first-day gain of +3.24% was much stronger than AMZN's +0.77%.

### Evidence
AMZN's official Q1 2026 release disclosed net sales, AWS results, operating income, TTM OCF/FCF, net PPE purchases, and Q2 guidance. AAPL's FY2026 Q2 Consolidated Financial Statements disclosed three-month revenue, EPS, category revenue, and six-month cash flow. Price reactions came from Yahoo Finance historical OHLCV.

### Caveats
AMZN does not disclose a standalone AI revenue line, so AWS growth is not identical to AI revenue. AAPL's PDF provides a six-month cash-flow statement, not a quarterly operating-cash-flow split. EPS and revenue consensus estimates are third-party numbers, not SEC or company disclosures.

## Question 4: Liquidity Gap

### Method
I first confirmed that May 1 had no NFP release, then checked major U.S. ETF and stock volume versus 20-day averages, intraday ranges, and VIX changes; global Labor Day closures were treated as background rather than mechanically as proof of a U.S. liquidity squeeze.

### Findings

| Indicator | May 1 value | Benchmark | Conclusion |
| :--- | ---: | ---: | :--- |
| SPY volume | 42.889M | 0.78x 20-day average | Lower than normal, but not paired with unusual range expansion. |
| SPY intraday range | 0.61% | 1.31% on Apr 30 | May 1 volatility was lower than the Apr 30 PCE/GDP/FOMC aftershock. |
| QQQ volume | 39.055M | 0.93x 20-day average | Close to normal. |
| VIX | 16.99 | +0.59% on the day, -9.19% on the week | No panic-style volatility amplification. |
| AAPL volume | 79.845M | 1.84x 20-day average | Stock-specific earnings volume, not systemic liquidity stress. |

The core fact is that no NFP was released on May 1, so the proposed "global closures amplified the NFP shock" question cannot be populated. U.S. trading volume was uneven: SPY was below its 20-day average, while AAPL and some consumer/communication ETFs traded heavily because of earnings and constituent effects. Price action does not show a liquidity squeeze: SPY's intraday range was 0.61%, QQQ's was 1.07%, and VIX rose only slightly on the day while falling over the week. The better interpretation is that global Labor Day reduced some cross-border participation, but U.S. trading absorbed earnings and macro repricing without an observable break.

### Evidence
BLS schedule confirms that the April Employment Situation release date is 2026-05-08. China, Hong Kong, and many other markets were closed for Labor Day, while U.S. NYSE/Nasdaq were open. U.S. volume and ranges came from Yahoo Finance historical OHLCV.

### Caveats
ETF volume does not measure full market depth; the analysis lacks NYSE TAQ, futures order-book depth, bid-ask spreads, and cross-border fund-flow data. Global holiday effects may appear in intraday depth rather than daily volume.
