## Question 1: Was The Risk-Asset Rally Driven By Real Demand Or By AI/Tech Valuation And Positioning Expansion?

### Method

I used AP daily index closes and weekly returns from 2026-05-04 to 2026-05-08, comparing Nasdaq, S&P 500, Dow, and Russell 2000 relative performance. I anchored events around Uber/DoorDash/Instacart on 2026-05-06, CoreWeave on 2026-05-07, and BLS payrolls on 2026-05-08.

### Findings

| Metric | 2026-05-08 close | Weekly point change | Weekly return | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| S&P 500 | 7,398.93 | +168.81 | +2.3% | Broad market at a new high |
| Nasdaq Composite | 26,247.08 | +1,132.63 | +4.5% | Strongest technology/AI risk appetite |
| Dow | 49,609.16 | +109.89 | +0.2% | Traditional blue chips lagged sharply |
| Russell 2000 | 2,861.21 | +48.39 | +1.7% | Small caps participated but did not lead |
| Nasdaq - S&P 500 | N/A | N/A | +2.2 pp | Weekly excess return from tech concentration |
| Nasdaq - Dow | N/A | N/A | +4.3 pp | Growth/tech expansion versus value/blue chips |

Interpretation: The labor report did provide a macro floor: April payrolls rose +115,000 and unemployment held at 4.3%, above expectations. But if the rally were mainly about broad real-demand improvement, Dow, Russell 2000, and consumer/industrial exposures should have moved more in sync; instead, Nasdaq’s +4.5% clearly led. Weekly catalysts clustered around oil relief, platform earnings, chip/AI orders, and Friday’s jobs beat. The conclusion is that macro data reduced left-tail risk, while marginal pricing came from AI/technology valuation and positioning expansion.

## Question 2: Is Consumer Resilience Broad-Based Or Concentrated In Gasoline, Grocery, Tax Refunds, Mother’s Day Pull-Forward, And Inflation?

### Method

I combined Redbook weekly same-store sales, BofA card spending, EIA fuel prices, and bank card-sales disclosures. The spread between total spending and ex-gas spending is used to estimate the energy-price contribution, while Redbook and platform order data help separate transaction volume from nominal price effects.

### Findings

| Metric | Latest value | Comparison | Change | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Redbook YoY | +7.8% | Prior +7.7% | +0.1 pp | High-frequency nominal retail still strong |
| BofA total card spending/household YoY | +4.3% | February +3.2% | +1.1 pp | March spending growth accelerated |
| BofA ex-gas card spending/household YoY | +3.6% | February +3.6% | 0.0 pp | No further acceleration after removing gasoline |
| BofA gas spending MoM | +16.5% | Prior same-basis value not disclosed | N/A | Gasoline lifted nominal readings |
| EIA regular gasoline | $4.452/gal | Prior week $4.123/gal | +$0.329 | Disposable-income squeeze increased |
| EIA on-highway diesel | $5.640/gal | Prior week $5.351/gal | +$0.289 | Delivery and freight costs increased |
| JPMorgan debit/credit card sales volume | $487.6 billion | Q1 2025 $448.7 billion | +9% YoY | Bank-card transaction value still firm |

Interpretation: Consumer spending is resilient, but not broadly and unconditionally improving. Redbook +7.8% and JPMorgan card sales volume +9% YoY show nominal transaction value has not rolled over; BofA ex-gas +3.6% also remains positive. The issue is that incremental acceleration is heavily influenced by gasoline, tax refunds, and higher-income households, while lower-income discretionary spending is weaker. For retail equities, the key is to verify unit volume, traffic, basket size, and promotion depth rather than rely on nominal sales.

## Question 3: Can Uber, DoorDash, And Instacart Order/User Growth Be Verified By Third-Party App Usage, Transaction Data, And Offline Traffic?

### Method

I calculated frequency, AOV, and growth sources from Q1 2026 operating disclosures. I used Similarweb’s 2026-05-03 Android Food & Drink usage rankings as public third-party directional validation and cross-checked the demand environment with BofA/JPMorgan consumer data.

### Findings

| Company | Volume metric | YoY | Dollar metric | YoY | Calculated AOV/frequency | Third-party validation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| UBER | Trips 3.643 billion | +20% | Gross Bookings $53.720 billion | +25% | Gross Bookings/Trip $14.75; MAPCs 199 million, +17% | Uber Eats Android Food & Drink usage rank #3 |
| DASH | Total Orders 933 million | +27% | Marketplace GOV $31.604 billion | +37% | GOV/Order $33.87; ex-Deliveroo Orders +16%, GOV +24% | DoorDash Android Food & Drink usage rank #1 |
| CART | Orders 91.2 million | +10% | GTV $10.288 billion | +13% | GTV/Order $112.81 | Instacart Android Food & Drink usage rank #6 |

Interpretation: All three platforms show both order growth and AOV/transaction-value growth. Uber’s growth is the most balanced across users and frequency: MAPCs +17%, Trips +20%, and monthly trips per MAPC up roughly 3%. DoorDash’s headline +27% orders and +37% GOV are very strong, but M&A matters: excluding Deliveroo, orders rose +16% and GOV +24%. Instacart’s growth is more moderate, with Orders +10% and AOV about +2.9%, suggesting more contribution from basket value, ads, and retail platformization than from explosive order growth.

### Evidence

## Question 5: Have AI Data Centers And Chip Supply Chains Become The Most Important Alternative-Data Theme? Which Indicators Can Verify Capex Delivery Early?

### Method

I tracked this week’s large cloud/TPU contract reports, CoreWeave backlog and capex, Broadcom AI semiconductor revenue, and grid-interconnection constraint reporting. I decomposed “order value” into verifiable physical chains: power, transformers, land/permits, server delivery, GPU/TPU supply, cooling, construction progress, and financing cost.

### Findings

| Metric | Latest value | Comparison/change | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Anthropic-Google Cloud/TPU commitment | $200 billion / 5 years | Reported by Reuters/The Information; more than 40% of Google backlog | Cloud backlog quality is now central |
| CoreWeave Q1 revenue | $2.078 billion | +112% YoY | AI cloud demand is converting into revenue |
| CoreWeave revenue backlog | $99.4 billion | +284% YoY | Multi-year orders are very strong |
| CoreWeave Q1 capex | $6.786 billion | Q1 2025 $1.858 billion | Physical expansion accelerated |
| CoreWeave net loss | $(740) million | Q1 2025 $(315) million | Financing/deployment costs are front-loaded |
| CoreWeave interest expense | $(536) million | Q1 2025 $(264) million | Capital structure is a constraint |
| Broadcom Q1 FY26 AI revenue | $8.4 billion | +106% YoY | TPU/custom accelerator chain benefits |
| Data-center power constraint | Denmark requests 60 GW, datacenters 14 GW | Peak load about 7 GW | Grid interconnection is a global bottleneck case study |

Interpretation: AI infrastructure has moved from “software demand” to “physical delivery.” CoreWeave’s $99.4 billion backlog and $6.786 billion capex are strong demand evidence, but net losses, interest expense, and pre-revenue deployment costs show that capex delivery is not the same as free-cash-flow delivery. If Anthropic’s $200 billion commitment is accurate, it turns Google TPU, Broadcom, data centers, and power supply into one trackable chain. The most leading indicators for the next stage are not cloud revenue, but power interconnection queues, transformer lead times, building permits, server shipments, memory pricing, and satellite imagery of data-center construction.

### Evidence

## Question 6: Which Sectors Or Stocks Have Price Reactions That Diverge From Alternative-Data Direction?

I built an “alternative-data surprise versus market reaction” table. Order, user, transaction-value, backlog, and cost-pressure indicators are converted into a surprise score: +2 strong positive, +1 positive, 0 neutral, -1 negative, -2 strong negative. I then compare that score with reported stock or index reaction.

### Findings

| Ticker/theme | Alternative-data surprise | Market reaction | Divergence | Next-week focus |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| CART | +1 | About -8% on 2026-05-06 | Positive data, negative price | Check AOV, ad growth, Q2 guide, competition |
| CRWV | +2 demand, -1 financing | About -10% after-hours | Strong backlog but capital-cost pressure | Track interest expense, capex, deployment timing |

| UBER | +2 | About +10% | Data and price aligned | Verify MAPCs, driver supply, airport/city traffic |

| Retail nominal spending | +1 nominal, 0 ex-gas | XRT weekly value not obtained | Data split incomplete | Wait for Placer/BofA/retail sales |
| Transportation/logistics | -1 cost pressure | IYT weekly value not obtained | Market data needed | Diesel, jet fuel, freight rates, port congestion |

DASH and UBER have price action aligned with operating data, though DASH needs to be rechecked after stripping out Deliveroo.
