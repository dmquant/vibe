# Weekly Research Report: AI Earnings Resilience Offsets Macro Pressure

**Coverage window: 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01. Bottom line: this was not a simple “tech rally” week. Markets repriced around strong AI/cloud revenue, stagflation-sensitive macro data, sharp yen moves, and weaker energy prices.**

## Priority Conclusions

1. **AI spending is showing up in cloud revenue, but capital intensity keeps rising.** MSFT Azure grew +40%, GOOGL Google Cloud grew +63%, and AMZN AWS grew +28%; at the same time, MSFT quarterly CapEx was about $31.9B, GOOGL CapEx was $35.67B, and AMZN management pointed to roughly $150B of 2026 CapEx, keeping free-cash-flow payback front and center.
2. **Stagflation concerns rose, but they are not yet the base case.** BEA reported Q1 2026 real GDP at -0.3% annualized and March core PCE at 2.6% YoY / 0.0% MoM; negative growth plus inflation above the 2% target makes the June Fed debate more dependent on jobs and services inflation.
3. **The Bank of Japan’s unchanged rate did not fully anchor yen volatility.** The BoJ kept the uncollateralized overnight call rate target around 0.75%, while USD/JPY fell -3.38% on the week to 144.97; the move looks more like a mix of dollar weakness and carry-trade reduction than policy alone.
4. **Market breadth improved, but leadership was still uneven.** LPL reported S&P 500 +2.95% for the week, with Information Technology +5.69% and Communication Services +5.23% leading, while Energy fell -3.69%; that is growth-led broadening, not a value/energy rotation.
5. **The May 1 holiday mainly affected cross-market liquidity, not U.S. trading.** Mainland China, Hong Kong, and many European markets were closed for Labour Day, while U.S. markets traded normally; VIX ended at 16.10, down -11.20% on the week, so volatility did not spike despite the holiday gap.

## Question 1: AI Return on Investment

### Method

I used the latest quarterly earnings released between 2026-04-28 and 2026-04-30 for MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN, and AAPL. The comparison uses total revenue, cloud/AI-related growth, EPS, and CapEx or a CapEx proxy. Company releases are the primary source; where companies do not disclose AI CapEx directly, public earnings coverage and management guidance are used as supporting proxies.

### Findings

| Company | Reporting period | Revenue | EPS | Cloud / AI-related growth | CapEx / AI investment proxy | Assessment |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| MSFT | FY2026 Q3 | $82.9B, +18% | $3.46 | Azure +40%; AI business ARR $37B, +123% | Quarterly CapEx about $31.9B | ✅ Revenue conversion is clear, but capital intensity is high |
| GOOGL | 2026 Q1 | $109.9B, +22% | $2.81 | Google Cloud $20.0B, +63% | CapEx $35.67B | ✅ Cloud revenue accelerated alongside larger AI infrastructure spend |
| AMZN | 2026 Q1 | $181.5B, +17% | $1.59 | AWS $37.6B, +28% | 2026 CapEx guide about $150B; TTM PP&E cash spend +59.3% | ⚠️ AWS is strong, but CapEx pressure on FCF is larger |
| AAPL | FY2026 Q2 | $111.2B, +17% | $2.01 | Services $31.0B, +16% | No comparable AI infrastructure CapEx disclosed | ❓ AI monetization evidence is limited; services and device resilience matter more |

Interpretation: MSFT, GOOGL, and AMZN all reported cloud growth materially above total company revenue growth, which means AI demand is already appearing in customer workloads and cloud consumption. The strongest evidence of AI monetization comes from MSFT’s AI ARR and Azure growth, plus GOOGL’s +63% Google Cloud revenue growth. AMZN’s AWS growth remains solid, but investors are more sensitive to CapEx and free-cash-flow pressure. AAPL cannot be evaluated on the same cloud-AI basis; its near-term evidence is stronger in services and the device cycle than in disclosed AI infrastructure returns.

## Question 2: Stagflation Risk

### Method

I used BEA’s 2026-04-30 Q1 GDP advance estimate and 2026-05-01 March personal income and outlays release, then compared real GDP, PCE inflation, core PCE, the 10-year Treasury yield, and VIX over the same week.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest value | Comparison / change | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| U.S. Q1 2026 real GDP | -0.3% annualized | Q4 2025 was +2.4% | Growth slowed sharply |
| Q1 PCE price index | +3.6% | Q4 2025 was +2.4% | Nominal inflation pressure increased |
| March core PCE | +2.6% YoY; +0.0% MoM | Above the 2% target, but monthly pressure stabilized | Sticky, but not worsening at the margin |
| 10-year Treasury yield | 4.31% | Down -6 bps on the week | Bonds emphasized growth risk |
| VIX | 16.10 | Down -11.20% on the week | Equities did not price panic |

Interpretation: The combination of negative Q1 GDP and core PCE still above target raised the market’s stagflation discussion. However, core PCE was flat on the month, limiting the intensity of the stagflation trade. The decline in the 10-year yield suggests bond markets focused more on slower growth than runaway inflation. If payrolls and services inflation soften next, rate-cut expectations can reassert themselves; if jobs stay firm while services prices rebound, stagflation risk will intensify.

## Question 3: Yen Defense

### Method

I checked the BoJ’s 2026-04-28 policy statement, the weekly move in USD/JPY, DXY, and the U.S. 10-year yield. The test is whether policy changed the rate-differential backdrop, and whether yen strength was a stand-alone policy effect or part of a broader dollar and carry-trade move.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest value / event | Weekly change | Assessment |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| BoJ policy rate | 0.75%, unchanged | No change | ⚠️ Policy alone was not enough to strongly reverse rate differentials |
| USD/JPY | 144.97 | -3.38% | ✅ Yen rebounded sharply in the short term |
| DXY | 99.76 | -0.23% | ⚠️ Dollar weakness contributed to the yen rebound |
| U.S. 10-year yield | 4.31% | -6 bps | ⚠️ Lower U.S. yields reduced carry appeal |

Interpretation: The BoJ held the policy rate at 0.75%, so it did not deliver a policy shock large enough to change the global carry backdrop by itself. The -3.38% weekly fall in USD/JPY likely reflected dollar softness, lower U.S. yields, and carry-trade reduction together. In the short run, the yen defense worked; over the medium term, USD/JPY remains vulnerable to renewed upside if U.S.-Japan rate differentials stay wide and BoJ tightening is gradual. The key risk to monitor is whether rapid yen strength forces broader cross-asset deleveraging.

## Question 4: Market Breadth

### Method

I used LPL’s weekly sector performance data to compare the S&P 500 with the 11 sectors, focusing on technology/communication services versus energy and other value-oriented sectors. The breadth test looks at how many sectors led or matched the index and whether laggards were concentrated.

## Question 5: Liquidity Window

### Method

I checked the 2026-05-01 holiday calendars for major exchanges and compared same-week VIX, major indices, FX, and commodities. Because public, cross-market real-time order-book depth is not available here, holiday coverage, price movement, and VIX are used as liquidity proxies.

### Findings

| Market / asset | 2026-05-01 status or level | Weekly change | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Mainland China | Labour Day holiday closure | N/A | Asian price discovery delayed |
| Hong Kong | Labour Day holiday closure | N/A | Hong Kong and China ADR linkage delayed |
| Many European markets | Labour Day holiday closure | N/A | European liquidity lower |
| U.S. equities | Open | S&P 500 +2.95%; Nasdaq Composite +3.42% | U.S. risk appetite held up |
| VIX | 16.10 | -11.20% | Holidays did not lift volatility |
| Brent | $61.42 | -7.53% | Commodity volatility was driven more by fundamentals/risk appetite |

Interpretation: The main May 1 effect was not a U.S. market closure, but missing Asian and European participation that delayed cross-market hedging and price discovery. VIX fell to 16.10 for the week, so U.S. risk assets did not come under visible pressure from the holiday window. The more important watchpoint is the catch-up move when Asia and Europe reopen after digesting U.S. earnings, the dollar, and oil. If a geopolitical shock occurs during a holiday closure, thinner liquidity can amplify opening gaps.
