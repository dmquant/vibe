## Priority Conclusions

1. The week’s risk-asset message was not “oil shock crushes growth,” but “labor is still stable, AI/semiconductor earnings keep validating the rally, and oil fell by week-end.” The S&P 500 gained 2.3% on the week to 7,398, while the Nasdaq Composite gained 4.5% to 26,247; both set new closing highs on 2026-05-08.[1]
2. The rates message was “rate cuts keep moving further away.” April nonfarm payrolls rose 115,000, the unemployment rate held at 4.3%, and average hourly earnings rose 0.2% month over month and 3.6% year over year; however, the 61.8% labor-force participation rate and 4.9 million people working part time for economic reasons show that growth resilience and labor-quality softening coexist.[2]
3. The commodity message shifted from a 2026-05-04 oil-risk-premium spike to a late-week retreat: Brent jumped 5.8% to $114.44 on 2026-05-04; WTI ended 2026-05-08 at $94.92, down almost 7% for the week, but still up almost 42% since the Middle East war began.[1][3]
4. Bond allocation must handle three things at once: labor data that support higher front-end rates, an oil shock that raises inflation risk, and the U.S. Treasury’s 2026-05-06 decision to keep coupon auction sizes unchanged while announcing a $125 billion refunding package.[4]
5. Globally, the RBA raised rates 25 bp to 4.35% on 2026-05-05, showing that some central banks still need to lean against inflation under an energy shock. That aligns directionally with U.S. “no cut for now” pricing, but has different implications for AUD, global duration, and commodity-sensitive assets.[5]

## 2. Core Analytical Questions

1. Was the improvement in risk appetite driven by earnings quality, or by lower oil prices and momentum chasing?
2. Are labor-market data strong enough for the Fed to remain on hold through 2026? If so, should duration exposure be reduced, held steady, or added on yield spikes?
3. Does the near-7% weekly drop in oil reduce inflation tail risk, or does Middle East risk premium still need to be hedged through energy, gold, or volatility?
4. Is the AI/semiconductor rally broad enough? Should technology overweight remain concentrated in winners, or rotate toward equal weight, value, industrials, and financials?
5. With equities at new highs, likely tight credit spreads, and consumer sentiment at only 48.2, where should next week’s main risk budget sit in a risk-parity/all-weather portfolio?
6. Do the RBA hike, U.S. Treasury supply, and dollar path weaken the diversification value of non-U.S. assets and commodities?
