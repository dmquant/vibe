# China Macro Weekly Research Draft: Policy Support Meets Earnings Validation

**Coverage window: 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01. Bottom line: this was not a pure policy rally. The week repriced four facts at once: manufacturing stayed in expansion, non-manufacturing softened, real-estate support became more explicit, and earnings strength concentrated in technology manufacturing and resource sectors. May is more likely to bring rotation within confirmed earnings leaders than a wholesale style switch.**

## 2. Question 1: What Is New in the Policy Tone?

### Method

I read the April 28 Politburo readout from Xinhua/People’s Daily and compared it with the December 2025 Central Economic Work Conference and the December 8, 2025 Politburo readout. The comparison focuses on real estate, macro policy, domestic demand, new productive forces, and risk prevention, using both keyword frequency and the position of each phrase in the policy text.

### Findings

| Policy dimension | Dec 8 2025 Politburo | Dec 10/11 2025 CEWC | Apr 28 2026 Politburo | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Mentions of “new productive forces” | 1 | 2 | 0 | The April meeting did not repeat the phrase; it shifted toward “AI+”, manufacturing share, and the unified national market, suggesting a move from concept-setting to industrial execution. |
| Mentions of “real estate” | 0 | 2 | 1 | December specified “control new supply, destock, improve supply”; April condensed the message to “stabilize the market” and “urban renewal,” narrowing the near-term implementation focus. |
| Mentions of “risk” | 2 | 6 | 2 | Risk prevention remains central, but April placed real estate, local government debt, small and mid-sized financial institutions, and capital-market confidence in one policy block. |
| Macro policy language | More proactive and effective | More proactive fiscal policy and moderately loose monetary policy | Use macro policies fully and well; maintain ample liquidity | Additional policy expectations remain, but execution efficiency and expectation management matter more in the near term. |

The new point in April is that real-estate policy moved from an inventory-destocking list into an implementation frame built around market stabilization and urban renewal. The December CEWC called for city-specific control of new supply, destocking, better supply, and purchases of existing commercial housing mainly for affordable housing. April compressed that into “working hard to stabilize the real estate market” and “solidly advancing urban renewal,” which points to three tool lines: existing-home purchases, urban renewal investment such as village renovation and underground networks, and coordinated handling of local debt and developer cash-flow risks. The same readout also called for stabilizing and strengthening capital-market confidence, putting real-estate risk resolution inside a broader balance-sheet stabilization framework.

### Caveats

The April Politburo readout did not disclose quotas, responsible agencies, or a timetable, so policy-tool mapping is an inference from official wording. Real-estate destocking still depends on local fiscal capacity, policy-bank funding, affordable-housing demand, and household expectations. Keyword counts are manual counts from public readouts and should not be treated as a formal policy-weight model.

## 3. Question 2: What Signal Did PMI Send?

### Method

I used the NBS April 30 release on China’s April 2026 PMI, comparing manufacturing PMI, non-manufacturing business activity, and the major sub-indices against March, then reviewing the past 12 months. Note: the prior planning note used 50.2 for manufacturing PMI; the official NBS value is 50.3, and this report uses the official value.

### Findings

| Indicator | 2026-03 | 2026-04 | M/m change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Manufacturing PMI | 50.4 | 50.3 | -0.1 |
| Production | 51.4 | 51.5 | +0.1 |
| New orders | 51.6 | 50.6 | -1.0 |
| New export orders | 49.1 | 50.3 | +1.2 |
| Imports | 49.8 | 50.1 | +0.3 |
| Raw-material purchase prices | 63.9 | 63.7 | -0.2 |
| Output prices | 55.4 | 55.1 | -0.3 |
| Employment | 48.6 | 48.8 | +0.2 |
| Non-manufacturing business activity | 50.1 | 49.4 | -0.7 |
| Non-manufacturing new orders | 45.0 | 44.3 | -0.7 |
| Composite PMI output index | 50.5 | 50.1 | -0.4 |

Manufacturing did not stall; it moved from a March rebound high to mild expansion. Production rose to 51.5 and new export orders returned to 50.3, showing resilience in production and external demand. The weaker point is domestic demand: new orders fell from 51.6 to 50.6. The larger concern is non-manufacturing, where business activity fell below the 50 threshold to 49.4, with construction at 48.0 and services at 49.6. Price sub-indices stayed high, with purchase prices at 63.7 and output prices at 55.1, which helps upstream and parts of midstream manufacturing, but profit transmission will be uneven if final demand remains soft.

### Caveats

PMI is a month-on-month diffusion index, not a year-on-year output or profit measure. April includes post-Qingming reopening, pre-Labor-Day stocking, and seasonal-adjustment effects, so one month of weaker new orders should not be mechanically extrapolated. Non-manufacturing details are partly directional or range-based, limiting precise decomposition within services.

## 4. Question 3: What Was the Quality of Q1 Earnings?

### Method

I used the Wind-based A-share 2026Q1 earnings panorama reposted by Sina Finance, Eastmoney’s earnings list, and public strategy-note excerpts from China Merchants Strategy and Soochow Strategy. The comparison covers all A-shares, ex-financials, listing boards, broad indices, sector profit growth, gross margin, and ROE.

### Findings

| Earnings indicator | 2026Q1 value |
|---|---:|
| Companies with Q1 reports disclosed | 5503 |
| All A-share revenue | RMB17.68 trillion |
| All A-share attributable net profit | RMB1.59 trillion |
| All A-share revenue growth | +4.66% y/y |
| All A-share attributable net profit growth | +6.59% y/y |
| All A-share ex-financial attributable net profit growth | +10.69% y/y |
| All A-share gross margin | 18.55%, +0.80ppt vs 2025 annual report |
| All A-share ROE(TTM) | 7.59%, -0.15ppt vs 2025 annual report |
| All A-share ex-financial ROE(TTM) | 6.46%, +0.02ppt vs 2025 annual report |
| CSI 300 attributable net profit growth | +4.93% y/y |
| CSI 500 attributable net profit growth | +18.80% y/y |
| CSI 1000 attributable net profit growth | +5.24% y/y |
| SSE 50 attributable net profit growth | +3.07% y/y |

The earnings story is all-A recovery, stronger ex-financials, and still uneven ROE. All A-share profit grew 6.59% y/y, while ex-financials grew 10.69%, indicating that bank net-interest-margin pressure is still weighing on aggregate elasticity. By sector, software services, semiconductors, and non-ferrous metals led attributable net profit growth at +259.57%, +148.00%, and +85.79%, respectively, and were the main sources of alpha. Steel, durable consumer goods, autos and components, and food & beverage remained under pressure. Mid-to-downstream manufacturing improvement was concentrated in technology manufacturing, lithium battery and storage, high-end equipment, and export chains rather than a broad recovery in traditional consumption.

Food & beverage and banks lagged, suggesting the market has not yet priced domestic-demand repair as a broad traditional-consumption or financial-sector rally. If May brings rotation, it should look more like a move from crowded tech into resources, real-estate chain, equipment manufacturing, and low-positioned cyclicals, not a one-step switch from tech into consumption.
