# HK/US Weekly Research: First Week of May 2026

**Date anchor:** shell `date +%Y-%m-%d` returned `2026-05-10`. In this report, “this week” means `2026-05-04` to `2026-05-08`, and “previous trading day” is interpreted against that date.  
**Bottom line:** This was not a simple “energy up, risk assets down” week. It was an oil shock that faded quickly, while technology stocks repriced higher. Brent futures closed at `114.44` per barrel on `2026-05-04`, with an intraday high of `115.30`, but fell to `101.29` by `2026-05-08`, down `-6.36%` from `2026-05-01`; over the same period the Nasdaq Composite rose `+4.51%`, QQQ rose `+5.50%`, and XLK rose `+8.44%`. In Hong Kong, the Hang Seng Index gained `+2.39%`, but the S&P Global Hong Kong SAR PMI fell from `49.3` to `48.6`, showing that energy and shipping costs are already flowing through to orders and pricing. HKEX’s TECH Channel was not newly created in 2026; it launched on `2025-05-06`. The fresh 2026 story is that the IPO pipeline and technology derivatives activity have already recovered materially.

## Q1: How deep is the transmission from the energy shock into Hong Kong logistics and manufacturing PMI?

**Method.** I used Brent/WTI futures prices from `2026-05-04` to `2026-05-08`, the S&P Global Hong Kong SAR PMI final April reading, and shipping transit data after Project Freedom. Benchmarks were the March PMI, the pre-week oil close on May 1, and reported normal/pre-war transit context.

| Indicator | Latest/week value | Comparison basis | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| S&P Global Hong Kong SAR PMI | `48.6` | March `49.3` | Second month of contraction and a ten-month low |
| PMI input costs | `14.5-year` high | Last comparable high: `2011-10` | Oil, fuel, and raw-material costs are feeding into prices |
| Brent futures | `101.29`, weekly change `-6.36%` | `2026-05-01` `108.17` | Reversed after Monday’s spike, but still high in absolute terms |
| Brent intrawweek high | `115.30` | `2026-05-04` | Short-term shock to logistics and inventory planning |
| WTI futures | `95.42`, weekly change `-6.40%` | `2026-05-01` `101.94` | Energy risk premium eased |
| Hormuz transit | `10` ships on `2026-05-04` | Still below pre-war normal | Shipping risk has not normalized |

**Findings.** The negative transmission is already visible: the PMI fell to `48.6`, output and new orders declined for a second month, and companies explicitly linked price pressure to the Middle East war, oil, and fuels. For Hong Kong logistics and manufacturing-linked firms, the pressure is not just one oil-price print; it is the combination of high fuel costs, route uncertainty, and defensive raw-material purchasing. Markets priced partial relief by Friday, but the PMI survey period was `2026-04-09` to `2026-04-27`, meaning the cost shock was already embedded before this week. Near term, the margin hit looks deeper than the volume hit unless Brent keeps falling from around `101` and Hormuz traffic recovers.

**Evidence.** S&P Global Hong Kong SAR PMI release; S&P Global global PMI supply-chain commentary; Investing.com Brent/WTI historical prices; S&P Global Commodities at Sea report on `10` transits; CENTCOM Project Freedom release.  
**Caveats.** The PMI release does not publish a standalone Hong Kong logistics sub-index. Full daily Southbound net-flow data were not available in a complete public source, so fund-flow analysis uses HKEX Q1 ADT and turnover data as proxies.

## Q2: Can TECH Channel turn a quiet first half into a second-half IPO engine?

**Method.** I checked the original HKEX/SFC TECH Channel announcement and HKEX’s Q1 2026 results for IPO, application-pipeline, and technology derivatives indicators. Important correction: TECH Channel launched on `2025-05-06`; it was not newly introduced on `2026-05-06`.

| Indicator | Value | Basis | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---|---|
| TECH Channel launch date | `2025-05-06` | HKEX/SFC joint announcement | Existing framework, not a new policy this week |
| Eligible companies | Chapter `18C` Specialist Technology and Chapter `18A` Biotech | HKEX Guide | Covers hard tech and biotech |
| Key facilitation | Confidential filing, early/tailored guidance, presumed satisfaction of WVR innovation requirements | HKEX/SFC | Improves listing certainty and reduces sensitive disclosure risk |
| Q1 2026 IPO count | `40` | HKEX Q1 2026 | Strongest Q1 since 2021 |
| Q1 2026 IPO proceeds | `HK$110.4bn` | HKEX Q1 2026 | Nearly `6x` Q1 2025 |
| Active IPO applications | `431` | `2026-03-31` | Up `25%` from `2025-12-31` |
| Hang Seng TECH derivatives ADV | `232,760` contracts | Four products in Q1 2026 | Up `28%` YoY, showing renewed demand for tech exposure |

**Findings.** TECH Channel is not a single-handed cure for a weak first half, because Q1 2026 was already strong: `40` IPOs, `HK$110.4bn` raised, and `431` active applications. Its real value is institutionalizing early communication for 18A/18C and WVR candidates, reducing the cost of premature disclosure before commercialization. If volatility eases in the second half, the channel can help convert the application backlog into listings. Strategically, the better watchlist is HKEX, brokers, potential 18C issuers, and Hang Seng TECH derivatives activity, rather than treating TECH Channel as a brand-new policy shock.

**Evidence.** HKEX/SFC TECH Channel announcement dated `2025-05-06`; HKEX Q1 2026 results.  
**Caveats.** Public sources do not disclose the number of confidential filings through TECH Channel. The `431` active applications cannot be split publicly into 18A, 18C, or TECH Channel sources.

## Q3: How did payrolls change the Fed’s June rate path?

**Method.** I used the BLS April Employment Situation released on `2026-05-08`, compared it with market expectations of about `+65k`, reviewed February/March revisions, wages, and participation, and used the Federal Reserve’s official calendar for the June FOMC window.

| Indicator | April actual | Forecast/previous | Rate implication |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Nonfarm payrolls | `+115k` | Consensus about `+65k` | Stronger than expected; weakens the June-cut case |
| Unemployment rate | `4.3%` | Expected `4.3%` | No employment-side urgency |
| Labor-force participation | `61.8%` | March `61.9%` | Employment resilience has a quality discount |
| Part-time for economic reasons | `4.9mn` | Monthly increase `+445k` | Labor-quality risk is rising |
| Average hourly earnings | `+0.2% MoM/+3.6% YoY` | Softer than March | Wages not breaking out, but oil still clouds inflation |
| February/March revisions | `-156k` / `+185k` | Combined revision `-16k` | Monthly signal remains volatile |
| Next FOMC | `2026-06-16` to `2026-06-17` | Official calendar | Base case: hold |

**Findings.** Payrolls moved the June debate away from “should the Fed preemptively cut?” and back toward “can the Fed wait for more inflation evidence?” The hawkish minority probably does not control the full narrative, but high oil prices and PMI input-cost pressure give it enough leverage to block a quick easing pivot. Labor quality is not flawless: participation fell and involuntary part-time work rose, so consumer resilience should not be overstated. For allocation, this argues against aggressive duration trades and keeps the burden of proof on tech earnings and AI order books.

**Evidence.** BLS Employment Situation April 2026; Federal Reserve FOMC calendar; Axios/Kiplinger coverage of consensus and sector details.  
**Caveats.** CME FedWatch’s full historical probability table is dynamic/subscription-dependent. This report uses only publicly verifiable meeting dates and news snapshots.

## Q4: Why did Amazon, AMD, and US mega-cap tech behave like “safe havens”?

**Method.** I compared AMZN, AMD, QQQ, XLK, XLE, JETS, and XRT from the `2026-05-01` close to the `2026-05-08` close, and cross-checked the moves against Nasdaq and S&P 500 index data.

| Asset | `2026-05-01` close | `2026-05-08` close | Weekly return | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| AMZN | `268.26` | `272.68` | `+1.65%` | Cloud, advertising, and logistics platform cash-flow defensiveness |
| AMD | `360.54` | `455.19` | `+26.25%` | AI semiconductor beta and earnings-upgrade trade |
| QQQ | `674.15` | `711.23` | `+5.50%` | Mega-cap growth absorbed capital |
| XLK | `161.87` | `175.52` | `+8.44%` | Technology materially outperformed the index |
| XLE | `58.85` | `55.70` | `-5.35%` | Energy equities gave back gains as oil risk premium faded |
| JETS | `25.76` | `27.48` | `+6.68%` | Pressured early by oil, then repaired as oil fell |
| XRT | `85.58` | `84.72` | `-1.01%` | Retail still carries cost and confidence pressure |

**Findings.** The “safe-haven” bid in tech is not classic low volatility; it is capital choosing assets that can still prove demand and margins in a stagflationary noise environment. AMD’s `+26.25%` shows that the AI compute chain remains the strongest beta, while AMZN’s `+1.65%` is closer to a platform-cash-flow defense. Energy did not lead: XLE fell `-5.35%`, showing that once oil risk premium retreated, investors preferred “benefits from lower oil + AI demand” over direct energy exposure. The risk is that valuations have already repriced quickly; if June-cut hopes fade further, concept-driven tech should be more vulnerable than cash-flow tech.

**Evidence.** StockAnalysis price history for AMZN, AMD, QQQ, XLK, XLE, JETS, XRT; Investing.com Nasdaq Composite; AP Friday US market close.  
**Caveats.** This does not include company-level earnings-model revisions or implied volatility. “Safe haven” here means relative capital preference, not low drawdown.
