# Weekly Research Report: Inflation Rebound, AI Capex, and Inventory-Led Manufacturing Strength

**Coverage window: April 27, 2026 to May 1, 2026. Bottom line: this was not a simple risk-on week. Inflation re-accelerated, policy stayed restrictive, AI revenue showed real traction, and investors simultaneously repriced the cost of the AI capex cycle.**

## Priority Conclusions

| Conclusion | Numeric Evidence | Research Judgment |
|---|---:|---|
| Inflation pressure rose again, but the plan’s “core PCE 3.5%” needs correction | March PCE YoY 3.5%, core PCE YoY 3.2%, core MoM 0.3% | Disinflation has not fully broken, but energy and supply-chain shocks make a rapid Fed pivot unlikely. |
| The Fed remains in restrictive territory | Fed funds target range 3.50%-3.75%; 10Y Treasury yield 4.31% on Apr. 24 and 4.40% on Apr. 30 | Using core PCE YoY of 3.2% as a rough comparison, the top of the policy range is only 55bp higher; the real-rate buffer is thin. |
| AI revenue is real, but capex is moving faster | MSFT AI annual revenue run-rate $37B, +123%; Q1 capex intensity across the four major cloud/platform names was about 24.4%-37.3% | The market rewarded GOOGL’s AI monetization in Cloud and Search, but punished META/MSFT/NVDA for capex intensity and compute-demand uncertainty. |
| DeepSeek V4 is an efficiency shock, not evidence of immediate order cuts this week | DeepSeek-V4 released Apr. 24; 1.6T Pro, 284B Flash, 1M context; NVDA fell -4.72% for the week | The impact on Nvidia is best read as a valuation and long-run unit-economics reset, not confirmed evidence of a collapse in cloud rental prices. |

## Question 1: AI Valuation Reset

### Method
I compared MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN, and META on reported AI/cloud revenue signals, capex intensity, and margin movement. Stock moves are calculated from the Apr. 24, 2026 close to the May 1, 2026 close. DeepSeek V4 impact is assessed through release data, system-efficiency descriptions, and weekly price reactions in NVDA and the cloud platforms.

### Numeric Findings

| Company | AI/cloud revenue signal | Latest quarterly revenue | Revenue YoY | Capex or PPE purchases | Capex/revenue | Operating-margin change | Weekly stock move |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| MSFT | AI annual revenue run-rate $37B, +123% | $82.886B | +18% | $30.876B | 37.3% | +60bp | -2.40% |
| GOOGL | Google Cloud $20.028B, about +63% | $109.896B | +22% | $35.674B | 32.5% | +200bp | +11.99% |
| AMZN | AWS $37.6B, +28%; chips run-rate >$20B | $181.5B | +17% | $44.203B | 24.4% | +140bp | +1.62% |
| META | Ad revenue $55.024B, +33%; AI improving ad efficiency | $56.311B | +33% | $19.840B | 35.2% | -90bp | -9.82% |
| AAPL | Apple Intelligence/on-device AI narrative; no cloud-style AI revenue disclosed | $111.184B | +17% | Six-month PPE $4.344B | Not applicable | Not used in radar chart | +3.35% |
| NVDA | No earnings this week; used as compute-demand proxy | Not applicable | Not applicable | Not applicable | Not applicable | Not applicable | -4.72% |

### Interpretation
The numeric answer is that AI revenue validation exists, but the capex match is uneven. MSFT’s AI run-rate growth of +123% supports the long-term story, but quarterly PPE additions of $30.876B, equal to 37.3% of revenue, keep free cash flow and depreciation risk in focus. GOOGL had the best combination this week: revenue +22%, Google Cloud at $20.028B, and operating margin up about 200bp, so investors rewarded both AI monetization and scale economics. META was the opposite: revenue +33% was not enough to offset concern around its raised full-year capex guide of $125B-$145B, and the stock fell -9.82%. DeepSeek V4 is better understood as a long-run inference-cost shock than as hard evidence that cloud capex orders were canceled this week.

### Evidence
- Microsoft FY26 Q3: revenue $82.9B, EPS $4.27, AI run-rate $37B, Microsoft Cloud $54.5B, from Microsoft Investor Relations.
- Alphabet Q1 2026: revenue $109.896B, Google Cloud $20.028B, PPE $35.674B, from Alphabet earnings release.
- Amazon Q1 2026: revenue $181.5B, AWS $37.6B, PPE purchases $44.203B, and Trainium/OpenAI/Anthropic customer disclosures, from Amazon earnings release.
- Meta Q1 2026: revenue $56.311B, capex $19.840B, full-year capex guide $125B-$145B, from Meta Investor Relations.
- Apple Q2 FY2026: revenue $111.184B and EPS $2.01, from Apple earnings release.
- DeepSeek Transparency: DeepSeek-V4 release date April 24, 2026; LMSYS described V4 Pro 1.6T, Flash 284B, 1M context, and system optimizations.

### Caveats
Cloud compute rental prices do not have an auditable, consistent public weekly time series, so this report does not claim that DeepSeek V4 already caused rental prices to fall. Company capex definitions differ: MSFT uses additions to property and equipment, Alphabet/Amazon use purchases of property and equipment, and Meta includes finance leases in capex. AAPL does not disclose standalone AI revenue, so it is not directly comparable with hyperscalers.

## Question 2: Inflation Stickiness and Rate-Cut Room

### Method
I checked BEA Personal Income and Outlays, BEA GDP advance estimate, and the Fed’s Apr. 29 FOMC statement. I compared monthly PCE, quarterly PCE price measures, and the policy-rate range.

### Numeric Findings

| Indicator | Latest | Prior/comparison | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| PCE YoY, March 2026 | 3.5% | February 2.8% | Energy and supply shocks lifted headline inflation. |
| Core PCE YoY, March 2026 | 3.2% | February 3.0% | The plan’s 3.5% was headline, not core. |
| Core PCE MoM, March 2026 | 0.3% | February 0.4% | Monthly core inflation remained high but did not accelerate. |
| Q1 PCE price index SAAR | 4.5% | Q4 2.9% | A clear quarterly inflation pulse. |
| Q1 core PCE SAAR | 4.3% | Q4 2.7% | Difficult for the Fed to ignore. |
| Fed funds target range | 3.50%-3.75% | Held steady | 2026 cuts require renewed disinflation and labor-market cooling. |

### Interpretation
The numeric answer is that disinflation has not fully stalled, but the March and Q1 data are enough to delay rate cuts. Core PCE YoY at 3.2% remains well above the 2% target, and Q1 core PCE annualized at 4.3% reinforces Fed caution. The Fed statement directly referenced elevated inflation, global energy prices, and Middle East uncertainty while holding the target range at 3.50%-3.75%. Rate cuts are still possible in 2026, but they depend on the next two to three core monthly readings moving closer to 0.2% and energy shocks fading.

### Evidence
- BEA Personal Income and Outlays, March 2026: PCE YoY 3.5%, core PCE MoM 0.3%, core PCE YoY 3.2%.
- BEA GDP Advance Estimate, Q1 2026: real GDP +2.0%; PCE price index +4.5% SAAR; core PCE +4.3% SAAR.
- Fed FOMC statement, April 29, 2026: target range 3.50%-3.75%, with emphasis on inflation and Middle East uncertainty.
- FRED DGS10: 10Y Treasury yield was 4.31% on Apr. 24, 2026 and 4.40% on Apr. 30, 2026.

### Caveats
Monthly PCE data are subject to revision. Q1 GDP is the advance estimate, with the second estimate due May 28. Subtracting core PCE YoY from the policy-rate range is only a rough real-rate lens and is not the same as an ex-ante real policy rate.

## Question 3: Growth “No Landing”

### Method
I combined BEA Q1 GDP, ISM Manufacturing, and S&P Global Manufacturing PMI, with emphasis on separating final demand from inventory and stock-building effects.

### Numeric Findings

| Indicator | Latest | Comparison | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Real GDP Q1 SAAR | 2.0% | Q4 2025 was 0.5% | Growth rebounded. |
| Real final sales to private domestic purchasers | 2.5% | Q4 2025 was 1.8% | Private domestic demand stayed firm. |
| ISM Manufacturing PMI | 52.7 | March 52.7; consensus 53.2 | Expansion continued but missed expectations. |
| S&P Global Manufacturing PMI | 54.5 | March 52.3 | Strongest since May 2022. |
| ISM Employment | 46.4 | March 48.7 | Expansion is not translating into hiring. |
| ISM Prices Paid | 84.6 | March 78.3 | Cost pressure is extreme. |

### Interpretation
The numeric answer is that the U.S. economy is not recessionary, but it is also not cleanly overheating; the better description is “resilient growth plus cost shock plus inventory building.” Real GDP +2.0% and real final sales to private domestic purchasers +2.5% show that high rates have not crushed domestic demand. Manufacturing PMI confirms expansion, but the employment index at 46.4 remains contractionary, suggesting firms are not treating the order improvement as durable. S&P Global explicitly tied part of the order and output strength to stock building, so the “no landing” risk is real but lower quality than the headline suggests.

### Evidence
- BEA GDP Q1 2026 advance estimate.
- S&P Global US Manufacturing PMI April 2026: 54.5, collected Apr. 9-27.
- ISM/First Trust April 2026: PMI 52.7, New Orders 54.1, Employment 46.4, Prices Paid 84.6.

### Caveats
The original ISM release was not fully accessible through the current retrieval path, so this report uses First Trust’s reproduction of the ISM data table, which cites the National Association of Purchasing Management. PMI is a diffusion index, not a dollar value of output.

## Question 4: Geopolitical Risk Pricing and Inventory Building

### Method
I compared PMI supply-chain and price subcomponents with the BNO oil proxy, UUP dollar proxy, 10Y Treasury yields, and sector ETF performance. The goal was to distinguish a temporary inventory pulse from structural supply-chain cost.

### Numeric Findings

| Indicator | Weekly/latest value | Direction | Meaning |
|---|---:|---|---|
| BNO Brent proxy | +8.90% | Up | Energy risk premium rose. |
| XLE Energy | +3.48% | Led sectors | Equity market priced the energy shock. |
| UUP dollar proxy | -0.25% | Slightly down | The dollar was not the main haven asset this week. |
| 10Y yield | 4.31% -> 4.40% | +9bp | Inflation and term-premium pressure rose. |
| S&P Global PMI | 54.5 | Strong expansion | Release linked strength to inventory building, prices, and delivery delays. |
| ISM Supplier Deliveries | 60.6 | March 58.9 | Delivery delays intensified. |

### Interpretation
The numeric answer is that inventory building looks more like a short-term impulse, but it is exposing longer-term supply-chain costs. BNO +8.90%, XLE +3.48%, ISM Prices Paid 84.6, and Supplier Deliveries 60.6 all point in the same direction: geopolitical stress has entered corporate pricing and inventory behavior. If conflict and shipping/energy disruptions ease in May, the inventory-driven portion of PMI strength can fade. If they persist, firms may shift from one-off safety stock to more durable supply-chain redundancy and localized sourcing, with margin pressure showing up later.

### Evidence
- S&P Global PMI April 2026: inventory building, supply disruption, and price increases as drivers of stronger orders and output.
- ISM/First Trust April 2026: Prices Paid 84.6 and Supplier Deliveries 60.6.
- Nasdaq ETF historical data: BNO, XLE, UUP, Apr. 24 to May 1, 2026.
- FRED DGS10: Apr. 24 to Apr. 30, 2026.

### Caveats
BNO and UUP are ETF proxies, not official Brent spot or DXY index levels. Some global markets were closed on May 1, so cross-market repricing may be delayed to May 4-May 5.
