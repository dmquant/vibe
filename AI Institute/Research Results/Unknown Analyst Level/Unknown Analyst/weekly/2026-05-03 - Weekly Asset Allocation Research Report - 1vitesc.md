## Question 1: Labor Resilience vs Inflation Pressure

### Method
I checked the official BLS release calendar and the latest CES release, compared the plan’s assumption of a May 1 April payrolls release with the official schedule, and used already released January-March 2026 employment data plus consensus records as a substitute sample.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest available value | Consensus/prior | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| April NFP | Not released | BLS schedule: **2026-05-08 08:30 ET** | This week cannot verify April labor cooling |
| March NFP | **+178K** | Dow Jones consensus about **+59K/60K** | Stronger than expected; not supportive of rapid cuts |
| March unemployment | **4.3%** | February **4.4%** | Labor market remained resilient |
| February NFP | **-92K**; common revised media references show **-133K** | January about **+126K/+160K** | Monthly data are volatile; one month is not decisive |

Interpretation: The key labor conclusion this week is that there was no new April payroll conclusion. The April Employment Situation had not been released, so it cannot confirm that labor cooling is sufficient for rate cuts this year. The latest official March data instead showed upside payroll surprise and a lower unemployment rate at 4.3%, which does not fit an abrupt labor-market deterioration narrative. Given the negative February print and strong March rebound, a three-month average is more useful than any one month; the current picture is “slowing with volatility,” not recession. Allocation should not add front-end duration or rate-cut beneficiaries solely on the plan’s May 1 assumption.

### Evidence
- BLS CES page: March nonfarm payrolls **+178K**, unemployment **4.3%**, and the April Employment Situation release date of **2026-05-08**. Source: https://www.bls.gov/ces/
- BLS 2026 release calendar: Employment Situation for April 2026 is **Friday, May 08, 2026 08:30 AM**. Source: https://www.bls.gov/schedule/news_release/current_year.asp
- Econoday/CME summary shows the April NFP consensus at **+130K**, but the release was still pending as of this report. Source: https://www.cmegroup.com/education/events/econoday/636599

### Caveats
April NFP has not been released, so the actual-surprise calculation cannot be made. Some market calendars place the employment report in the following week, not this week, so the original plan date needs correction. February data also carry revision ambiguity; this report preserves the BLS initial figure and common market-reported revised figure.

## Question 2: Quality of Big Tech Earnings

### Method
I compared reported revenue, AI/cloud metrics, capex guidance, and post-earnings share-price reactions across Apple, Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, and Meta, using company releases plus AP/LPL market summaries and public financial reporting.

### Findings

| Company/index | Key earnings number | Price/index reaction | What the market rewarded or penalized |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Apple (AAPL) | FY2Q26 revenue **$111.2B**, EPS **$2.01**, net income about **$29.6B** | AP said AAPL rose **3.3%** Friday | Rewarded revenue and profit beat |
| Amazon (AMZN) | AWS sales **$37.6B**, **+28%** YoY | Most reports were positive, though after-hours/intraday windows differ | Rewarded cloud growth, while scrutinizing AI capex |
| Alphabet | Revenue **$110B**, net income **$62.6B**; Google Cloud **$20B** | LPL cited Alphabet as a key April 30 leader | Rewarded cloud and AI monetization |
| Meta | Revenue **$56.3B**, capex guidance raised to **$125B-$145B** | Reports showed share-price pressure | Penalized capex expansion |
| Microsoft | Revenue **$82.89B**, EPS **$4.27**; 2026 capex about **$190B** | Fell more than **2%** after hours | Cloud growth was not enough to offset capex concerns |
| Nasdaq Composite | Weekly return **+1.12%** | Record close | At the index level, earnings growth was rewarded |

Interpretation: The market did not punish AI capex across the board; it differentiated between spending that is already converting into cloud, advertising, or device revenue and spending that is still an open-ended promise. Apple, Alphabet, and Amazon show that investors will keep paying a premium when revenue and profits cover the investment burden. Meta and Microsoft show that when capex guidance rises faster than visible incremental revenue, the stock reaction can be negative. In allocation terms, technology can remain overweight, but the exposure should tilt toward free cash flow, cloud backlog, and verified margin resilience rather than simply the largest AI spending plans.

### Evidence

- Axios: Apple net income about **$29.6B**, EPS **$2.01**, revenue **$111.2B**. Source: https://www.axios.com/2026/04/30/apple-earnings-q2-2026
- Amazon Q1 2026 release: AWS sales **$37.6B**, **+28%** YoY. Source: https://s2.q4cdn.com/299287126/files/doc_earnings/2026/q1/earnings-result/AMZN-Q1-2026-Earnings-Release.pdf

### Caveats

## Question 3: China Growth Momentum

### Method
I used the official NBS/CFLP April 2026 PMI release and subindexes, compared March and April sequential changes, and focused on headline manufacturing PMI, new orders, new export orders, production, and non-manufacturing activity.

### Findings

| Indicator | 2026-03 | 2026-04 | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Manufacturing PMI | **50.4** | **50.3** | **-0.1** |
| Production | **51.4** | **51.5** | **+0.1** |
| New orders | **51.6** | **50.6** | **-1.0** |
| New export orders | **49.1** | **50.3** | **+1.2** |
| Imports | **49.8** | **50.1** | **+0.3** |
| Purchases | **50.9** | **51.1** | **+0.2** |
| Non-manufacturing business activity | **50.1** | **49.4** | **-0.7** |
| Composite PMI output | **50.5** | **50.1** | **-0.4** |

Interpretation: Manufacturing stayed in expansion, but the quality of expansion was uneven: production edged higher, new export orders moved back above 50, while new orders fell from 51.6 to 50.6. External demand was the bright spot, and the improvement in imports and purchases suggests firms did not move into contraction. The problem is that non-manufacturing activity fell to 49.4, which weakens the case for a balanced recovery across services and construction. For A-shares and Hong Kong equities, this supports a shift from “broad recovery beta” toward selected exporters, equipment makers, AI hardware, and advanced manufacturing.

### Evidence
- NBS April 2026 PMI: manufacturing PMI **50.3**, production **51.5**, new orders **50.6**, new export orders **50.3**. Source: https://www.stats.gov.cn/sj/zxfbhjd/202604/t20260430_1963473.html
- NBS interpretation: manufacturing PMI stayed above the threshold for the second consecutive month, with production and demand still expanding. Source: https://www.stats.gov.cn/sj/sjjd/202604/t20260430_1963474.html
- Hong Kong market: Hang Seng Index closed **25,776.53** on April 30, down **1.28%** that day. Source: https://english.news.cn/20260430/09d4d449accf4b08b3ec4b08e960a73f/c.html

### Caveats
PMI is a diffusion index and does not measure output or profit magnitude. Mainland China and Hong Kong markets were closed for Labor Day after April 30, so they have not fully repriced Friday’s overseas information.

### Method

|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 3M | **3.69%** | **3.68%** | **-1bp** |

Interpretation: The curve delivered a modest bearish flattening: 2Y and 5Y yields rose 10bp, while the 10Y rose 8bp, indicating that markets did not interpret the FOMC as clearly dovish. Duration assets are more attractive than they were in a low-yield regime, but the weekly rise in yields still created price losses, with the Bloomberg U.S. Aggregate down 0.50%. The formal Treasury quarterly refunding announcement was not inside this week’s window; the next key supply signal is on 2026-05-06. The better implementation is an intermediate-duration ladder, not an aggressive long-bond chase.

## Question 5: Cross-Asset Correlation and Risk Parity

### Method
I calculated daily S&P 500 returns from AP closing levels between 2026-04-24 and 2026-05-01. I proxied 10Y Treasury bond returns as daily 10Y yield changes multiplied by **-7** years of duration, then calculated the five-observation correlation.

### Findings

| Date | S&P 500 close | S&P 500 daily return | 10Y yield | 10Y duration-proxy bond return |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 2026-04-24 | **7,165.08** | -- | **4.31%** | -- |
| 2026-04-27 | **7,173.91** | **+0.12%** | **4.35%** | **-0.28%** |
| 2026-04-28 | **7,138.80** | **-0.49%** | **4.36%** | **-0.07%** |
| 2026-04-29 | **7,135.95** | **-0.04%** | **4.42%** | **-0.42%** |
| 2026-04-30 | **7,209.01** | **+1.02%** | **4.40%** | **+0.14%** |
| 2026-05-01 | **7,230.12** | **+0.29%** | **4.39%** | **+0.07%** |
| Correlation | -- | -- | -- | **+0.54** |

Interpretation: Stock-bond price correlation was positive this week, not the negative relationship risk parity portfolios prefer. Midweek inflation, oil, and FOMC repricing pressured both equities and bonds; Thursday and Friday then saw a joint recovery as earnings and lower oil restored risk appetite. In a positive-correlation regime, bonds provide less cushion against equity drawdowns and realized portfolio volatility can run above historical calibration. Portfolio construction should lower leverage, hold more cash/T-bills, and supplement diversification with gold or other risk-budget controls.

### Evidence
- AP: S&P 500 closing levels for April 24, April 27, April 28, April 29, April 30, and May 1. Source: https://apnews.com/

### Caveats
The sample contains only five daily returns, so the correlation estimate is unstable. Bond returns are a duration approximation, not actual TLT or Bloomberg Treasury index returns.
