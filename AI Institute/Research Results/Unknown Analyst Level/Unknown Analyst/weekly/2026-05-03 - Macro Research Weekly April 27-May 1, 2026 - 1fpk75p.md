# Macro Research Weekly: April 27-May 1, 2026

## Priority Conclusions

The week’s macro tone was “growth resilience with a higher inflation bar for rate cuts.” The Fed kept the federal funds target range at 3.50%-3.75% on April 29; its statement described activity as expanding at a “solid pace,” job gains as “low,” and inflation as “elevated,” while the vote split widened to 8-4. Reporting based on CME FedWatch showed an 83.8% probability on May 1 that the target range would still be unchanged at year-end 2026. Euro area flash HICP rose to 3.0% in April from 2.6% in March, with energy at 10.9% year over year, and the ECB kept the deposit facility rate at 2.00% on April 30; the BoJ held at 0.75% on April 28 in a 6-3 vote, with three members seeking a hike to 1.0%. This is not a coordinated easing setup; it is a hawkish pause under an energy-driven inflation shock.

China’s recovery is still tilted toward industry, external demand, and policy-linked supply chains rather than balanced domestic momentum. The official manufacturing PMI was 50.3, its second straight month in expansion; production at 51.5 exceeded new orders at 50.6, new export orders returned to expansion at 50.3 after 23 consecutive months below 50, but non-manufacturing business activity fell to 49.4. The U.S. April payrolls report was not released on May 1: the BLS calendar schedules the April 2026 Employment Situation for May 8. As of this report, the latest hard labor data are March payrolls at +178k, unemployment at 4.3%, participation at 61.9%, and average hourly earnings at $37.38; wage growth is roughly 3.5% year over year, inside the lower end of the 3.5%-4.0% “non-inflationary” monitoring range, but March PCE at 3.5% and core PCE at 3.2% mean wage cooling has not yet restored inflation confidence.

## Question 1: Has the Fed’s “Hawkish Floor” Shifted Lower?

### Method
I compared the April 29, 2026 FOMC statement, the official voting outcome, and May 1 market pricing. The benchmark was the current 3.50%-3.75% target range, March PCE, and March labor data, with the focus on willingness to ease rather than the hold decision alone.

### Findings

| Indicator | Latest | Prior/comparison | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Federal funds target range | 3.50%-3.75% | Third straight hold | No rate cut |
| FOMC vote | 8 in favor, 4 dissents | 1 preferred a cut, 3 opposed “additional adjustments” language | Dissent skewed hawkish |
| Probability current range holds through 2026 | 83.8% | CME FedWatch, May 1 reporting | Markets largely priced out 2026 cuts |
| March PCE y/y | 3.5% | February 2.8% | Inflation confidence fell |
| March core PCE y/y | 3.2% | February 3.0% | Core still above target |

Interpretation: The Fed’s hawkish floor has not moved lower; it has become firmer. The statement kept optionality around “additional adjustments,” but the inflation language explicitly referenced higher global energy prices, indicating concern about the inflation path. Labor cooling is not yet enough to force a pivot because the unemployment rate at 4.3% was still “little changed,” and payrolls rebounded to +178k in March from -133k in February. The key signal is the three regional Fed presidents objecting to language that could imply further cuts, which limits the room for a rapid easing campaign under the next chair.

## Question 2: How Balanced Is China’s Recovery?

### Method
I used the NBS official April PMI release and sub-indexes, comparing March with April, manufacturing with non-manufacturing, and domestic orders with export orders. Production, new orders, new export orders, non-manufacturing activity, and composite PMI were used to judge recovery quality.

### Findings

| China PMI Indicator | March 2026 | April 2026 | m/m change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Manufacturing PMI | 50.4 | 50.3 | -0.1 |
| Production | 51.4 | 51.5 | +0.1 |
| New orders | 51.6 | 50.6 | -1.0 |
| New export orders | 49.1 | 50.3 | +1.2 |
| Imports | 49.8 | 50.1 | +0.3 |
| Purchases | 50.9 | 51.1 | +0.2 |
| Raw-material inventories | 47.7 | 49.3 | +1.6 |
| Employment | 48.6 | 48.8 | +0.2 |
| Non-manufacturing business activity | 50.1 | 49.4 | -0.7 |
| Composite PMI output | 50.5 | 50.1 | -0.4 |

Interpretation: China’s recovery is not balanced. Manufacturing expanded for a second month, but production ran ahead of new orders, pointing to recovery along policy, export, and restocking channels while final demand momentum slowed. The return of new export orders to 50.3 is the key positive surprise, showing stronger external demand in April. However, non-manufacturing fell to 49.4, suggesting services, property-linked activity, and household consumption did not improve in tandem. Employment at 48.8 remained below the threshold, so labor-income expectations are still too weak to confirm self-sustaining household demand.

### Evidence

### Caveats
PMI is a diffusion index and does not report output value or actual consumption volume. April industrial production, retail sales, and fixed-asset investment have not yet been released; conclusions on private investment and household consumption should be revisited after mid-May activity data.

## Question 3: Is the Wage-Price Spiral Broken?

### Method
The plan expected April nonfarm payrolls on May 1, but the BLS calendar schedules the April Employment Situation for 8:30 ET on May 8, 2026. I therefore used the latest published March BLS labor report, BEA March PCE, and the BLS release calendar, while treating any April conclusion as pending.

### Findings

| U.S. labor/inflation indicator | Latest | Period | Assessment |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Nonfarm payrolls | +178k | March 2026 | Rebounded, but 12-month net change still limited |
| Unemployment rate | 4.3% | March 2026 | Stable |
| Labor force participation rate | 61.9% | March 2026 | Stable |
| Average hourly earnings | $37.38 | March 2026 | About +0.2% m/m |
| Average hourly earnings y/y | About 3.5% | March 2026 | Inside the 3.5%-4.0% range |
| PCE y/y | 3.5% | March 2026 | Reaccelerated |
| Core PCE y/y | 3.2% | March 2026 | Above the 2% target |
| April jobs report release | 2026-05-08 | BLS calendar | No official April NFP on May 1 |

Interpretation: The wage-price spiral is weakening, but it is too early to declare it broken. Wage growth near 3.5% sits at the lower end of the monitoring range, implying less incremental labor-cost pressure on services inflation. The problem is that energy lifted headline PCE to 3.5%, while core PCE at 3.2% remains too high, so inflation stickiness is not only a wage story. The missing April jobs report is the decisive data gap; if May 8 shows average hourly earnings still in the 3.5%-4.0% range without a sharp rise in unemployment, the wage-risk downgrade would be more convincing.

## Question 4: Where Is the “Resonance Point” in Global Manufacturing?

### Method
I compared April manufacturing PMIs for China, the U.S., Germany, and Japan using the most timely official or S&P Global/ISM series available. I assessed whether the inventory cycle is entering restocking by looking at headline PMI, orders, inventories/customer inventories, export orders, and employment.

### Findings

| Economy | April manufacturing PMI | March manufacturing PMI | April key sub-indexes | Assessment |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| U.S. ISM | 52.7 | 52.7 | New orders 54.1, production 53.4, employment 46.4, customers’ inventories 39.1 | Orders and low inventories support restocking, but employment is weak and prices are strong |
| China NBS | 50.3 | 50.4 | Production 51.5, new orders 50.6, new export orders 50.3, raw-material inventories 49.3 | Mild expansion, with better external demand |
| Germany HCOB | 48.4 | 48.3 | Output rose at the fastest pace in more than three years; export orders improved; employment still declined | Still contracting, but close to a turn |
| Japan au Jibun Bank | 52.3 | 51.6 | April final 52.3 | Expansion improved |

Interpretation: The global resonance point is not a broad boom; it is an early restocking chain led by U.S. orders, Chinese exports, Japanese expansion, and improving German output. U.S. ISM customers’ inventories at 39.1 are “too low,” which usually supports future production. China’s new export orders rose from 49.1 to 50.3, showing that Asian supply chains are receiving stronger external-demand signals. Germany’s PMI remains below 50 at 48.4, so Europe has not fully joined expansion. Price pressure is the main constraint: U.S. ISM prices were reported as the strongest since late 2021, while euro area energy inflation was 10.9%, threatening real demand.

### Evidence
ISM Manufacturing PMI, April 2026, released 2026-05-01: https://www.ismworld.org/supply-management-news-and-reports/reports/ism-pmi-reports/pmi/april/  
NBS, China PMI for April 2026: https://www.stats.gov.cn/sj/zxfbhjd/202604/t20260430_1963473.html  
S&P Global/HCOB Germany Manufacturing PMI, April 2026: https://www.pmi.spglobal.com/Public/Home/PressRelease/c62bd0397ef64333a71fd83d0513000a  
S&P Global au Jibun Bank Japan Manufacturing PMI, April final 52.3, source page: https://www.pmi.spglobal.com/Public/Home/PressRelease/226a56271ae1420fb140d1bf472c50be

### Caveats
The PMI definitions differ: the U.S. uses ISM, Germany and Japan use S&P Global surveys, and China uses the official NBS survey. Germany is still below 50, so global manufacturing cannot yet be described as synchronized expansion.
