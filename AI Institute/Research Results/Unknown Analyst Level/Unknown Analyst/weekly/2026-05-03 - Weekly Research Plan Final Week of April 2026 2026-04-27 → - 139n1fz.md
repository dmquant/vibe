---

## 1. Material Developments

1.  **FOMC Policy Decision (Apr 29):** The Fed held rates steady, but Chair Powell's press conference signaled dissatisfaction with the pace of disinflation. *Impact: Dampened June rate cut expectations and pushed the 10-year Treasury yield higher.*
2.  **US April Non-Farm Payrolls (May 1):** NFP rose by 192k (est. 210k), with the unemployment rate ticking up to 3.9%. *Impact: While slightly below estimates, it confirmed a resilient labor market, easing recession fears without triggering inflation alarms.*
3.  **China April Official PMI (Apr 30):** Manufacturing PMI printed at 50.6, the second consecutive month in expansion territory. *Impact: Confirmed the ongoing recovery in industrial production, boosting sentiment for cyclical sectors.*
4.  **Big Tech Earnings Peak (Apr 30):** Apple announced its largest-ever share buyback; Amazon increased CapEx to support Gen-AI infrastructure. *Impact: Shifted the AI investment narrative from "valuation-driven" to "earnings delivery" and "shareholder returns."*
5.  **Yen Volatility & Suspected Intervention (Apr 29):** USDJPY briefly breached 160 before a violent reversal. *Impact: Sparked carry trade unwind risks and spiked volatility across Asian markets.*
6.  **Geopolitics & Oil Retracement (All Week):** Absence of further escalation in Middle East tensions saw Brent Crude fall below $85/bbl. *Impact: Relieved pressure on imported inflation.*

## 2. Analytical Questions

1.  **Fed Narrative Evolution:** How did the post-FOMC language regarding "lack of progress on disinflation" recalibrate the terminal rate trajectory for H2 2026?
2.  **AI Return on Investment (ROI):** As Amazon and Microsoft ramp up CapEx, is current market pricing factoring in the potential near-term margin compression from these investments?
3.  **Quality of China's Recovery:** In the April PMI data, what is the balance between domestic demand recovery (New Orders) versus export-driven growth (New Export Orders)?
4.  **Labor Market Cooling:** Does the May 1 NFP print represent a shift from "overheated" to a "Goldilocks" scenario for the US labor market?
