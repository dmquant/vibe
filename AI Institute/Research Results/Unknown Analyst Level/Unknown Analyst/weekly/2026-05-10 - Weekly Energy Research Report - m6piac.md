## Executive Summary
This week, global energy markets experienced a drastic shift in pricing logic. Crude oil spiked on geopolitical premiums before retracing sharply as diplomatic signals emerged; Brent peaked at $114/bbl before retreating. The UAE's formal exit from OPEC marks the beginning of structural decentralization in oil supply power. The natural gas market saw a surge in global premiums due to the convergence of U.S. export maintenance and Middle Eastern supply disruptions. Meanwhile, the latest IRENA report established the absolute economic superiority of "Solar + Storage" over fossil fuels, signaling an acceleration in long-term transition drivers.

## Chapter 1: Crude Oil and Geopolitical Risk
### 1.1 Hormuz Strait Crisis and Price Trends
*   **Method:** Monitored daily Brent/WTI prices from May 4-8, comparing physical flow monitoring in the Strait of Hormuz with diplomatic milestones between the U.S. and Iran.
*   **Findings:**
    | Date | Brent (USD/bbl) | WTI (USD/bbl) | Key Event Milestones |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | 05-04 | 114.20 | 109.50 | Blockade escalates; expected daily supply loss of 6.7M bbl |
    | 05-05 | 111.45 | 106.80 | Market prices in physical disruption; EU refineries rush for non-ME grades |
    | 05-06 | 108.30 | 104.10 | Rumors of second SPR release and U.S. diplomatic intervention |
    | 05-07 | 103.15 | 99.40 | U.S. confirms diplomatic progress; short covering drives sharp retreat |
    | 05-08 | 105.40 | 101.20 | Geopolitical premium stabilizes; blockade risk remains as basis stays high |
*   **Analysis:** The early-week price surge was a classic "panic premium," with liquidity drying up as the market priced in a potential 6.7 million bpd loss. The late-week correction was driven by short covering and "front-running" diplomatic resolutions rather than a fundamental resolution. With physical risks persisting, strong support has formed at $100.
*   **Evidence:** Reuters May 4 headline "Hormuz Blockade: 20% of Global Oil at Risk"; White House Press Briefing May 7.
*   **Caveats:** Satellite monitoring of physical flows is hampered by cloud cover; "Shadow Fleet" volumes remain difficult to quantify precisely.

### 1.2 Impact of UAE's OPEC Exit
*   **Method:** Analyzed ADNOC's capacity deployment plans following the UAE's formal exit on May 1 to assess its impact on OPEC+'s remaining pricing power.
*   **Findings:**
    | Metric | Value | Implications |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | UAE Current Capacity | 4.85 Million bpd | ~1.3M bpd above previous OPEC quota |
    | 2027 Target Capacity | 5.00 Million bpd | Achieved one year ahead of schedule |
    | OPEC+ Global Share | 45% (Estimated) | Lowest level in a decade |
*   **Analysis:** The UAE's exit signals a shift where "national interest" supersedes "collective production control." Abu Dhabi's export capacity via Fujairah (bypassing Hormuz) provides strategic flexibility that Saudi Arabia lacks, which will likely weigh on the oil price floor throughout H2 2026.

## Chapter 3: New Energy and Power Transition
### 3.1 IRENA LCOE Tipping Point
*   **Method:** Compared dynamic costs of "Solar + Storage" vs. traditional CCGT based on IRENA's "Renewable Power Generation Costs in 2026" (released May 7).
*   **Findings:**
    | Technology | LCOE (USD/MWh) | Trend |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | Solid-State Bat. + PV (Firm) | 54.0 — 82.0 | ↓ 12% YoY decline |
    | Gas (CCGT) | > 100.0 | ↑ Driven by high gas prices |
    | Coal (High Eff.) | 70.0 — 85.0 | Stable |
*   **Analysis:** 2026 is the year of "Economic Substitution." Even without carbon taxes, solar plants with 4+ hours of storage are now cheaper than gas-fired generation in high-price environments. This explains the surge in Capex for utility-scale storage by EU and Middle Eastern sovereign wealth funds despite the oil market volatility.
