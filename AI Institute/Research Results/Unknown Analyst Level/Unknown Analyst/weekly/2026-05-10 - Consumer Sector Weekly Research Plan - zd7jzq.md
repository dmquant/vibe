# Consumer Sector Weekly Research Plan

Coverage window: 2026-05-04 (Monday) → 2026-05-08 (Friday)

Local date anchor: 2026-05-10. Mainland A-share markets were closed on 2026-05-04 and 2026-05-05 for the Labor Day holiday, so trading-day metrics should include only 2026-05-06 to 2026-05-08; Hong Kong traded normally from 2026-05-04 to 2026-05-08.

## Priority Research Judgments

1. This week's consumer story is not a clean "holiday boom"; it is a mix of strong traffic, strong services and still-to-be-tested ticket size. Official data showed 325 million domestic trips during the 2026 May Day holiday, up 3.6% YoY, tourism spending of RMB 185.49 billion, up 2.9% YoY, implying about RMB 570.7 per trip; VAT-invoice data showed consumption-related sales revenue up 14.3% YoY.[1][2]
2. A-share consumer lagged growth and home-appliance themes after the holiday. From the 2026-04-30 close to the 2026-05-08 close, CSI 300 rose 1.34%, Shanghai Composite rose 1.65%, Shenzhen Component rose 3.02%; liquor ETF `512690.SS` fell 1.65%, consumer ETF `159928.SZ` fell 1.74%, food and beverage ETF `515650.SS` fell 1.34%, while home appliance ETF `159996.SZ` rose 2.49%.[8]
3. The baijiu section must separate Wuliangye from the rest of the industry. `000858.SZ` opened down 6.5% on the first post-holiday trading day and fell 5.10% over the A-share trading window; the catalysts were 2025 net profit attributable to shareholders of RMB 8.954 billion, down 71.89% YoY, plus an RMB 8-10 billion buyback plan and an RMB 3-5 billion shareholding-increase plan.[3][4][8]
4. Travel, duty-free, restaurants and local services need separate treatment. Traffic and catering VAT data were strong, but `601888.SS` fell 4.48% during the week, while Hong Kong-listed consumer names diverged: `3690.HK` +0.96%, `6862.HK` +2.58%, `9987.HK` -2.36%, `9633.HK` -1.37%.[2][8]
5. Cost and macro risk should not sit in the appendix. Brent crude was still fluctuating around USD 100 per barrel during the week, affecting F&B, retail and travel margins through logistics, packaging, travel costs and household disposable income.[7]

## 2. Key Questions

1. Was May Day consumption a traffic recovery or a ticket-size recovery?
2. Have trade-in-policy benefits spread from autos to home appliances, digital products and offline retail?
3. Is the baijiu selloff a Wuliangye-specific event, or a broader repricing of premium demand and channel inventory?
4. Which restaurants, travel, duty-free and local-services companies can convert holiday traffic into profit?
5. Why did consumer underperform growth and home appliances: short-term rotation or insufficient fundamental confirmation?
6. How will Brent near USD 100 per barrel affect F&B, packaging, logistics, travel and household consumption budgets?
