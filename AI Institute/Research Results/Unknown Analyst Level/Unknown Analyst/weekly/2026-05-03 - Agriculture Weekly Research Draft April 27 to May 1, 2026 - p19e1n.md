# Agriculture Weekly Research Draft (April 27 to May 1, 2026)

## Priority Conclusions

The week’s agricultural market theme was not “smooth planting pushes prices lower,” but “fast U.S. planting improves the supply cushion while weather, energy, and input risks rebuild risk premium.” As of April 26, 2026, U.S. corn planting reached 25%, above the five-year average of 19%; yet from April 27 to May 1, CBOT July corn still rose from $4.6925/bu to $4.8025/bu, up 2.3%. China’s hog sector remains deeply below breakeven, with the hog-to-corn ratio at only 4.00 in the fourth week of April, so the short-term rebound is not enough to stop sow-herd liquidation. On inputs, nitrogen is the binding pressure, not potash; stable potash looks more like cost stabilization than a strong incentive to raise application rates. The climate premise in the plan also needs correction: NOAA’s April 9, 2026 update issued a “Final La Niña Advisory / El Niño Watch,” not a rapid La Niña transition; oilseed risk premium should therefore be reframed around El Niño and weather disruption across South America, Australia, and Asia.

## Question 1: Has Fast Planting Been Fully Priced Into CBOT Corn?

### Method
I used USDA NASS Crop Progress, released April 27, 2026 and covering the week ending April 26, to compare 2026 corn and soybean planting against 2025 and the 2021-2025 average. I then used Successful Farming daily closing-market reports from April 27 to May 1 to track CBOT July corn and July soybean closes and test whether the planting news was enough to pressure prices.

### Findings

| Metric | Apr 26, 2026 | 2025 same week | 2021-2025 average | 2026 vs. 5-year average | Weekly change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| U.S. corn planted | 25% | 22% | 19% | +6ppt | +14ppt |
| U.S. corn emerged | 7% | 5% | 4% | +3ppt | +3ppt |
| U.S. soybeans planted | 23% | 17% | 12% | +11ppt | +11ppt |
| CBOT July corn | $4.8025/bu | N/A | N/A | N/A | +2.3% ($4.6925 to $4.8025) |
| CBOT July soybeans | $12.0325/bu | N/A | N/A | N/A | +0.9% ($11.9200 to $12.0325) |

The planting pace is clearly bearish for the initial supply setup, especially in soybeans, where progress was 11 percentage points above the five-year average. But prices did not trade on a simple “fast planting equals lower futures” rule: July corn and soybeans both rose over the report week, meaning weather, war/energy, managed-money buying, and crop risk premium offset the planting pressure. On April 28, soybeans did fall and market commentary explicitly cited a “record fast planting pace” as pressure. By the end of the week, however, El Niño concerns and cold/freeze risk had rebuilt support. Conclusion: fast planting has been partly priced, but not fully priced as a bearish curve signal; deferred contracts still carry meaningful weather premium.

### Evidence
- USDA NASS, Crop Progress, released April 27, 2026: 18-state corn planted at 25%, versus 22% in 2025 and a 19% five-year average; soybeans at 23%, versus 17% in 2025 and a 12% five-year average.
- DTN, April 20, 2026 Crop Progress: April 19 corn at 11% and soybeans at 12%, above five-year averages of 9% and 5%.
- Successful Farming: April 27 July corn at $4.6925 and July soybeans at $11.9200; May 1 July corn at $4.8025 and July soybeans at $12.0325.

### Caveats
CBOT prices are taken from public closing-market reports for July contracts, not the full CME settlement database; other curve months are used only directionally. Crop progress is survey-based, and USDA notes that some weekend progress may be projected by respondents and can be revised the following week.

## Question 2: Will the Hog Price Rebound Slow Capacity Liquidation?

### Method
I used the Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Affairs livestock-market monitoring across 500 counties, the fourth-week April price bulletin reposted by Chongqing’s agriculture authority, daily wholesale pork monitoring, and public reports of the National Bureau of Statistics’ Q1 2026 breeding-sow inventory. The hog-to-corn ratio is calculated as hog price divided by corn price.

### Findings

| Metric | 3rd week of April | 4th week of April | Change | YoY | Implication |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Hog price | RMB9.75/kg | RMB10.00/kg | +2.6% | -34.6% | Rebound, but still low |
| Piglet price | RMB23.11/kg | RMB22.94/kg | -0.7% | -41.9% | Weak restocking signal |
| Pork retail-market price | RMB20.10/kg | RMB20.08/kg | -0.1% | -23.1% | Weak pass-through |
| Corn price | RMB2.50/kg | RMB2.50/kg | 0.0% | +5.0% | No feed-cost relief |
| Hog-to-corn ratio | 3.90 | 4.00 | +0.10 | N/A | Below 6:1 breakeven reference |
| Breeding sow inventory | 39.04 million head at Q1 2026 end | N/A | -1.4% QoQ | -3.3% | Still near old 39.00 million normal holding level |

The pre-Labor Day hog-price rebound looks more like holiday restocking and a bounce from depressed levels than a cycle turn. The hog-to-corn ratio was only 4.00 in the fourth week of April, far below the commonly used 6:1 breakeven reference, so producers remain under cash-flow pressure. Piglet prices continued to fall, which means restocking behavior has not confirmed a new upcycle. With the breeding sow herd still at 39.04 million head and policy pushing toward roughly 36.50 million head, a short-lived price bounce is unlikely to materially halt capacity reduction.

### Evidence
- MARA Animal Husbandry and Veterinary Bureau, April 22, 2026: third-week April piglets at RMB23.11/kg, hogs at RMB9.75/kg, pork at RMB20.10/kg, corn at RMB2.50/kg.
- Chongqing Agriculture and Rural Affairs Commission repost, April 27, 2026: fourth-week April piglets at RMB22.94/kg, hogs at RMB10.00/kg, pork at RMB20.08/kg, corn at RMB2.50/kg.
- Cailian Press/Sina Finance, April 30, 2026: national wholesale pork at RMB15.19/kg, up 0.6% day on day.
- Public reports citing NBS: breeding sows at 39.04 million head at the end of Q1 2026, down about 1.4% quarter on quarter and 3.3% year on year.

### Caveats
Retail-market, wholesale-market, and farmgate series are different price concepts and should not be subtracted directly to infer slaughter margins. Sow inventory has a reporting lag; non-quarter-end monthly estimates often rely on MARA fixed-point monitoring changes applied to NBS quarterly baselines.

## Question 3: Will Stable Potash Prices Lift Input Intensity?

### Method
I compared DTN U.S. retail fertilizer prices for April 13-17 and April 20-24, the World Bank’s April 2026 Commodity Markets Outlook, and public China urea/DAP price references. The analysis separates nitrogen, phosphate, and potash because their impacts on corn and soybeans differ materially.

### Findings

| Product | Apr 13-17, 2026 | Apr 20-24, 2026 | Weekly change | YoY/background | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| Urea | $858/short ton | $866/short ton | +0.9% | Around +50% YoY | Nitrogen remains the main pressure |
| DAP | $894/short ton | $901/short ton | +0.8% | Around +15% YoY | Phosphate firm, but less severe than nitrogen |
| Potash/MOP | $491/short ton | $492/short ton | +0.2% | Around +5% YoY | Potash comparatively stable |
| China Shandong small-granular urea | RMB1,840-1,850/ton on Apr 22 | N/A | N/A | Domestic coal-based supply cushion | Domestic pressure is lower than global pressure |
| China DAP reference | RMB3,600/ton in early April | N/A | N/A | Higher during the month | Phosphate affected by sulfur and export policy |

Stable potash alone is not enough to trigger a meaningful increase in Northern Hemisphere input intensity, because nitrogen is the real margin constraint for corn. U.S. retail urea was already $866/short ton, roughly 50% above last year, while potash was up only about 5%, concentrating the cost problem in nitrogen and parts of phosphate. At this point in the spring planting window, acreage is mostly determined by March intentions and fieldwork windows; fertilizer prices are more likely to affect application rates, side-dress timing, and cash flow than to trigger large last-minute acreage changes. Soybeans are less exposed to nitrogen costs because of biological nitrogen fixation, so the input shock is less severe than for corn.

### Evidence
- Agroinformacion/DTN Fertilizer Index: April 13-17 urea at $858/short ton, DAP at $894, potash at $491.
- Agrolatam/DTN: April 20-24 urea at $866/short ton, DAP at $901, potash at $492.
- World Bank Commodity Markets Outlook, April 2026: global urea at $725.6/ton in March, up 53.7% month on month; DAP at $658.3/ton; MOP at $380.6/ton.
- GuideChem: Shandong small-granular urea mainstream ex-factory price at RMB1,840-1,850/ton on April 22, 2026.

### Caveats
U.S. retail prices, global FOB/CFR prices, and China ex-factory prices are different concepts; dollars per short ton and dollars per metric ton also differ. Actual farm exposure depends heavily on pre-booking. farmdoc cited a Farm Bureau survey showing 67% of Midwest respondents had secured fertilizer ahead of the season, while about 70% nationally still said they could not afford all the fertilizer they needed.

## Question 4: La Niña Expectations and Soybean Premium

### Method
I verified the April 2026 NOAA CPC and IRI/CPC ENSO probability and strength tables, then retested the plan’s “rapid La Niña formation” premise. I also compared CBOT soybean price action and market commentary on South America, Australia, and Asia weather stress to judge the oilseed risk-premium channel.

### Findings

| Season window | La Niña probability | Neutral probability | El Niño probability | Oilseed weather-risk implication |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| AMJ 2026 | 0% | 80% | 20% | Near-term baseline remains neutral |
| MJJ 2026 | 0% | 39% | 61% | El Niño becomes the main scenario |
| JJA 2026 | 0% | 21% | 79% | Northern Hemisphere summer risk premium rises |
| ASO 2026 | 0% | 10% | 90% | South America planting-season risk rises |
| OND 2026 | 0% | 7% | 93% | South American soybean window needs close monitoring |

The La Niña premise in the plan does not match the latest official data; NOAA’s April conclusion is that ENSO-neutral conditions have emerged and El Niño probabilities rise quickly thereafter. For U.S. Midwest soybeans, El Niño does not automatically imply a systematic drought outcome, so a “La Niña drought premium” should not be applied mechanically to U.S. soybeans. The more important watchpoints are rainfall distribution in Brazil’s Center-West and Argentina during the 2026/27 South American planting season, plus cross-market support from drought stress in Asia and Australia. In price terms, July soybeans rose 0.9% over the week, showing some weather/energy premium but not a full weather-driven bull market.

### Evidence
- NOAA CPC ENSO Diagnostic Discussion, April 9, 2026: ENSO-neutral favored through AMJ 2026 with an 80% chance; El Niño likely in MJJ 2026 with a 61% chance; El Niño Watch issued.
- NOAA CPC ENSO Strength Probabilities, issued April 2026: OND 2026 total El Niño probability at 93%, including a 23% chance of Very Strong El Niño (Index ≥ 2.0°C).
- IRI April 2026 Quick Look: mid-April weekly NINO3.4 near +0.5°C; IRI models assign 70% El Niño probability in AMJ and 88-94% thereafter.
- Successful Farming, April 29 and May 1, 2026: market commentary cited strengthening/developing El Niño as support for weather risk premium.

### Caveats
ENSO forecasts face a spring predictability barrier, and IRI explicitly advises caution on longer-lead probabilities. ENSO phase does not map one-for-one into local weather; the next stage needs U.S. Drought Monitor, NOAA seasonal precipitation outlooks, Brazil CONAB, and Argentina Bolsa de Cereales data.
