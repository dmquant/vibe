# Weekly Research Report: April 27 to May 1, 2026

## Priority Conclusions

1. **The Fed did not deliver a simple hawkish pivot; it raised the practical bar for cuts.** On April 29, 2026, the FOMC kept the federal funds target range at **3.50%–3.75%** and retained language about “additional adjustments.” But inflation was described as elevated, and **three voters supported holding rates while objecting to the easing bias in the statement**, showing clear disagreement over forward guidance.
2. **Alphabet was the clearest AI-ROI winner, followed by Microsoft; Amazon is still converting cash flow into AWS capacity.** Alphabet Q1 2026 revenue was **$109.896B**, Google Cloud reached **$20.028B** (about **+63%** year over year), and free cash flow was still **$10.116B** after **$35.674B** of CapEx. Microsoft FY26 Q3 revenue was **$82.886B**, Microsoft Cloud was **$54.5B** (**+29%**), and its AI business had a **$37B** annual revenue run rate (**+123%**), but quarterly CapEx of **$30.876B** was already almost twice quarterly FCF of **$15.803B**.
3. **The planned May 1 payrolls event did not occur.** The BLS release calendar shows the April 2026 Employment Situation is scheduled for **May 8, 2026 at 8:30 ET**. Therefore, there is no valid causal answer to “how did the April payrolls surprise change FedWatch”; the week’s rate-pricing shift came from FOMC communication, oil/Middle East risk, and earnings, not April payrolls.
4. **Sector rotation was not classic defensiveness.** The S&P 500 rose **0.95%** for the week. Defensive sectors were steady: Consumer Staples **+1.08%**, Utilities **+0.89%**, Health Care **+0.96%**. But Communication Services **+4.42%**, Energy **+3.53%**, and Financials **+1.16%** were stronger, while Information Technology rose only **+0.22%**. This was selective risk appetite, not broad risk aversion.
5. **DeepSeek V4 pressured the NVDA valuation story but did not reverse the data-center spending cycle.** DeepSeek V4 was released on **April 24, 2026**; V4-Pro has **1.6T** parameters and a 1M-token context window, with Huawei Ascend compatibility emphasized. NVDA fell from **$208.27** to **$198.45**, a weekly move of **-4.72%**. Yet hyperscaler 2026 CapEx guidance is still rising, so one model release did not directly weaken U.S. data-center demand.

## Question 1: Did the Fed send a clear “higher for longer” signal?

### Method
I compared the April 29, 2026 FOMC statement, Powell’s press-conference transcript, CME FedWatch references, and the 10-year Treasury yield from April 24 to April 30, 2026. The test was whether policy stayed unchanged, whether statement language pointed toward cuts or patience, whether markets pushed cuts out, and whether long yields rose.

### Findings

| Indicator | Value / Change | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---|
| Federal funds target range | **3.50%–3.75%** | Hold |
| Dissenting votes | **4 votes**: 1 for a 25bp cut; 3 against the easing-bias wording | Dispute centered on guidance |
| March unemployment rate cited by Powell | **4.3%** | Labor market softer, not broken |
| March headline/core PCE estimates cited by Powell | **3.5% / 3.2%** | Inflation still above target |
| 10Y Treasury yield | **4.31% → 4.40%** from 2026-04-24 to 2026-04-30 | Long yields rose **+9bp** |
| CME FedWatch year-end hold probability reference | **86%** probability of staying at 3.50%–3.75% through year-end | Market pushed cuts out |

Interpretation: The FOMC did not literally say “higher for longer,” because the statement preserved optionality for “additional adjustments.” But Powell focused on energy prices, tariff-related core inflation, and inflation expectations, while three regional Fed presidents objected to the easing bias in the statement. The 10-year yield rose **9bp** from the pre-meeting reference date to April 30, matching the idea that the hurdle for cuts moved higher. The best conclusion is that the official text remained two-sided, while votes and market pricing created a de facto higher-for-longer signal.

### Evidence
- FOMC statement, April 29, 2026: held the range at 3.50%–3.75% and listed the dissents.[1]
- Powell press conference, April 29, 2026: cited unemployment at **4.3%**, headline PCE at **3.5%**, core PCE at **3.2%**, and emphasized meeting-by-meeting decisions.[2]
- CME FedWatch probability cited by Kiplinger: **86%** probability the range remains 3.50%–3.75% through year-end.[5]
- FRED DGS10: **4.31%** on April 24 and **4.40%** on April 30.[11]

### Caveats
FedWatch is a live tool, so historical snapshots are difficult to audit after the fact; this report uses public references plus the CME tool itself. FRED DGS10 data visible on May 3, 2026 only ran through April 30, not May 1.

## Question 2: Did Microsoft and Alphabet cloud growth offset AI infrastructure spending?

### Method
I used the latest quarterly reports for Microsoft FY26 Q3, Alphabet Q1 2026, Amazon Q1 2026, and Apple FY26 Q2. The comparison basis was revenue, EPS, cloud/services revenue, CapEx, operating cash flow, and free cash flow. FCF is operating cash flow minus property-and-equipment purchases; Amazon’s disclosed sequence is on a trailing-twelve-month basis.

### Findings

| Company | Latest-quarter revenue | EPS | Cloud / AI-related revenue | CapEx / PPE purchases | FCF | Read-through |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| MSFT | **$82.886B** | **$4.27** | Microsoft Cloud **$54.5B** (+29%); AI run rate **$37B** (+123%) | **$30.876B** | **$15.803B** | Revenue supports the story, but cash-flow leverage is rising |
| GOOGL | **$109.896B** | **$5.11** | Google Cloud **$20.028B** (about +63%) | **$35.674B** | **$10.116B** | Strongest ROI evidence |
| AMZN | **$181.519B** | **$2.78** | AWS **$37.587B** (+28%) | TTM **$147.299B** | TTM **$1.232B** | AWS is accelerating, but FCF is squeezed by CapEx |
| AAPL | **$111.184B** | **$2.01** | Services **$30.976B**; no cloud disclosure | Q2 derived **$1.971B** | Q2 derived **$26.731B** | Not a hyperscaler; AI CapEx is not directly comparable |

Interpretation: Alphabet gave the cleanest AI ROI signal this week: Google Cloud reached **$20.028B**, Cloud operating income reached **$6.598B**, and FCF stayed positive despite **$35.674B** of CapEx. Microsoft’s cloud base is larger, and the **$37B** AI run rate is direct revenue evidence, but CapEx rose for three straight quarters and FY26 Q3 CapEx/FCF was about **1.95x**. Amazon’s AWS growth accelerated to **+28%** on a large base, but TTM FCF fell to **$1.232B**, showing how much cash is being absorbed during the buildout. Apple had a strong report, but Services revenue is not proof of hyperscale AI infrastructure ROI.

### Evidence
- Microsoft FY26 Q3: revenue **$82.886B**, EPS **$4.27**, Microsoft Cloud **$54.5B**, Azure +40%, CapEx, and operating cash flow.[6]
- Alphabet Q1 2026: revenue **$109.896B**, Google Cloud **$20.028B**, Cloud operating income **$6.598B**, CapEx **$35.674B**, operating cash flow **$45.790B**.[7]
- Amazon Q1 2026: revenue **$181.519B**, AWS **$37.587B**, TTM CapEx **$147.299B**, TTM FCF **$1.232B**.[8]
- Apple FY26 Q2 and FY26 Q1 financial statements used to derive quarterly operating cash flow, PPE purchases, and FCF.[9][10]

### Caveats
“AI revenue” is not disclosed consistently. Microsoft provides an AI run rate; Alphabet and Amazon mainly disclose cloud revenue plus AI infrastructure demand; Apple has no comparable AI revenue line. Amazon’s CapEx/FCF chart uses TTM values, so it should not be treated as a like-for-like quarterly margin comparison with MSFT, GOOGL, or AAPL.

## Question 3: How did payrolls change CME FedWatch probabilities?

### Method
I checked the BLS official release schedule, the latest available Employment Situation report as of this week (March 2026, released April 3), post-FOMC FedWatch references, and VIX from April 24 to April 30, 2026. If April payrolls were not released, I did not attribute market moves to payrolls.

### Findings

| Item | Value / Status | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---|
| April Employment Situation release time | **May 8, 2026, 8:30 ET** | Outside this week’s window |
| March nonfarm payrolls | **+178,000** | Latest official payroll number |
| March unemployment rate | **4.3%** | Above the plan’s “below 4.0%” assumption |
| March average hourly earnings | **+0.2% m/m, +3.5% y/y, $37.38** | Wage growth cooled but remained sticky |
| January/February combined revision | **-7,000** | Slightly negative revision |
| VIX | **18.71 → 16.89** from 2026-04-24 to 2026-04-30 | Volatility fell |
| FedWatch change attributable to April payrolls | **None** | Event had not occurred |

Interpretation: The direct answer is that the question cannot be answered as stated because April payrolls were not released by May 1. Markets pushed cuts further out during the week, but the drivers were energy prices, Middle East risk, FOMC wording/votes, and large-cap tech earnings, not a payroll surprise. The March jobs report showed resilience without overheating: payrolls rose **178,000**, unemployment was **4.3%**, and average hourly earnings rose **0.2% m/m**. The actual test for current pricing is next week’s May 8 report.

### Evidence
- BLS release schedule: April 2026 Employment Situation is scheduled for **May 8, 2026 at 8:30 AM**.[4]
- BLS March Employment Situation: payrolls **+178,000**, unemployment **4.3%**, average hourly earnings **$37.38**, year-over-year **+3.5%**.[3]
- VIXCLS from FRED through April 30, 2026.[11]

### Caveats
Because the key data release did not happen, any claim that April payrolls moved FedWatch during this week would be a false attribution. This report provides the event gap and a replacement interpretation.

## Question 4: Did defensive sectors outperform growth, signaling risk aversion?

### Method
I used LPL Research/Bloomberg weekly sector performance published May 1, 2026 for the week of April 27. The defensive basket is Utilities, Consumer Staples, and Health Care. The growth basket is Information Technology, Communication Services, and Consumer Discretionary.

### Findings

| Group | Components | Average weekly return | Conclusion |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Defensive basket | Utilities +0.89%; Consumer Staples +1.08%; Health Care +0.96% | **+0.98%** | Roughly in line with the S&P 500’s **+0.95%** |
| Growth basket | Information Technology +0.22%; Communication Services +4.42%; Consumer Discretionary +0.80% | **+1.81%** | Lifted by Communication Services |
| Weekly leaders | Communication Services **+4.42%**, Energy **+3.53%** | Not traditional defensives | Not broad risk aversion |
| Weekly laggards | Materials **-1.51%**, Information Technology **+0.22%** | Hardware/AI chain pressure | Internal tech dispersion |

Interpretation: Defensives did not systematically outperform growth, because Communication Services surged on Alphabet’s earnings. A better description is that investors avoided some high-CapEx or hardware-pressure assets while buying internet/cloud platforms with visible AI revenue conversion, energy, and financials. Staples and utilities were stable, but they did not define the week’s leadership. Breadth improved beyond pure technology, but the tone was not risk-off.

### Evidence
- LPL Weekly Market Performance, May 1, 2026: S&P 500 **+0.95%**, Nasdaq Composite **+1.24%**, and sector returns shown in Chart 1.[12]
- Nasdaq historical API: NDX **27,303.67 → 27,710.36**, weekly gain **+1.49%**; Magnificent 7 returns shown in Chart 5.[13]

### Caveats
Sector returns use Bloomberg/LPL aggregates and were not rebuilt from constituent-level total-return data. Growth/defensive grouping is a research convention, not an official index-provider classification.
