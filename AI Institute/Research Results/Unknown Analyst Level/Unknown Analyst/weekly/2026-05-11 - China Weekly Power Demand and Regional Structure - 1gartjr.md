# China Weekly Power Demand and Regional Structure

Report date: 2026-05-11  
Date anchor: all references to “this week,” “last week,” and “latest” are resolved against the shell date `2026-05-11`.

## Key Conclusions

1. China’s power-demand data remain a “monthly official data plus weekly high-frequency proxy” framework. As of 2026-05-11, the latest official all-society electricity consumption data from the National Energy Administration are for March 2026: 859.5 TWh, up 3.5% year on year; 1Q26 consumption was 2,514.1 TWh, up 5.2%.
2. Converting official monthly data into a weekly run-rate, 1Q26 averaged about 193.4 TWh per week. March alone was about 194.1 TWh per week, below the 196.3 TWh per week implied by January-February, but still positive year on year.
3. Weekly high-frequency proxies show a sharp mid-April rebound in coal-fired generation after a weak first April week. CECI-sampled coal-fired generators’ average daily generation fell 13.7% week on week during April 3-9, then rebounded 12.8% week on week and rose 12.4% year on year during April 10-16. After compounding the two weekly moves, the April 10-16 sample level was still about 2.6% below March 27-April 2, meaning the rebound was material but had not fully restored the late-March level.
4. Regional demand is still tilting toward western China, eastern China, and new-economy loads. In 1Q26, all-society electricity consumption rose 5.5% in the east, 4.1% in central China, 5.6% in the west, and 3.8% in the northeast. Western and eastern China led, while central China and the northeast lagged. High-tech and equipment-manufacturing electricity demand showed a “central-western lead”: west +11.8%, central +10.0%, east +7.9%, northeast +1.3%.
5. The summer risk is not national electricity volume, but regional peak-load balance and inter-regional transfer capacity. CEC expects around 5% growth in 2Q all-society electricity consumption. The National Energy Administration expects China’s 2026 maximum power load to be around 1.575-1.600 TW and possibly above 1.600 TW under extreme heat. Parts of eastern, central, and southern grids may be tight during peak hours; northern and northeastern grids are expected to be balanced, while northwestern China should have surplus power.

## Data Scope

| Indicator | Available frequency | Use in this report | Main limitation |
|---|---:|---|---|
| All-society electricity consumption | Monthly | Official demand benchmark; converted into weekly run-rate from monthly daily averages | As of 2026-05-11, public official data are available only through March 2026 |
| Above-scale industrial power generation | Monthly | Supply-side cross-check for generation and structure | Excludes some distributed and smaller-scale entities, so it is not the same as all-society demand |
| CECI coal-fired generator sample | Weekly | High-frequency proxy for marginal power demand and regional changes | Coal-biased sample; affected by hydro, wind, solar, maintenance, and heating demand; not a direct measure of total electricity consumption |

## Weekly High-Frequency Proxy: Marginal Change in Coal-Fired Generation

| Period | Sample indicator | National WoW | National YoY | Regional feature |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| 2026-03-27 to 2026-04-02 | CECI-sampled coal-fired generators’ average daily generation | -3.2% | +3.9% | Eastern and central China fell 8.5% and 6.7% WoW, respectively |
| 2026-04-03 to 2026-04-09 | Same | -13.7% | -4.5% | Northwest, north, and northeast fell 21.7%, 17.5%, and 16.4% WoW, respectively |
| 2026-04-10 to 2026-04-16 | Same | +12.8% | +12.4% | East, south, northeast, and north rose 18.6%, 16.0%, 14.2%, and 11.6% WoW, respectively |

Interpretation: sampled coal-fired output dropped sharply in the first April week and then rebounded quickly. Because the 12.8% rebound during April 10-16 came after a 13.7% fall, the compounded two-week level was still about 2.6% below March 27-April 2. This looks more like a shoulder-season catch-up than the start of a sustained load upslope.

## Regional Structure: West and East Stronger, Central and Northeast Softer

| Region | 1Q all-society electricity consumption YoY | High-tech and equipment-manufacturing electricity YoY | Structural meaning |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| East | +5.5% | +7.9% | Services, export-oriented manufacturing, and digital loads remain supportive, but the east is no longer the sole growth leader |
| Central | +4.1% | +10.0% | Aggregate growth is middling to soft, but high-tech manufacturing improved quickly |
| West | +5.6% | +11.8% | Both total demand and advanced manufacturing lead, supported by renewable manufacturing, resource processing, computing loads, and transmission corridors |
| Northeast | +3.8% | +1.3% | Both total demand and emerging manufacturing loads are weak, with traditional industry and weather effects more visible |

At the provincial level, the 1Q growth leaders were Tibet +13.5%, Hainan +9.1%, Jilin +8.9%, Guangxi +8.9%, Xinjiang +8.1%, Guangdong +7.6%, Yunnan +7.4%, Inner Mongolia +7.2%, and Hubei +7.1%. Low-growth provinces and municipalities included Shaanxi +1.2%, Chongqing +1.3%, Shanxi +1.9%, Liaoning +2.4%, and Heilongjiang +2.9%. The split shows that marginal demand elasticity is coming from two types of regions: composite manufacturing-and-services provinces such as Guangdong and Hubei, and resource, green-power, and industrial-transfer recipients such as Xinjiang, Inner Mongolia, Yunnan, and Guangxi.

| Sector or segment | 1Q26 data | Demand impact |
|---|---:|---|
| Secondary industry | 1,598.7 TWh, +4.7% | Still the main contributor to incremental electricity demand |
| Tertiary industry | 483.3 TWh, +8.1% | Faster than total demand, with a 29.4% contribution rate |
| Urban and rural residential use | 398.5 TWh, +3.4% | Stable growth, but not the core source of the current increase |
| High-tech and equipment manufacturing | 274.6 TWh, +8.6% | Manufacturing upgrading offsets some weakness in property-chain and high-energy-consuming segments |
| Charging and battery-swapping services | 37.6 TWh, +53.8% | EV parc growth and charging frequency are lifting load |
| Internet data services | 22.9 TWh, +44.0% | Computing-related load continues to grow rapidly |

Charging and battery-swapping services plus internet data services together lifted all-society electricity consumption growth by 0.8 percentage points. This is the main structural signal in 1Q26: demand growth is shifting away from traditional high-energy-consuming expansion and toward advanced manufacturing, transport electrification, and data-center loads.

## Supply and Regional Balance Implications

The supply side is not creating a nationwide constraint. In 1Q26, above-scale industrial power generation reached 2,378.2 TWh, up 3.4% year on year. In March alone, above-scale industrial power generation was 802.5 TWh, up 1.4%, or 25.89 TWh per day. On the renewable side, 1Q renewable generation reached 882.9 TWh, about 37.1% of total generation; wind and solar generation together reached about 580.9 TWh, accounting for more than 23% of all-society electricity consumption.

The main tracking issue is the mismatch between regional peaks and renewable output. The National Energy Administration’s May wind and solar outlook indicates that 100-meter wind speeds are expected to be below normal in eastern, central, southern, and eastern-southwestern China, while horizontal irradiance is expected to be below normal in south-central north China, eastern China, and central China. These areas overlap partly with the regions flagged for possible summer tightness, so spot power prices, inter-provincial trading, and coal-fired unit utilization should be watched from May onward.

## Follow-Up Items

1. April all-society electricity consumption: confirm whether March’s slower growth was seasonal noise or the start of a softer 2Q slope.
2. CECI weekly sample: check whether late-April to early-May coal-fired daily generation can regain and hold the late-March level.
3. Regional spot power prices: focus on Guangdong, Jiangsu, Shanghai, Shanxi, and Inner Mongolia to see whether tightness expectations in eastern, central, and southern regions are entering prices.
4. Summer peak load: monitor whether the 1.575-1.600 TW maximum-load range is revised upward ahead of time.
5. New load categories: track whether charging and battery-swapping, internet data services, and high-tech manufacturing continue to contribute more than 1 percentage point to electricity-demand growth.
