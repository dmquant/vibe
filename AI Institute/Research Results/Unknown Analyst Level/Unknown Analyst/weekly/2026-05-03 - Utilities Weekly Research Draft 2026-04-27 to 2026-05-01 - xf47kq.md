## Q1: Which Power Companies Have the Most Stable Unit Gross Margin?

### Method

For 2026Q1, I calculated a “proxy gross profit per kWh” as `(operating revenue - operating cost) / on-grid power volume`. The sample prioritizes companies with disclosed Q1 on-grid power volume: Huaneng Power, Huadian Power, GD Power, Zheneng Power, and Huaneng Hydropower. I then cross-checked earnings quality through recurring net profit growth and operating cash-flow growth. The comparison basis is 2025Q1 YoY growth and 2026Q1 peer ranking.

| Zheneng Power | 600023 | Mainly thermal | 378.86 | -0.02% | -0.000 | -3.37% | +21.23% |

Hydro has the most stable unit gross margin and cash-flow quality because it has little direct fuel-cost exposure; water inflow and utilization hours are the main variables. Thermal power is clearly split internally: Huaneng Power has the highest proxy unit gross profit, but recurring profit and cash flow still declined YoY, showing that lower tariffs, lower utilization hours, and fuel costs continue to cap profit elasticity. Huadian Power and GD Power have thinner unit margins, with operating cash flow down -48.11% and -36.88% YoY, respectively, making them more sensitive to marginal coal-price and tariff changes. Zheneng Power’s Q1 gross margin was near zero; despite +21.23% operating cash-flow growth, its operating profit quality lagged peers.

### Caveats

The proxy gross profit per kWh is not an official company disclosure. ## Q2: Is Active GEC Trading a Short-Term Policy Rush or Stable Industrial Demand?

### Method

I prioritized public disclosures from the National Energy Administration and the national GEC platform. Daily GEC transaction details for the target week were not publicly verifiable, so I used March 2026 official monthly data as the nearest complete sample and separated standalone GEC trades from GECs linked to green-power transactions. The comparison basis is Q1 cumulative trading of 240mn certificates and the March transaction mix.

### Findings

The conclusion is that demand has formed, but trading rhythm is still heavily affected by policy deadlines. In March, 2025 production-year certificates accounted for 22.28mn certificates, or 74.6% of standalone trades, at an average price of RMB 5.95/certificate, indicating that companies prefer recent-vintage certificates usable for near-term green-consumption accounting. 2026 production-year certificates traded at RMB 7.76/certificate, above 2025-vintage certificates, showing a freshness premium, but their volume was only 0.95mn certificates, so depth remains limited. Because the reported April 28 “5mn certificates” daily number could not be verified, the visualization stage should use official monthly data rather than unsupported daily claims.

## Q3: Which Provincial Spot Markets Offer Better VPP Spread Revenue?

### Method

I split VPP revenue into three buckets: spot power spread, demand-response/peak-shaving compensation, and capacity/platform rewards. I compared public price caps or compensation standards for Guangdong, Shandong, Shanxi, Gansu, the Yangtze River Delta, Shanghai, and Zhejiang. “Theoretical spread = price cap - price floor” is used for ranking, with separate caveats on actual spot-volatility frequency, entry requirements, and restrictions on double compensation.

### Findings

| Region | Verifiable Price/Compensation Reference | Theoretical Revenue Space | Conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Guangdong | Spot floor -50 RMB/MWh and cap 1,800 RMB/MWh; Guangzhou active demand-response subsidy up to RMB 3.5/kWh | Spot spread 1,850 RMB/MWh; demand response 3,500 RMB/MWh | Highest commercialization torque; watch load-side VPP market-entry rules |
| Shandong | Public references show spot cap of RMB 1.5/kWh; negative prices have occurred repeatedly | About 1,500 RMB/MWh or more, depending on the floor | High renewable penetration; suitable for storage plus interruptible-load aggregation |
| Shanxi | Some rules show cap 764.93 RMB/MWh and floor 95.62 RMB/MWh; another national comparison reference shows a 1.5 RMB/kWh cap | 669 to 1,500 RMB/MWh | Coal-power benchmark is strong; spread is below Guangdong but the mechanism is mature |
| Gansu | Spot cap 650 RMB/MWh and floor 40 RMB/MWh | 610 RMB/MWh | Strong renewable-consumption angle; many low-price charging windows but lower cap |
| Yangtze River Delta | Peak-shaving compensation for VPP and adjustable load at 800 RMB/MWh; storage peak-shaving compensation at 160 RMB/MWh | 160 to 800 RMB/MWh | Auxiliary-service rules are clearer, but double-compensation limits are stricter |
| Shanghai | VPP resource aggregation platform reward of 50 RMB/kW-year, capped at RMB 1.5mn/year per platform | Mainly capacity reward | Better for platform operators; less suitable as a high-frequency spread-arbitrage market |
| Zhejiang | Market-response positive-adjustment price caps reported in the 800 to 1,200 RMB/MW range | Needs event- and capacity-based conversion | Trial-stage rules; useful as a policy-tracking sample |

Guangdong is the top province to track for this VPP commercialization cycle because both the spot cap and floor have been widened, and the province has strong peak-load, distributed-resource, and commercial/industrial customer foundations. Shandong’s value comes from frequent low-price and negative-price opportunities caused by high renewable penetration, making it suitable for storage plus interruptible-load portfolios. Shanxi and Gansu are more mature but have lower upside caps, so they lean toward steady arbitrage and auxiliary-service income. The Yangtze River Delta and Shanghai offer more visible platform and auxiliary-service revenue, but lower elasticity than Guangdong spot spreads.

### Caveats

Theoretical spread is not realizable profit. VPPs must meet entry, communication, baseline, response-assessment, and settlement requirements. Many local rules prohibit receiving demand-response, auxiliary-service, and spot-market compensation for the same time interval. Some provinces publish bid caps rather than actual clearing-price distributions; real tradable spreads and frequency can be far below theoretical values.

## Q4: How Much Valuation Upside Could Water-Tariff Linkage Create?

### Method

I used Shenzhen’s 13.05% comprehensive water-tariff increase proposal as a megacity tariff-reform benchmark, then separated the impact on water operators from the impact on design/consulting firms. For operators, I estimated profit sensitivity using water-supply revenue exposure and EBITDA operating leverage. For Shenzhen Water Planning & Design Institute and similar firms, I estimated order and revenue sensitivity rather than direct tariff sensitivity.

### Findings

| Company Type | Representative Companies | Observable Benchmark | Estimated Sensitivity | Valuation Meaning |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| Local water/wastewater operators | Xingrong Environment 000598, Chongqing Water 601158, Jiangnan Water 601199 | If end-user comprehensive tariff rises +13.05% | EBITDA about +4% to +9% | Fair valuation midpoint could rise about 0.5 to 1.0x EV/EBITDA |
| Integrated environmental operators | Grandblue Environment 600323, Zhongshan Public Utilities 000685 | Lower water-revenue exposure than pure water utilities | EBITDA about +2% to +6% | More about earnings stability than a primary valuation driver |
| Water design/consulting | Shenzhen Water Planning & Design Institute 301038 | 2026Q1 revenue RMB 83mn; recurring net loss RMB 34mn | If pipeline inspection/smart-water orders recover, revenue sensitivity can exceed tariff sensitivity | Valuation depends on order conversion and expense-ratio improvement |
| Hong Kong/Shenzhen water-related assets | Guangdong Investment and peers | Hong Kong water-supply agreement capped revenue of about HKD 5.18-5.26bn/5.32-5.39bn for 2025/2026 | Strong revenue stability | More of a dividend-asset pricing story |

The core of water-tariff reform is not “price hikes,” but a return to “allowed cost plus reasonable return.” For operators, if tariff-linkage mechanisms cover depreciation, power cost, labor cost, pipeline renewal, and reasonable profit, discounted-cash-flow visibility improves and valuation midpoints can move modestly higher. For design firms such as Shenzhen Water Planning & Design Institute, tariff increases do not directly create profit, but if the pricing mechanism supports pipeline upgrades, integrated plant-network operations, and smart-water investment, order recovery can be more meaningful. I could not find an official same-day announcement for the 2026-04-30 “Shenzhen-Hong Kong water-asset pricing mechanism meeting,” so this report treats it as a thematic water-pricing catalyst rather than a confirmed policy event.

Design-firm valuation sensitivity depends more on orders, collections, and expense control than on water tariffs alone.
