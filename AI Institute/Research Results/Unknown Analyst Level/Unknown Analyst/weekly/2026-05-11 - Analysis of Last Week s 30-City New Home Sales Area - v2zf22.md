# Analysis of Last Week's 30-City New Home Sales Area

## Key Conclusions

This report anchors all relative dates to the local shell date, `2026-05-11`; “last week” refers to `2026-05-04` through `2026-05-10`.

New home transactions in the 30 large and medium-sized cities remained in a weak recovery pattern last week. Public high-frequency tracking shows that the average daily commodity-home transaction area in the 30 cities fell `10.9%` week on week and rose slightly by `0.3%` year on year. Because the current public text discloses the daily week-on-week and year-on-year changes but not the absolute weekly transaction area, this report derives the weekly level from the comparable 2025 week of `1.53 million square meters` and the current `+0.3%` year-on-year growth rate. The implied weekly transaction area was about `1.535 million square meters`, or roughly `219,000 square meters` per day.

The city-tier split was uneven. Tier-1 cities remained the main support, Tier-2 cities weakened, and Tier-3 cities posted a small positive reading: Tier-1 cities were up `10.4%` year on year, Tier-2 cities were down `7.6%`, and Tier-3 cities were up `3.8%`. This points to a non-uniform recovery, with core-city resilience offsetting weaker Tier-2 demand.

## Assessment

Tier-1 cities still show resilience, but the market has moved from the more visible April improvement to a cooler sequential reading. China Post Securities' weekly tracking for `2026-04-20` to `2026-04-26` showed 30-city new home transaction area of `1.7974 million sq m`, with a four-week average of `1.7542 million sq m`; the Tier-1 four-week average was up `36.1%` year on year. That confirms a stronger core-city recovery in late April.

Last week's estimated `1.535 million sq m` was below the late-April weekly level, mainly reflecting a post-holiday slowdown. Since the year-on-year reading was still slightly positive, this should not be read as a renewed demand stall. The better characterization is “low-level stabilization, sharp structural divergence, and core cities outperforming non-core cities.”

The weakest point is the Tier-2 reading of `-7.6%` year on year. If Tier-2 sales do not recover in mid-to-late May, developers' sell-through and launch confidence will remain dependent on Tier-1 and a few strong Tier-2 cities, and any call for a broad sector recovery would need to be toned down.
