# Real Estate Weekly Research: 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-01

## Priority Conclusions

One sentence: the market is not in a broad-based turnaround; it is a structural recovery led by core cities and high-quality assets while weaker segments remain in a bottoming phase. The keywords are **Silver April extension, point-specific land heat, policy easing, REITs correction**.

### Method

I used CRIC and China Index Academy data released from April 30 to May 1, 2026, comparing cumulative YoY, April single-month YoY, and MoM performance for Top 100, Top 50, and attributable sales. Demand structure was cross-checked with CRIC’s key-city transaction data and hot-project examples. The windows are April 2026 and January-April 2026.

April was not a volume-level reversal: Top 100 managed sales fell 3.9% MoM from March, so “Silver April” did not beat the absolute strength of March’s mini spring. But the YoY decline narrowed in both cumulative and single-month terms, with Top 100 monthly managed sales down only 8.9% YoY versus the Jan-Apr cumulative decline of 19.7%. The demand mix skewed toward upgrade demand and core assets: Shenzhen’s CR Land-CMSK Guanchao Fu sold 95% of 210 launched units on April 26 and booked RMB 6.0bn in one day; Shanghai Yunqi Binjiang recorded a 119% valid subscription rate; Hangzhou Binjiang Jindi Aoying Shiji sold over 94% of 86 first-launch units. Basic demand is not absent, but it is mainly visible in value-for-money near-suburban upgrade projects, such as Beijing CSCI Guoxianfu PARK, which had 335 units registered within one month of launch and an online-registration conversion rate close to 72%; far-suburban projects generally have sell-through cycles above 150 days and still carry inventory pressure.

## Question 2: Uneven Land-Market Heat

### Method

I used Hangzhou’s April 28, 2026 residential land auction as the week’s key sample, and the April land-market monthly report as the national backdrop for transaction value, average premium rate, and failed-auction rate. I compared plot location, premium rate, winning bidder type, and consortium participation.

The key land-market issue is not whether the market is hot, but that only high-certainty assets are hot. In Hangzhou’s five-plot auction, the Liangzhu East plot’s 43.33% premium pulled up the average, while the other plots were reserve-price or low-premium deals; developers are willing to pay for scarcity but not for ordinary submarkets with inventory risk. Competition has also shifted toward consortium bidding: 3 of the 5 plots were won by consortia, indicating that developers are still sharing funding, development, and sales risk. Nationally, operating land transaction value fell 41% YoY in April while the average premium rate rose to 7.5%, suggesting that weaker volume is being offset by a higher share of quality plots.

Land-market heat usually leads sales realization by 3-6 months and should not be directly translated into near-term profit recovery.

## Question 3: Policy Transmission Efficiency

### Method

I reviewed the number of April local policy announcements, the April 28 Politburo wording, Shenzhen’s April 29 policy, and Wuhan’s April 30 policy. I then used CRIC’s 50-city new-home and 20-city secondary-home transaction data to evaluate transmission, comparing YoY, MoM, and city-tier divergence.

### Findings

| Policy/transaction indicator | Date/window | Figure or content | Transmission read-through |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Local stabilization policy frequency | April 2026 as of publication | 74 announcements; housing provident fund optimization was mentioned most, followed by urban renewal and housing security | Policy density is high, but tools are mostly structural. |
| Politburo meeting | 2026-04-28 | “Strive to stabilize the real estate market and solidly advance urban renewal” | Stabilization and urban renewal are paired, reinforcing expectations for local easing. |
| Shenzhen policy | Published 2026-04-29, effective 2026-04-30 | Eligible households may buy one additional commodity home in Futian, Nanshan, and Bao’an Xin’an; provident fund loan cap is RMB 1.89mn for individuals and RMB 3.51mn for households | Directly targets core-area upgrade demand and high-payment-capacity buyers. |
| Wuhan policy | Published 2026-04-30 | Existing-home provident fund loan ratio up to 80% of total price for first homes and 70% for second homes; selected areas offer 1.5% subsidy for first homes and 1% for second homes | Supports basic demand, upgrade demand, and outer-area destocking. |
| 50-city new-home transactions | April 2026 | 12.78mn sqm, slight MoM decline, roughly flat YoY | New-home transmission is slower and depends on quality new supply. |
| 20-city secondary-home transactions | April 2026 | 17.93mn sqm, flat MoM, YoY +17%; Jan-Apr cumulative +8% | Secondary homes have already shown volume recovery. |
| Four Tier 1 cities new homes | April 2026 | 1.87mn sqm, YoY +1% | Tier 1 cities turned positive, helped by policy and high-end project supply. |

Policy is translating into transactions, but efficiency is “secondary faster than new homes, core cities faster than weaker cities.” The 17% YoY increase in secondary-home transactions shows that price adjustment, credit optimization, and replacement demand are already being released; new-home transactions are only roughly flat YoY, indicating buyers remain highly selective. Shenzhen’s policy concentrates purchase-rule easing in Futian, Nanshan, and Bao’an Xin’an, and pairs it with a large provident fund loan increase, clearly aiming to bring upgrade demand into high-certainty assets. Wuhan’s policy is more inclusive and destocking-oriented, using existing-home loan ratios, purchase subsidies, and regional incentives to lower the entry threshold; whether it produces sustained online registration improvement must be verified with May-June high-frequency data.

The core change in C-REITs this week was “active primary-market expansion, rising secondary-market digestion pressure.” During April 25-May 1, the total return index fell 1.3%, and average daily traded value dropped from RMB 660mn to RMB 450mn, indicating weaker chasing after the quarterly-report trade. Yield attractiveness still needs to be judged by asset type: concession-right REITs still carry a sizeable spread over 10Y government bonds, but property-right REIT dividend yields are not cheap versus dividend equities. Rental housing and consumption REITs showed relatively stable Q1 operations, but industrial parks and warehouse/logistics assets still face rent and occupancy pressure; the “income asset” narrative cannot replace bottom-up operating review.
