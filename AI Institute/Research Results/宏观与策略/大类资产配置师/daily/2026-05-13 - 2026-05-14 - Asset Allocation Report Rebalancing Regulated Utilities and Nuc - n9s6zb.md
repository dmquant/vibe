# 2026-05-14 - Asset Allocation Report: Rebalancing Regulated Utilities and Nuclear Capacity Assets

**Date:** 2026-05-14, Asia/Singapore  
**Analyst:** Asset Allocator  
**Board ID:** f200f46d-3539-4f3a-92a1-f4b2e06cbaf0 | **Card:** 05/08  
**Stance:** Support, with portfolio-level risk controls

As of 2026-05-14, I support the prior line of reasoning: nuclear IPPs are no longer just merchant-power cyclicals. They have become scarce, carbon-free, high-load-factor capacity assets with direct AI infrastructure optionality. The portfolio conclusion is not to abandon regulated utilities, but to split the old utility sleeve into two distinct risk factors: **regulated-grid stability** and **nuclear-capacity scarcity**.

## 1. Core Judgment

In an all-weather portfolio, nuclear capacity should move from a satellite exposure to a core real-asset equity factor, while regulated utilities should be reduced from the dominant defensive equity proxy to a smaller stabilizer position.

The recommended 100-unit ex-ante portfolio risk budget is:

| Sleeve | Old risk budget | New risk budget | Illustrative nominal weight | Portfolio role |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Regulated utilities | 6.0-7.0 units | 3.5-4.5 units | 4.0-5.0% NAV | Defensive equity, dividend carry, rate-base inflation pass-through |
| Nuclear capacity IPPs | 0.5-1.0 units | 4.0-5.0 units | 2.5-3.5% NAV | Scarce 24/7 clean capacity, AI-power hedge, merchant upside |
| Grid equipment and transmission enablers | 0.5 unit | 1.0-1.5 units | 1.0-2.0% NAV | Picks-and-shovels exposure to the residual-grid capex cycle |
| Uranium and fuel-cycle optionality | 0.0 unit | 0.5-1.0 unit | 0.5-1.0% NAV | Convex supply-chain hedge, not a defensive asset |
| Total power-reliability sleeve | 7.0-8.5 units | 9.0-11.0 units | 8.0-10.0% NAV | Structural hedge against AI-driven power scarcity |

Within the power-reliability sleeve, the new target is roughly **40% regulated utilities, 45% nuclear capacity IPPs, and 15% grid or fuel-cycle enablers by risk contribution**. By nominal capital, that usually translates closer to **50% regulated utilities, 35% nuclear IPPs, and 15% enablers**, because nuclear IPPs carry materially higher equity volatility than regulated utilities.

## 2. Why The Risk Factor Has Changed

Three developments justify a higher nuclear-capacity risk weight.

First, demand growth is now a macro variable, not a niche utility footnote. The IEA's **Electricity 2026** outlook projects global electricity demand growth of **3.6% per year over 2026-2030**, driven by electrification, industry, cooling and data centers. Its **Energy and AI** work states that data-center electricity demand could rise to about **945 TWh by 2030**, with data centers reaching roughly **3% of global electricity demand**. This shifts power availability from an operating input into a growth constraint for AI infrastructure. Sources: [IEA Electricity 2026](https://www.iea.org/reports/electricity-2026/executive-summary), [IEA Energy and AI](https://www.iea.org/topics/artificial-intelligence).

Second, signed nuclear-linked AI contracts have converted optionality into cash-flow visibility. Constellation and Microsoft announced a **20-year** agreement on **2024-09-20** to support the restart of the **835 MW** Crane Clean Energy Center. Talen expanded its Amazon relationship on **2025-06-11**, with a PPA for up to **1,920 MW** of carbon-free nuclear power through **2042**. Vistra and Meta announced on **2026-01-09** **20-year** PPAs for more than **2,600 MW** of zero-carbon energy from Vistra nuclear plants. Sources: [Constellation/Microsoft](https://investors.constellationenergy.com/news-releases/news-release-details/constellation-launch-crane-clean-energy-center-restoring-jobs/), [Talen/Amazon](https://ir.talenenergy.com/node/8676/html), [Vistra/Meta](https://investor.vistracorp.com/2026-01-09-Vistra-and-Meta-Announce-Agreements-to-Support-Nuclear-Plants-in-PJM-and-Add-New-Nuclear-Generation-to-the-Grid).

Third, regulation is moving from ambiguity toward a tariff-defined co-location regime. FERC rejected the contested Susquehanna co-location amendment on **2024-11-01**, then directed PJM on **2025-12-18** to create clearer rules for co-located large loads while protecting reliability and consumers. FERC also approved broader reliability-safeguard actions on **2026-03-19**. This does not eliminate regulatory risk, but it makes nuclear capacity more financeable because future projects can price grid-support charges, backup obligations and interconnection terms explicitly. Sources: [FERC PJM co-location fact sheet](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/fact-sheet-ferc-directs-nations-largest-grid-operator-create-new-rules-embrace), [FERC March 2026 reliability actions](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-action-new-reliability-safeguards-american-power-grid), [NERC 2024 LTRA](https://www.nerc.com/news/Pages/Statement-on-NERC%E2%80%99s-2024-Long-Term-Reliability-Assessment.aspx).

## 3. Portfolio Construction

The correct structure is a barbell, not a full rotation.

**Regulated utilities remain the recession and duration hedge.** They still belong in an all-weather portfolio because allowed returns, dividend carry and rate-base growth help in slower-growth or falling-rate environments. But they should no longer absorb the entire power sleeve. Nuclear baseload migration and customer-specific tariffs mean some grid costs are socialized, creating regulatory-lag risk and ratepayer backlash.

**Nuclear IPPs become the scarcity-growth leg.** CEG, VST and TLN-type exposures now hedge a risk that hyperscalers cannot diversify away: time-to-power. They should be funded from growth equity, low-quality cyclicals and part of the traditional utility sleeve, not from Treasury duration. The position should be implemented as a basket because single-asset outage, refueling, contract and FERC risks are too concentrated.

**Grid equipment is the middle factor.** Transformers, HVDC, switchgear, grid software and thermal management suppliers can benefit whether AI load is served behind the meter or through the public grid. This sleeve has less direct commodity upside than IPPs, but it also has lower single-plant risk.

**Uranium is optionality, not ballast.** A 0.5-1.0% NAV position can protect against a fuel-cycle squeeze, but it should not be counted as defensive because it can behave like a high-beta commodity equity during liquidity shocks.

## 4. Risk Controls

The higher nuclear allocation should be governed by four rules.

| Rule | Implementation |
| --- | --- |
| Volatility cap | Keep nuclear IPP contribution at **4.0-5.0 risk units**; do not let price momentum lift it above **6.0 units** without trimming. |
| Single-name cap | Limit any one nuclear IPP to **1.0-1.25% NAV** unless the position is hedged with options or paired against AI beneficiaries. |
| Regulatory stop | Reduce nuclear IPP exposure by one-third if co-location tariffs impose a grid-support charge above **$25/MWh** without contract pass-through. |
| Diversifier test | Maintain at least **3.5 risk units** in regulated utilities or regulated infrastructure so the sleeve does not become a pure AI-beta trade. |

## 5. Regime Matrix

| Macro and power regime | Regulated utilities | Nuclear capacity IPPs | Allocation action |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| AI capex boom, power tight, real rates stable | Neutral to modest positive | Strong positive | Hold the 45% nuclear risk share and rebalance gains monthly |
| Recession or AI capex pause, rates falling | Positive | Negative to neutral | Rotate 1.0 risk unit from nuclear IPPs back to regulated utilities |
| Higher real rates, tariff pressure, ratepayer backlash | Negative | Mixed | Cut total power sleeve to 8.0 risk units and keep only contracted nuclear exposure |
| FERC/PJM tariff clarity plus signed PPA acceleration | Neutral | Positive | Add up to 1.0 risk unit to nuclear IPPs, funded from broad growth equity |
| Plant outage, refueling delay or counterparty renegotiation | Mildly positive | Negative | Enforce single-name caps and shift to grid equipment until event risk clears |

## 6. Answer To The Mission

For an all-weather portfolio, the rebalance should be explicit:

1. Lower regulated utilities from **6.0-7.0** to **3.5-4.5** units of total portfolio risk.
2. Raise nuclear capacity IPPs from **0.5-1.0** to **4.0-5.0** units of total portfolio risk.
3. Add **1.5-2.5** units across grid equipment and uranium/fuel-cycle optionality.
4. Keep the total power-reliability sleeve capped at **9.0-11.0** risk units, or **8.0-10.0% NAV** in typical nominal implementation.

This turns the old utility allocation from a single defensive dividend trade into a two-factor hedge: regulated utilities protect the portfolio in slow growth and falling rates, while nuclear capacity protects against the new macro risk that AI growth is bottlenecked by reliable power rather than GPUs alone.
