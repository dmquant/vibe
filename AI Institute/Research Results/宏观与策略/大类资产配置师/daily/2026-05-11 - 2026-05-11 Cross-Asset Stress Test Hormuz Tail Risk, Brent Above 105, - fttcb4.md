# 2026-05-11 Cross-Asset Stress Test: Hormuz Tail Risk, Brent Above $105, and 10-Year U.S. Treasury at 4.50%-4.75%

As of the institute work-date **2026-05-11** (Asia/Singapore), this card **stress-tests and partly supports** Card 1: Hormuz risk is still not fully paid in across risk assets, but the portfolio hedge cannot be the classic "buy long Treasuries" answer. If Brent holds above $105 into June while the 10-year U.S. Treasury retests 4.50%-4.75%, the shock is inflationary first and recessionary only later. Compensation is strongest in direct energy exposure, energy cash-flow equities, short real duration, gold with position limits, and FX baskets that favor USD/oil exporters against energy-importing currencies.

## Starting Point and Stress Map

Card 1 argued that oil, rates, and FX were underpricing the Hormuz tail. I agree with the direction, but the allocation test is stricter: an exposure must work when **oil rises and nominal yields rise at the same time**. That rules out large unhedged 10-year/30-year nominal duration, broad equity beta, and "JPY as generic safe haven" positions.

Key facts anchoring the stress:

The relevant stress is not a one-day oil spike. It is a two-to-six-week regime in which energy inventories are consumed, inflation breakevens remain sticky, central-bank easing optionality is constrained, and energy importers face a terms-of-trade shock.

## What Still Pays Enough

| Sleeve | Preferred configuration | Why it still compensates |
|---|---|---|
| Equities | **Overweight global energy cash flows**: U.S./Canada integrated majors, low-leverage E&Ps, midstream, selected LNG/export infrastructure; modest defense and regulated utilities with pass-through pricing. Underweight airlines, cruise, autos, chemicals, EM Asia refiners, Europe/Japan cyclicals, and high-duration growth. | Energy equities are imperfect hedges but have earnings convexity when Brent stays above $105. Midstream and integrated majors reduce single-well and financing risk. Defense/utilities help if the shock broadens into security spending and regulated cash-flow preference. |
| Duration | **T-bills, floating-rate notes, 0-2y nominal duration, and 0-5y TIPS**. Avoid unhedged 10-year/30-year nominal duration until the 10-year is closer to 4.75% or oil momentum breaks. | If 10-year yields move from 4.38% to 4.75%, a 10-year Treasury with roughly 8-year duration loses about **3%** before carry; a 30-year exposure can lose roughly **6%**. Short TIPS keep inflation linkage while limiting real-duration drawdown. |
| Commodities | **Direct Brent/WTI call spreads or futures with stop discipline**, plus refined-product exposure where liquidity permits; **gold 5%-8%** as political-risk collateral, not as the only hedge. Avoid industrial-metal beta as the primary hedge. | Direct energy exposure is the cleanest positive beta to the scenario. Gold can work when geopolitical risk rises, but high real yields cap its reliability. Copper/aluminum may rise on supply risk but are exposed to demand destruction. |
| FX | **Long USD vs EUR, JPY, KRW, INR, and CNY proxy baskets; long CAD vs EUR/JPY; selective NOK only when European gas stress is contained.** CHF can be held as a low-beta risk-off diversifier but is less directly linked to oil. | USD gains from risk aversion, U.S. energy relative insulation, and high front-end rates. CAD has cleaner North American energy beta than NOK when Europe is under gas stress. JPY is not a clean haven in an imported-energy inflation shock. |
| Volatility and hedges | **Equity index put spreads financed by selling lower-probability downside, oil call spreads, and receiver swaptions only after the rate spike matures.** | Optionality is better than static beta when oil and yields rise together. But bond receivers should be staged, not front-loaded, because the first leg is rate-negative. |

## Model Portfolio Tilt Versus a 60/40 Benchmark

For a USD-based balanced mandate, I would not recommend abandoning risk assets entirely. I would recommend a **barbell between energy/inflation beneficiaries and liquidity**, funded by nominal duration and import-sensitive equity beta:

| Allocation action | Suggested active weight | Implementation notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Add direct energy commodity exposure | **+5% to +8%** | Brent/WTI call spreads or futures; cap sizing because gap risk cuts both ways if diplomacy improves. |
| Add energy equities and infrastructure | **+8% to +12%** | Favor balance sheets, free-cash-flow yield, and dividends over levered exploration beta. |
| Add gold | **+4% to +6%** | Useful as sanction/geopolitical collateral; do not rely on it to offset every rate-up day. |
| Add short TIPS / T-bills / FRNs | **+15% to +25%** | Replace intermediate and long nominal bonds; keep dry powder for post-spike recession risk. |
| Cut 7-30y nominal duration | **-15% to -25%** | The rate path in the mission statement makes long bonds a drawdown source, not a hedge. |
| Cut broad developed-market cyclicals and Asia import beta | **-8% to -15%** | Focus on airlines, transport, chemicals, autos, consumer discretionary, and energy-importing current-account losers. |
| Add FX shock basket | **+5% to +10% notional risk budget** | Long USD/CAD against EUR/JPY/KRW/INR/CNY proxy; rebalance if Brent falls below $95 or 10-year yields retreat below 4.25%. |

This mix is designed to survive the joint move: oil up, yields up, equities lower, USD firmer. The most important risk-control rule is that the hedge budget should not be spent on assets that need yields to fall immediately.

| **De-escalation:** Brent below $95, 10-year below 4.25% | Broad equities, long duration, importers, JPY relief | Energy futures, oil-exporter FX, gold | Take profits on energy beta; rebuild intermediate duration gradually. |

## Bottom Line

The adequately compensated portfolio is **not** a conventional risk-parity book. Classic risk parity assumes bonds diversify equities during shocks; this Hormuz path first attacks bonds through inflation and term premium. The right configuration is an **inflation-shock barbell**: direct energy upside, energy cash-flow equities, short real/nominal duration, measured gold, and USD/CAD FX hedges against energy-importer currencies.

My preferred handoff is to **fx-strategist** for a focused currency basket calibration: the next unresolved question is whether USD/CAD longs against EUR/JPY/KRW/INR/CNY proxy offer better payoff than simply holding USD cash once oil stays above $105 and the U.S. 10-year pushes toward 4.75%.
