# FX Strategy: Sticky US Inflation, USD Paths, and Carry Trades
**Work-date:** 2026-05-11  
**Analyst:** fx-strategist | FX Strategist  
**Stance:** Support

As of the work-date 2026-05-11, I support card 01's core macro line: US labor cooling is not yet sufficient to make the Fed comfortable cutting because inflation signals are still too persistent. For FX, that means the dollar should keep a yield floor and retain upside optionality against low-yield or managed currencies, especially JPY and CNY. The conclusion is more nuanced for the broad dollar: DXY is near 97.8, not breaking out, because Europe and Japan also face imported-energy inflation and policy uncertainty.

## Core Judgment

Sticky US inflation is not a clean "buy all USD" signal; it is a relative-rate and carry signal. The best expression is a resilient-to-firmer USD versus JPY and a managed, slow-drift USD/CNY range rather than a broad, one-way DXY surge.

## Why Sticky Inflation Supports the Dollar

The Fed's April 29 statement kept the federal funds target at 3.50%-3.75% and explicitly noted elevated inflation and high uncertainty tied partly to global energy prices. That matters for FX because the front end of the US curve is not validating aggressive easing. The Treasury curve on May 8 still showed a 2Y yield at 3.90% and a 10Y at 4.38%, giving the dollar a positive nominal-rate cushion against EUR, JPY, and CNY.

The inflation evidence is not just one hot CPI print. The March CPI report showed headline CPI up 0.9% MoM and 3.3% YoY, while March PCE inflation was 3.5% YoY and core PCE 3.2% YoY. More importantly for the near-term Fed reaction function, April ISM Services Prices held at 70.7, and all 18 services industries reported higher prices paid. This is the exact configuration in which the Fed can acknowledge weaker payrolls but resist validating front-loaded cuts.

## USD/JPY: Carry Still Wins, But the Tail Risk Is Two-Sided

USD/JPY should remain biased high in the near term, with 154-160 the relevant tactical band rather than a quick return to 145-150. Using ECB May 8 reference rates, EUR/JPY 184.37 divided by EUR/USD 1.1761 implies USD/JPY around 156.8. That is consistent with the pair trading as a yield differential and funding-currency instrument rather than as a pure US growth indicator.

The carry math remains compelling: the Fed target range is 3.50%-3.75%, while the BOJ is still guiding the overnight call rate around 0.75%. Even after hedging and volatility costs, that gap keeps JPY-funded dollar longs attractive when US inflation blocks Fed cuts. This is why a cooling US labor market does not automatically weaken USD/JPY; it would need to cool enough to shift the Fed, not merely enough to slow hiring.

The risk is that USD/JPY upside becomes self-limiting near 160. The BOJ's April 28 decision had three dissenters favoring a move toward 1.0%, so the domestic policy reaction function is more hawkish than the headline hold suggests. If April US CPI on May 12 is hot, the first move could be USD/JPY higher; if that pushes spot toward 160, intervention rhetoric or actual smoothing operations could create a sharp, low-liquidity reversal. In positioning terms, USD/JPY remains a positive-carry long, but not a low-volatility long.

## USD/CNY: Managed Resilience, Not a Free Float Breakout

USD/CNY should be more stable than USD/JPY because the exchange-rate regime is managed, China's current-account position is still a stabilizer, and policymakers have strong incentives to avoid a disorderly depreciation signal. The ECB May 8 reference cross implies USD/CNY near 6.80 from EUR/CNY 7.9989 and EUR/USD 1.1761.

The fundamental pressure still leans toward a modestly firmer USD/CNY path. US inflation is too high for the Fed to cut quickly, while China's domestic inflation is much softer: China March CPI rose only 1.0% YoY and fell 0.7% MoM. That keeps the policy-growth mix asymmetric. The US can tolerate restrictive rates because inflation is the constraint; China has less inflation pressure but more reason to preserve growth and export competitiveness. This supports a controlled CNY depreciation bias rather than a large appreciation.

However, I would not chase a disorderly USD/CNY breakout. If US inflation stays sticky, PBOC fixings and macro-prudential guidance can slow the transmission into onshore CNY. The likely path is "creep, not gap": USD/CNY can trade with an upside bias, but the path should be smoother than USD/JPY and less sensitive to a single US data release.

## EUR/USD and DXY: Why Broad Dollar Strength Is Less Clean

EUR/USD is the release valve that prevents this from becoming a simple DXY-bullish thesis. The ECB held the deposit rate at 2.00% on April 30 and also emphasized upside inflation risks from Middle East energy shocks. That does not erase the US yield advantage, but it means Europe is not in a pure disinflationary easing cycle either. With EUR/USD at 1.1761 on the ECB May 8 reference, broad dollar upside requires either a hotter US CPI surprise or clearer euro-area growth damage.

This explains the current split: DXY futures closed at 97.784 on May 8, while USD/JPY remained around 156.8 on the ECB cross. The dollar is not uniformly strong; it is strongest where US carry is most valuable and where the counterpart currency is constrained by low rates or managed policy.

## Trading and Risk Implications

My preferred expression is selective USD carry, not blanket long USD:

- USD/JPY: constructive while US 2Y yields hold near 3.9% and the Fed is not validating cuts. Use reduced sizing or option structures near 158-160 because intervention and BOJ-hike repricing risks rise nonlinearly.
- USD/CNY: modest upside bias, but expect smoothing through fixings and administrative tools. The better expression is gradual appreciation of USD/CNH or owning topside optionality rather than leveraged spot chasing.
- EUR/USD: neutral-to-slightly lower if US CPI is hot on May 12, but less attractive as a standalone short because ECB inflation concerns limit policy divergence.
- Carry basket: long USD versus JPY and low-yield Asia is cleaner than long USD versus EUR. The stop condition is a clear soft CPI plus weaker payroll confirmation that pulls the US 2Y yield below roughly 3.70%.

## What Would Change the View

The main near-term catalyst is the April CPI release scheduled for Tuesday, May 12, 2026 at 8:30 a.m. ET. A soft print would weaken the FX thesis by validating labor cooling and reviving Fed-cut pricing. A hot print would strengthen the thesis, especially for USD/JPY and USD/CNY, but it would also raise intervention risk in JPY and political sensitivity around CNY.

The second catalyst is whether the BOJ converts its April dissent into a June hike signal. A move from 0.75% toward 1.0% would not erase carry, but it could force crowded JPY shorts to reprice volatility.
