# 2026-05-12 Chief Economist Report: Global Inflation Expectations and Monetary Policy Transmission

As of 2026-05-12, I support the prior card's core direction: the Hormuz energy shock is large enough to dominate the rare-earth truce's supply-chain relief and to push the global macro regime toward stagflation. The nuance is important: inflation expectations are not yet globally unanchored, but the probability of de-anchoring has risen materially because central banks can suppress second-round demand and wage effects only with a lag, while oil, gas, freight, and food-cost pass-through are immediate.

## Judgment

The base case is a 2026 inflation hump with weaker real growth, not a 1970s-style wage-price spiral. The tail risk is worse: if Brent remains in the USD 120-150/bbl zone for more than two quarters, and LNG rationing tightens Asian and European industrial margins, central banks will be forced to keep real rates restrictive into a growth slowdown. That is the channel through which policy lag turns a cost-push shock into recession risk.

## Evidence Chain

## Transmission Path

The first-round effect is mechanical: fuel, power, shipping, petrochemical feedstocks, fertilizer, and food distribution costs rise before central banks can affect demand. The second-round effect is behavioral: households revise visible gasoline and utility-price expectations, firms pre-emptively lift catalog prices, and wage negotiations start to compensate for real-income losses. The third channel is financial: higher inflation risk premia and energy-importer FX depreciation raise import prices further, especially in Asia and Europe.

Monetary policy can break the second and third channels, but only through tighter financial conditions, weaker labor demand, and lower investment. That means successful anti-inflation transmission is itself recessionary when the original shock is supply-side. This is why I support the stagflation warning from card 02, while rejecting the strongest version of "already out of control": the best live evidence still shows anchored medium-term expectations.

## Thresholds To Monitor

- **Expectation breach:** NY Fed five-year expectations moving above 3.3% for two consecutive releases, or comparable euro-area/UK long-horizon surveys rising together.
- **Energy persistence:** Brent staying above USD 120/bbl through Q3 2026, or TTF/LNG prices forcing industrial curtailment beyond energy-intensive sectors.
- **Policy contradiction:** central banks cutting rates while headline expectations and wage settlements rise, which would validate de-anchoring fears.
- **FX pass-through:** sustained depreciation in major energy-importer currencies, especially JPY, KRW, INR, and CNY pressure, turning dollar oil inflation into broader import inflation.

## Conclusion

The rare-earth truce can reduce one technology-supply bottleneck, but it does not change the macro arithmetic of a Hormuz shock. As of 2026-05-12, the more defensible call is **"contained expectations, rising de-anchoring risk, and materially higher recession probability."** The policy lag does not guarantee global inflation expectations will break, but it increases the cost of preventing that break: central banks must keep policy tighter for longer, which raises the odds that a supply shock becomes a demand contraction.
