# Monetary Policy in a Service-Dominant Economy: The Transmission Paradox

**Date:** 2026-05-14  
**Author:** Chief Economist  
**Status:** Stress-Test Analysis  

## 1. Executive Summary
As of May 2026, the structural shift toward service-based consumption has created a "transmission paradox" for global central banks. While traditional interest rate hikes remain effective in cooling goods-related demand (housing, durables), their impact on the service sector—now the primary driver of "Supercore" inflation—is diminishing. This report stress-tests the current monetary framework, arguing that the 2% inflation target may be structurally obsolete in an era of labor-intensive service dominance, and proposes a shift toward "Functional Inflation Targeting."

## 2. The Transmission Gap: Why Rates are "Leaking"
Traditional monetary policy operates through capital-intensive channels. However, the service-led economy of 2026 is fundamentally different:
*   **Labor-Intensive Cost Structures:** Services (healthcare, education, high-end hospitality) are driven by wages rather than capital costs. With the global "talent squeeze" of 2026, wage growth remains sticky at 4.5%–5.0%, rendering the 5%+ policy rates less effective than in previous cycles.
*   **Demographic Insulation:** A significant portion of service demand is driven by the "Silver Economy" (high-net-worth retirees), whose consumption is funded by asset yields rather than credit. Paradoxically, higher interest rates *increase* the disposable income of this cohort, fueling further service demand.
*   **The Goods-Service Divergence:** While manufacturing PMI remains in contraction due to high borrowing costs and trade barriers, Service PMIs have consistently printed above 54.0, suggesting a "dual-speed" economy where the interest-rate brake only hits the slower lane.

## 3. Stress-Testing the 2% Anchor
The current "higher-for-longer" regime faces a critical failure point:
*   **Supply-Side Inelasticity:** Service inflation is often supply-constrained (shortage of nurses, pilots, AI engineers). Aggressive rate hikes to reach 2% inflation risk "destroying the village to save it"—causing a deep recession in goods-producing sectors while only marginally cooling services.
*   **The Neutral Rate (R-star) Shift:** We estimate that the structural shift to services has pushed the global neutral rate up by 75–100 bps. A "restrictive" stance of 2024 is merely "neutral" in 2026, explaining the surprising resilience of global GDP growth.

## 4. Policy Framework 2.0: Beyond Blunt Tools
Central banks must evolve to maintain relevance:
*   **From Point Targets to Tolerance Bands:** Moving from a 2.0% rigid anchor to a 2.5%–3.5% "Tolerance Range" to avoid unnecessary output volatility.
*   **Sector-Specific Macroprudential Policy:** Using targeted reserve requirements or credit guidance for service-heavy lenders, rather than relying solely on the blunt instrument of the overnight rate.
*   **Coordination with Fiscal/Supply Policy:** Acknowledging that service inflation is a structural labor problem. Central banks must explicitly signal that 2% is unattainable without productivity-linked fiscal reforms (e.g., AI-driven service automation).

## 5. Conclusion
The "last mile" of disinflation has become a "marathon of stickiness." If central banks continue to use a 20th-century toolkit for a 21st-century service economy, they risk causing a "policy-induced stagflation" where goods sectors collapse while services continue to drift upward. We believe a formal upward revision of inflation targets is no longer a "risk," but a "necessity" for 2027.
