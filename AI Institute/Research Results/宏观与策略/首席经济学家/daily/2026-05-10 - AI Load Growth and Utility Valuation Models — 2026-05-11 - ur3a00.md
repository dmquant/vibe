# AI Load Growth and Utility Valuation Models — 2026-05-11

As of 2026-05-11 (Asia/Singapore work-date), I synthesize the initial question as follows: the NERC Level 3 Alert does not abolish the regulated-utility valuation framework, but it does force a structural rewrite of the load-growth, risk, and terminal-value assumptions used inside that framework. Data centers are no longer simply incremental MWh and rate-base growth. They are now large, fast-moving, power-electronics-rich loads whose value depends on contract enforceability, system studies, curtailability, cost allocation, and the political tolerance for retail-rate pressure.

## Core Judgment

Yes, the transition from "growth opportunity" to "systemic reliability risk" fundamentally changes long-term utility valuation models at the margin that matters for equity duration. The old model could treat data-center demand as a high-load-factor customer class that expands sales, justifies capex, and lifts rate base. The new model must separate contracted and reliability-compliant demand from speculative demand, and it must add explicit haircuts for stranded assets, regulatory lag, equity issuance, wholesale power-price exposure, and affordability backlash.

The correct answer is therefore not "utilities are now uninvestable." It is more precise: utilities with binding large-load tariffs, collateral, minimum bills, dynamic-load data, and recoverable transmission/generation plans can deserve higher growth assumptions; utilities with weak cost causation, vague data-center queues, or fast capex ahead of enforceable contracts should trade at a higher risk premium despite stronger headline load growth.

## Why The NERC Alert Changes The Question

NERC issued its Level 3 Alert, "Computational Load Modeling, Studies, Instrumentation, Commissioning, Operations, Protection, and Control," with initial distribution on May 4, 2026. It requires acknowledgement by midnight Eastern on May 11, 2026 and reporting by midnight Eastern on August 3, 2026. NERC says the alert is aimed at existing and new computational loads interacting with the bulk power system, including colocated generation. The alert also states that prior Level 2 responses showed entities generally lacked sufficient processes, procedures, or methods for computational-load risks. NERC is working toward a "Computational Load Entity" registration concept that would currently include loads of 20 MW and above connected at 60 kV with more than 1 MW of IT load. Source: [NERC Level 3 Alert, May 4, 2026](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/programs/bpsa/alerts/level-3-computational-load-alert.pdf).

That is a different fact pattern from ordinary commercial load growth. The valuation issue is not merely how many GW are coming; it is whether those GW can be modeled, interconnected, commissioned, tripped, reconnected, and paid for without degrading bulk-power reliability. This shifts utility analysis from a sales-volume model to a reliability-adjusted contract model.

## Demand Is Real, But Not All Demand Is Bankable

The demand side is no longer speculative at the macro level. The DOE's December 20, 2024 release on the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory report estimated that U.S. data centers consumed about 4.4% of total U.S. electricity in 2023 and could reach 6.7% to 12% by 2028, with usage rising from 176 TWh in 2023 to 325-580 TWh by 2028. Source: [DOE / LBNL data-center energy report release](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers).

EIA's January 13, 2026 Short-Term Energy Outlook release framed the current cycle as the strongest four-year U.S. electricity-demand growth since 2000, driven largely by large computing facilities including data centers. Source: [EIA January 2026 STEO release](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php). NERC's 2025 Long-Term Reliability Assessment, released in January 2026, says new data centers for AI and the digital economy account for most projected North American electricity-demand growth over the next 10 years, with Texas, PJM, and WECC showing steep increases. It also warns that LTRA data-center forecasts are probably more conservative than technology-industry forecasts because they count projects that have moved beyond purely speculative stages. Source: [NERC 2025 Long-Term Reliability Assessment](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/our-work/assessments/nerc_ltra_2025.pdf).

The key valuation distinction is bankability. A 1 GW inquiry is not the same as a 1 GW signed service agreement; a signed agreement is not the same as a studied, collateralized, dynamically modeled, and recoverable load. NERC's alert widens the gap between headline pipeline and valuation-grade pipeline.

## The Contractualization Layer Becomes Central

Large-load tariffs are the market's institutional response to this new risk. AEP Ohio's Data Center Tariff became effective on July 23, 2025 after PUCO adopted the settlement on July 9, 2025. For projects of 25,000 kW or greater, AEP Ohio requires a load-study process, fees from $10,000 to $100,000, customer-specific load information, and signed letters of agreement and electric service agreements. The load ramp cannot exceed four years, with minimum contract capacity of 50%, 65%, 80%, and 90% in years one through four, followed by an initial term equal to the ramp period plus eight years. Source: [AEP Ohio Data Center Tariff](https://www.aepohio.com/company/about/rates/data-center-tariff/).

The Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory January 2025 technical brief on large-load rate design shows the broader toolkit: collateral, minimum bills, contract terms, exit fees, reassignment of capacity, ramp periods, marginal pricing, and clean-energy procurement structures. Source: [LBNL large-load rate design technical brief](https://eta-publications.lbl.gov/sites/default/files/2025-01/electricity_rate_designs_for_large_loads_evolving_practices_and_opportunities_final.pdf). SEPA's DELTa tracker, updated through March 31, 2026, shows 77 approved or proposed tariffs and service rules across 60 utilities in 36 states. Source: [SEPA DELTa update](https://sepapower.org/knowledge/u-s-data-center-gold-rush-drives-surge-in-new-utility-tariffs/).

For valuation, this means the data-center premium should be awarded to contract quality, not to geography alone. A utility in a hot data-center region may deserve a lower multiple if it must pre-build infrastructure without enforceable take-or-pay protection. Conversely, a utility with smaller load growth but stronger tariff mechanics may have a better risk-adjusted rate-base story.

## What Changes In The Long-Term Model

The classic regulated utility model remains: earnings power is still driven by rate base, allowed ROE, capital structure, O&M efficiency, tax treatment, and regulatory lag. What changes is the treatment of the numerator and the discount rate.

First, load growth must be probability-weighted. Analysts should split the pipeline into five buckets: inquiry, interconnection queue, signed service agreement, collateralized/minimum-bill contract, and NERC-ready dynamic-load model with approved studies. Only the last two should receive high conversion credit in long-term EPS and rate-base CAGR.

Second, capex should be haircut by cost-recovery confidence. Transmission, generation, storage, and distribution upgrades tied to data centers can lift rate base, but the NPV depends on whether regulators allow timely recovery and whether non-data-center customers are protected. Affordability has become a political constraint, not a footnote.

Third, the discount rate should include a reliability and execution premium. NERC's Level 3 alert is not a penalty-bearing Reliability Standard, but it signals future standards, registration, data requirements, and operational obligations. That raises the value of engineering competence and lowers the value of vague growth narratives.

Fourth, terminal value should be more conservative where the load is AI-training-heavy, less flexible, or dependent on uncertain hyperscaler capex. In contrast, inference, cloud, and diversified enterprise workloads with longer contracts and flexible operations deserve lower terminal haircuts.

Fifth, wholesale power and fuel exposure matter again. EIA's high-demand scenario finds that ERCOT and PJM are the fastest-growing data-center demand regions through 2027. Under a higher-demand case, EIA estimates the 2027 ERCOT wholesale power price would be $37/MWh, or 79%, above the February STEO forecast, while PJM would be $2.60/MWh, or 4%, higher. Source: [EIA high electricity demand scenario](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67344). That matters for utilities with fuel-cost lag, market purchases, or political sensitivity to bills.

## Evidence From Company Guidance

AEP is the clearest example of the upside and the risk becoming inseparable. Its Q1 2026 presentation shows 63 GW of incremental contracted load by 2030, up from 56 GW, a $78 billion 2026-2030 capital plan, nearly 11% rate-base CAGR through 2030, a 7%-9% annual operating earnings growth range with expected CAGR above 9% through 2030, and a targeted 14%-15% FFO/debt range. Source: [AEP Q1 2026 earnings presentation](https://docs.aep.com/docs/newsroom/resources/earnings/2026-05/1Q26EarningsReleasePresentation.pdf).

Those numbers justify higher growth assumptions, but they also explain why the market needs stricter valuation architecture. A five-year capital plan of that size is not automatically accretive if interconnection timing slips, required equity rises, regulators disallow costs, or computational-load rules force additional protection and control spending. AEP's own case supports the thesis: contracted load can change growth duration, but only because contractualization and regulatory strategy are being built into the model.

Dominion Virginia illustrates the regional concentration problem. EIA's May 2026 Virginia analysis says PJM expects the Dominion zone to post the largest absolute increase in summer peak demand from 2026 through 2030, largely because of data-center load. It also reports that the zone's 2025 summer peak was 23,905 MW, 23% above 2019, and the 2025-26 winter peak was 25,413 MW, 45% above the 2019-20 winter season. Source: [EIA Virginia data-center load analysis](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67664).

## Practical Valuation Framework

For long-term utility DCF and relative valuation, I would add four explicit modules:

| Module | Old treatment | 2026-05-11 treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Load growth | Sales and rate-base tailwind | Contract-weighted, study-weighted, curtailability-weighted pipeline |
| Capex | Rate-base growth driver | Rate-base growth minus regulatory lag, equity dilution, supply-chain delay, and stranded-asset risk |
| Reliability | Operational assumption | Model input affecting capex, O&M, standards compliance, and discount rate |
| Terminal multiple | Stable regulated duration | Higher for protected take-or-pay load; lower for speculative AI load and affordability-exposed jurisdictions |

My suggested rule of thumb: capitalize only collateralized/minimum-bill large-load contracts at near-normal utility multiples; discount signed-but-not-studied load by 25%-50%; treat uncontracted inquiries as strategic optionality rather than base-case earnings.

## Investment Conclusion

The NERC Level 3 Alert marks a regime shift in how utility growth should be valued. It does not make data centers bad customers. It makes them system-shaping customers. Long-term valuation should move from "more load equals more rate base equals higher EPS" to "enforceable, reliability-ready load equals durable rate-base growth; speculative or poorly allocated load equals political and balance-sheet risk."

I would support a selective overweight in utilities that can prove three things: enforceable large-load contracts, regulatory cost-causation protection for existing customers, and credible reliability integration. I would avoid paying premium multiples for mere queue size. The decisive variable is no longer data-center demand; it is who bears the risk when that demand is delayed, trips offline, or requires more grid investment than originally planned.
