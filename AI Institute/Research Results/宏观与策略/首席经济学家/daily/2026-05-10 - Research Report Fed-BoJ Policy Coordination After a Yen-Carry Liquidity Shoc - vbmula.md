# Research Report: Fed-BoJ Policy Coordination After a Yen-Carry Liquidity Shock
**Date:** 2026-05-11  
**Analyst:** Chief Economist (gemini)  
**Status:** Synthesize (Card 06/08)

As of **2026-05-11**, I synthesize Cards 03-05 rather than overturn them: the 30Y Treasury yield, USD/JPY, 5s30s, and NDX should be read as one liquidity system. The Fed's threshold for easing term premium is not simply an NDX drawdown greater than 10%; it is an NDX drawdown plus evidence that Treasury financing, repo rates, and dealer balance-sheet capacity are becoming disorderly. The BoJ is more likely to delay a hike because of excessive two-way FX volatility than because of yen weakness itself.

## Core Judgment
If the yen-carry unwind creates a 48-72 hour liquidity vacuum and NDX falls more than 10%, the Fed's first response should be communication, not an immediate rate cut. The base-case Fed "put" is activated when four signals cluster: NDX down 10-12% from the recent high, 30Y UST at 5.15-5.25%, 5s30s at +100 to +115 bps, and money-market stress visible through persistent repo/SOFR pressure or repeated SRF usage above roughly $75-100 billion. Below that, the Fed can tolerate a correction. Above that, term premium becomes a market-functioning problem, not just an equity valuation problem.

For the BoJ, the direction of USD/JPY matters less than the speed. An orderly move above 165 argues for higher, not lower, Japanese policy rates because import-price pass-through is stronger in the BoJ's current framework. A violent yen short squeeze of 5-8% over 3-5 trading days, combined with one-week USD/JPY implied volatility above 14-16% and a TOPIX/Nikkei drawdown near 8-10%, would likely push the BoJ to delay the next 25 bp hike by one meeting while keeping the hiking bias alive.

The key point is that **NDX -10% is necessary but not sufficient**. A 10% tech correction after rich AI-capex multiples would not, by itself, justify a Fed liquidity turn while April FOMC language still emphasizes elevated inflation. The threshold is crossed when the equity drawdown feeds back into Treasury market depth, repo funding, credit spreads, and term-premium instability.

## How The Fed Caps Term Premium Without Cutting Rates
The cleanest response is a sequencing package:

1. **Verbal separation:** the Fed distinguishes inflation policy from market functioning. It can say that the funds-rate path remains data-dependent while existing facilities are available to support orderly market functioning.
2. **SRF absorption:** because the SRF is full allotment with no aggregate cap, heavy take-up should be treated as a stabilizer rather than a stigma event. A jump from the March reference point of $30 billion to sustained $75-100 billion usage would be the real signal.
3. **Reserve-management purchases:** the current directive already allows bill purchases and, if needed, Treasuries with remaining maturities of 3 years or less. This leans against front-end funding stress without directly monetizing the 30Y selloff.
4. **Swap-line backstop:** if the yen unwind becomes a dollar-funding squeeze through Japanese banks and nonbanks, the Fed-BoJ standing swap framework is the right tool. It reduces forced dollar buying without changing the BoJ's domestic policy rate.

This mix would reduce term premium by lowering the liquidity premium embedded in long bonds. It would not necessarily lower expected real policy rates immediately. That is why TLT can rally before the Fed cuts.

## BoJ Decision Rule
I would not describe the BoJ as mechanically delaying hikes because USD/JPY is high. Its April decision contains two hawkish facts: three dissents for a move to 1.0%, and an inflation forecast materially above January. The delay condition is **excess volatility plus financial tightening**, not yen depreciation.

| USD/JPY regime | BoJ read | Hike timing |
|---|---|---|
| USD/JPY above 165, rising gradually; one-week implied vol below 12%; JGB and equity markets orderly. | Weak yen raises import prices and risks lifting inflation expectations. | No delay; June/July hike risk stays live. |
| USD/JPY above 165 followed by a 5-8% yen rally in 3-5 trading days; one-week vol above 14-16%; TOPIX/Nikkei down 8-10%; dollar-funding demand rises. | This is a market-functioning shock and a household/corporate confidence shock. | Delay one meeting, keep hiking bias, use dollar operations and communication. |
| USD/JPY above 168-170 with no yen squeeze but stronger import-price pass-through. | Inflation credibility dominates growth caution. | Higher probability of a 25 bp hike, unless oil shock simultaneously hits demand hard. |

## Feedback Loop With Cards 03-05
This card supports the prior FX-gated TLT framework with one refinement: the Fed trigger should be defined by **repo and Treasury functioning**, not only by 30Y yield points. Card 05's 4% NAV JPY-call firewall remains the correct 48-72 hour hedge. After that window, the decisive signal is whether the Fed response shifts from monitoring to Tier 1/Tier 2.

Execution rule:
- If NDX is down more than 10% but SRF use is still below $50 billion and 5s30s is below +100 bps, keep the half-sized TLT stance.
- If NDX is down 10-12% and 5s30s is +100 to +115 bps with clear Fed verbal support, finish the first TLT notch and take partial profit on JPY calls.
- If SRF use persists above $75-100 billion or repo rates push above the corridor, add TLT toward 15-18% NAV even before a formal FOMC cut.
- If BoJ delays one meeting because FX volatility is disorderly, do not read it as dovish reflation. It is a volatility brake and should reduce the left-tail speed of the yen squeeze.

## Conclusion
The Fed put is not an equity put; it is a market-functioning put. In this scenario, the practical threshold is **NDX -10% plus 30Y 5.15-5.25%, 5s30s +100 to +115 bps, and persistent repo/SRF stress**. The BoJ is unlikely to postpone hikes because USD/JPY is weak in an orderly way; it is likely to postpone by one meeting if the yen-carry unwind generates violent FX volatility, equity stress, and dollar-funding demand. The central-bank feedback loop therefore validates the Card 05 sequence: survive the first 48-72 hours with JPY optionality, then rotate into TLT as Fed market-functioning language and SRF usage confirm that term premium has become policy-relevant.
