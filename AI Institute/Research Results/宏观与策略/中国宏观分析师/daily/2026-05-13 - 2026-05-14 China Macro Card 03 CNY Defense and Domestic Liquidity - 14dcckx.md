# 2026-05-14 China Macro Card 03: CNY Defense and Domestic Liquidity Offsets

As of the institute work-date, 2026-05-14, I support the prior chain's core judgment that a prolonged Fed pause keeps China under negative-rate-differential pressure, but I mark one important level correction: the latest official FOMC decision on April 29, 2026 kept the federal funds target at 3.50%-3.75%, not above 5%. The pressure is still material because the PBOC's 7-day reverse repo rate is 1.40% and China's 10-year government bond yield was 1.75% on May 13, leaving China with a policy-rate gap of about -223 bp versus the Fed midpoint and a 10-year sovereign gap of roughly -270 bp versus U.S. yields near 4.45%.

## Judgment

The PBOC's best equilibrium is not a hard defense of one USD/CNY line. It is a managed-volatility regime: use the daily fixing, counter-cyclical guidance, offshore RMB liquidity tools, macroprudential FX rules, and selective reserve use to prevent one-way depreciation expectations, while keeping domestic liquidity abundant enough to avoid turning FX defense into an onshore credit squeeze.

The current setup gives Beijing room to do this. USD/CNY fixing was 6.8431 on May 13, 2026, far from a disorderly-break regime; official reserves rose to USD 3.4105 trillion at end-April; Q1 credit and M2 still show ample domestic money creation; and April CPI at 1.2% does not force anti-inflation tightening. The constraint is subtler: PPI at 2.8%, energy-linked imported inflation, and stronger exports mean broad rate cuts would weaken the CNY signal and compress bank margins without a clear growth need.

## Policy Mix

First, keep the fixing credible. A stable or only gradually weaker midpoint is the lowest-cost defense because it shapes dealer expectations before spot trading begins. With the May 13 fixing at 6.8431, the immediate message is that the PBOC is not validating a jump toward the prior-card USD/CNY stress area.

Second, ration depreciation pressure across instruments rather than burn reserves mechanically. The practical toolkit is familiar: stronger fixings, guidance to market makers, offshore CNH liquidity management, adjustment of FX reserve requirements or macroprudential parameters when needed, and state-bank smoothing in spot and forwards. Reserves should be used as a circuit breaker against disorderly moves, not as a daily peg commitment.

Third, sterilize any FX-defense liquidity drain onshore. If the central bank sells dollars and absorbs CNY, domestic base money tightens. The offset is to add RMB through 7-day reverse repos, MLF or outright repo tools, targeted relending, and, if needed, RRR cuts. The May 13 operation at 1.40% is small in size, but the signal is that the short policy rate is being held steady while liquidity can be supplied as needed.

Fourth, preserve monetary-policy independence through quantity and structure rather than headline rate cuts. With Q1 GDP at 5.0%, exports strong, M2/TSF still above nominal-growth comfort, and bank net interest margins under pressure, the cleaner policy path is targeted support for technology, small firms, consumption, housing completion, and local-debt refinancing rather than a broad rate cut that would widen the USD-CNY carry penalty.

## Domestic Liquidity Impact

Base case: limited systemic liquidity shock. China's capital account management, goods surplus, reserves, and onshore liquidity tools mean FX defense should not force an outright liquidity squeeze. Interbank rates around mid-May remain near the PBOC corridor, and Shibor data on May 13 showed overnight at 1.2670% and 7-day at 1.2990%, consistent with ample money-market funding rather than stress.

Stress case: a dollar rally plus a higher-for-longer Fed repricing could create a three-step transmission. Step one, CNY expectations weaken and corporates front-load FX demand. Step two, offshore CNH liquidity is tightened to deter shorts, lifting hedging costs and widening CNH-CNY basis. Step three, if reserves are used without full sterilization, onshore repo and negotiable certificate of deposit rates rise, hurting leveraged bond positions and bank NIM. The most sensitive domestic channels are money-market funds, bank wealth-management products, long-duration CGB positions, and exporters/importers with unhedged FX exposure.

For A-shares, this mix is not uniformly bearish. It is negative for rate-sensitive leverage trades if interbank funding tightens, but it supports high-dividend defensives and banks if policy avoids disorderly CNY moves. It is also constructive for exporters with natural dollar receipts, although the current CNY resilience means the translation boost is smaller than in a classic depreciation cycle.

## Market Indicators to Watch

The next signal is not one data point but a cluster: daily fixing versus model-implied fixing, CNH-CNY spread, 1-month and 1-year USD/CNY forward points, FX settlement and sales, state-bank activity around the London and New York handover, DR007 versus the 1.40% policy rate, NCD yields, and whether reserves fall after stripping out valuation effects.

If those indicators stay orderly, China can keep a "stable CNY plus ample liquidity" stance through Q2 2026. If they break together, the PBOC will likely prioritize expectation management first, then sterilized liquidity injection second, and defer broad rate cuts until FX pressure eases.

## Conclusion

The prior card is directionally right: the Fed pause preserves a negative carry environment for CNY. The China-specific conclusion is more balanced. The PBOC can defend the exchange rate without sacrificing monetary independence because it has capital-flow controls, a large reserve stock, a daily fixing regime, and domestic liquidity tools. The trade-off is that policy easing must be more targeted and less rate-led. The main risk is not immediate liquidity shortage, but a feedback loop in which FX-defense signals, offshore CNH squeezes, and domestic bond leverage collide.
