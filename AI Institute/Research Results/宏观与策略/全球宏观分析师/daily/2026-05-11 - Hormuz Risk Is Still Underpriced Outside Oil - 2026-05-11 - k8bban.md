# Hormuz Risk Is Still Underpriced Outside Oil - 2026-05-11

As of 2026-05-11 (Asia/Singapore), I synthesize the initial board question as follows: the modest oil reaction after Iran rejected the latest U.S. proposal should not be read as proof that the Hormuz shock is solved. It is mostly a blend of negotiation optionality, emergency-stock comfort, demand destruction, and positioning after a violent round trip in crude. The more important signal is that equities, rates, and FX are still behaving as if the disruption is a temporary headline risk rather than a macro regime constraint.

## Bottom Line

I would give partial credit to negotiation expectations and inventory buffers, but I would not trust them enough to validate current cross-asset pricing. A Brent move of roughly 3%-4% to the $104-$105 area after the rejection is small relative to a still-effective closure of a chokepoint that normally carries about 20 million b/d of oil and roughly one-fifth of global LNG trade. The market is pricing a narrow path: no clean deal yet, but no major escalation, and enough policy/inventory response to prevent a disorderly shortage. That path is possible, but it is not the central risk investors should be paid to underwrite.

My stance: **support the idea that risk assets are underpricing the tail**, while rejecting a simplistic "oil should already be at $150" claim. Oil itself is not asleep. It has already moved a long way from pre-war levels and is now constrained by demand weakness, official releases, and the possibility that diplomacy reopens the strait. The underpricing is more visible in risk assets, front-end rate complacency, and FX crosses that are not rewarding importers' energy vulnerability.

## What Happened

Iran sent a response to the latest U.S. proposal through Pakistani mediators on 2026-05-10. AP reported that Iranian state television described the U.S. offer as surrender-like and listed demands including U.S. war reparations, Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz, sanctions relief, and release of seized assets. The U.S. proposal, by contrast, covered ending the war, reopening the strait, and rolling back Iran's nuclear program. Trump rejected the response as "TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE," according to AP. Axios separately reported that Brent jumped about 3% in early trading, hovering near $104/bbl shortly before 8 p.m. ET on 2026-05-10.

That is a meaningful move, but it is mild compared with the size of the unresolved physical problem. EIA's latest structural chokepoint work says 2024 Hormuz oil flows averaged 20 million b/d, equal to about 20% of global petroleum liquids consumption, and that flows through Hormuz in 2024 and 1Q25 were more than one-quarter of global seaborne oil trade. EIA also estimated that Saudi and UAE pipelines had about 2.6 million b/d of available bypass capacity in a disruption, which is useful but far below the normal flow through the strait.

## Why Oil Did Not Panic

First, the market still assigns value to negotiation optionality. The diplomatic gap is wide, but both sides have incentives to keep a channel open: Iran is under export and revenue pressure, while the U.S. wants lower fuel prices and a visible reopening mechanism before the 2026 summer demand season. Al Jazeera's 2026-05-06 read on the talks framed the key unresolved issue as whether Washington would accept Iran's sequence of "Hormuz first, nuclear later"; it also noted a 30-day end-war framework under discussion and the May 14-15 Trump-Xi meeting as a diplomatic forcing point.

Second, emergency stocks and weak demand are doing real work. IEA's April 2026 Oil Market Report projected global oil demand to decline by 80 kb/d on average in 2026, a sharp downgrade from the prior month's expected 730 kb/d growth. The same report's base case assumed regular Middle East deliveries resume by mid-year, while explicitly warning that this could prove too optimistic if the conflict persists. On U.S. inventories, the EIA weekly release dated 2026-05-06 covered data through 2026-05-01. Rigzone's recap of that EIA report showed commercial crude stocks down 2.3 million bbl w/w to 457.2 million bbl, around 1% above the five-year average; gasoline stocks were 4% below the five-year average and distillates 11% below. That is a buffer, but not a comfortable all-products buffer.

Third, oil has already repriced part of the shock. Reuters/MarketScreener reported on 2026-05-06 that oil fell below $100 for the first time since 2026-04-22 on deal hopes, while S&P 500 and Nasdaq hit record highs. On 2026-05-11, Bloomberg via SWI reported Brent up 4.3% to above $105 and WTI up 4.7% to $99.93, with the 10-year Treasury yield up four basis points to 4.39%. In other words, the market has moved, but it is treating each headline as a probability update, not a binary supply stop.

## Where The Mispricing Is

The clearest mispricing is in the macro spillover, not necessarily the front Brent contract. The U.S. CPI release on 2026-04-10 showed March CPI up 0.9% m/m and 3.3% y/y, with energy up 12.5% y/y and core CPI at 2.6% y/y. OECD's 2026-05-06 release showed OECD headline inflation rising to 4.0% in March from 3.4% in February, with energy inflation reaching 8.1%, and euro-area flash April headline inflation rising to 3.0% with energy inflation at 10.9%. The Fed's 2026-04-29 statement explicitly said inflation was elevated "in part" because of global energy prices, and its implementation note kept the federal funds target at 3.50%-3.75%.

Dallas Fed research published 2026-04-17 estimated that a one-quarter Hormuz closure would lift U.S. 2026 Q4/Q4 headline inflation by 0.6 percentage points and core inflation by 0.2 percentage points. If the closure lasts three quarters, the modeled effect rises to 1.1 percentage points on headline and 0.3 percentage points on core. Those estimates matter because they turn Hormuz from an energy-sector headline into a central-bank reaction-function problem.

Equities look especially fragile because the rally is being carried by earnings and AI momentum while energy inflation is quietly tightening financial conditions. Reuters/MarketScreener reported that S&P 500 companies were on track for their strongest profit growth in more than four years and that 83% of the 314 companies reporting through 2026-05-01 beat profit estimates. That explains why equities can look through a single rejection. It does not explain why the equity risk premium should ignore a durable oil tax on consumers, airlines, chemicals, logistics, and import-dependent Asia/Europe.

Rates are mispriced if investors still expect fast easing. The correct policy mix is awkward: oil is a negative supply shock that weakens real growth but lifts headline inflation and can seep into expectations. A pure growth scare would pull yields lower. A sticky energy-inflation shock can keep front-end rates higher for longer while term premia rise. The 10-year Treasury at about 4.39% on 2026-05-11 is not absurd, but it does not look generous if CPI, gasoline, and inflation expectations keep printing hot into the May and June data windows.

FX is underpricing importer vulnerability. The Bloomberg/SWI market wrap on 2026-05-11 showed the euro down 0.3% to $1.1757 and yen down 0.3% to 157.16 per dollar while the Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index rose 0.2%. That direction is intuitive, but the scale is small given Japan's and Europe's energy-import exposure. The yen's safe-haven role is compromised when the shock is an imported-energy terms-of-trade shock and when Japan's 10-year yield is already around 2.495%.

## Trading And Research Implications

Base case: do not chase crude at any price, but do not treat the modest bounce as an all-clear. The market is discounting a fragile de-escalation path that requires at least three things to hold: no U.S.-Iran escalation, no material damage to Gulf energy infrastructure, and no breakdown of tanker insurance or naval escort capacity. If any one fails, the next repricing should land more heavily in equities, rates volatility, and importer FX than in the already-volatile oil tape.

Preferred cross-asset expression:

| Asset class | Interpretation as of 2026-05-11 | Risk bias |
|---|---|---|
| Oil | Partially prices the crisis, but still discounts reopening before a full inventory cliff | Upside tail remains |
| U.S. equities | Earnings and AI momentum are muting the macro tax | Vulnerable to multiple compression |
| U.S. rates | Energy inflation reduces the Fed's room to ease | Higher front-end and term-premium risk |
| EUR/JPY | Energy-importer FX reaction looks too small | Downside versus USD and selected commodity FX |
| Gold | Already high, but still a cleaner hedge than broad equity beta | Useful in escalation baskets |

## Answer To The Mission Question

Believe the negotiation channel enough to avoid a one-way panic call in crude. Believe the inventory buffer enough to explain why Brent is not instantly repricing to extreme scenarios. But treat the muted oil move as a warning sign for **stocks/rates/FX mispricing**, not as proof that the market has correctly digested Hormuz. The oil market is saying "time has been bought." Cross-assets are acting as if "risk has been removed." Those are different claims.

## Handoff

Recommended next analyst: `asset-allocator` [primary]. The right next question is not another headline chronology, but a portfolio-level stress test: if Brent stays above $105 through June and the 10-year Treasury revisits 4.50%-4.75%, which equity, duration, commodity, and FX allocations still compensate investors for the tail?
