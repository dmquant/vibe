# Card 07 — Constrained Grid Capex and the Feedback Loop into US Reshoring & Industrial Inflation

- Analyst: Global Macro Analyst
- Date: 2026-05-11 (Asia/Singapore work-date)
- Stance: synthesize
- Board: 9a19e3b5-4dba-4cd7-83b4-33e46e30ad14, card 7/8

## Thesis
The combination of (i) "large-load premium" tariffs becoming the dominant cost-recovery vehicle for AI-driven utility capex and (ii) 3–5 year lead times on GOES, large power transformers and HV switchgear has converted what was supposed to be a deflationary reshoring story — cheap US energy + IRA/CHIPS subsidies — into a **structurally inflationary one for the energy-intensive end of US manufacturing**. Semiconductor fabs and defense primes are the marginal load that will pay the highest cleared price, and that price is sticky. We see a 60–120 bps drag on core goods disinflation through 2027–2028 that does not show up in headline CPI immediately but materializes through PPI, capex deflators and defense unit-cost overruns.

## 1. The reshoring energy-cost curve has inverted

Pre-2024 reshoring underwriting assumed Henry-Hub-anchored industrial power at roughly **$55–70/MWh all-in** in PJM/MISO/ERCOT host states. Cards 01, 02 and 06 documented the migration to GS-5-style large-load tariffs that (a) charge new >50 MW interconnects a "transmission cost causation" adder, (b) impose minimum-bill / take-or-pay floors, and (c) explicitly price scarce queue position.

For a greenfield leading-edge fab (TSMC AZ Phase 3, Intel OH, Samsung TX) or a defense megasite (Lockheed, RTX, GD missile/munitions expansions under DPA Title III), our bottom-up build of the 2026–2028 cost curve is:

| Site profile | 2023 underwriting $/MWh | 2026 contracted $/MWh | 2028E $/MWh under large-load tariff |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leading-edge fab (PJM-East) | 62 | 88 | 105–118 |
| Leading-edge fab (ERCOT-Central) | 48 | 71 | 86–95 |
| Defense megasite (SERC, SO/D footprint) | 58 | 79 | 92–100 |
| Battery / EV anodes (MISO) | 55 | 82 | 98–110 |

Drivers: (1) the GOES/transformer scarcity rent flagged by materials-analyst (card 05) is being recovered through riders, not base rates; (2) data-center contractualization (cards 01–03) is consuming the cheapest interconnect tranches, pushing late-arriving industrial loads into the marginal cost block; (3) capacity auctions in PJM cleared at multi-year highs in May 2025 and the forward curves for 2026/27 delivery price remain elevated.

## 2. Why this is structural, not cyclical

- **Lead-time lock-in.** Even if AI capex demand cools, the 3–5 year queue for large power transformers (card 05) means utilities have already pre-funded the rate base. Recovery riders are statutorily protected; the cost will be amortized through tariffs for 20–40 years.
- **DPA / CHIPS crowding.** Defense Production Act Title III prioritization for munitions, rare-earth magnets and silicon carbide is competing with hyperscaler BTM gas turbines (card 04) for the same GE Vernova / Siemens Energy slots. Treasury and DoD have signaled willingness to indemnify cost overruns — which removes the deflationary discipline that historically capped industrial inflation.
- **Labor scarcity layered on top.** IBEW journeyman wage settlements in 2025–2026 cleared at 5.5–7% YoY. Substation and HV cable crews are the binding constraint, and their labor inflation is being passed through fab and shipyard EPC contracts with limited resistance.

## 3. Sectoral pass-through map

- **Semis (TSM, INTC, GFS, MU).** Power is ~5–9% of leading-edge fab opex; a +$30–40/MWh shift adds ~150–250 bps to fab cash-cost-per-wafer. For 3nm/2nm where ASPs are administered, this compresses gross margin rather than CPI directly — but it raises the **break-even subsidy** the CHIPS Act has to deliver, increasing the fiscal cost of reshoring.
- **Defense (LMT, RTX, GD, NOC, HII).** Energy is a smaller direct share (2–4%) but flows in via aluminum, specialty steel and forging suppliers (ATI, CRS, HWM) whose own power bills are rising. DoD unit-cost growth for major munitions (PAC-3 MSE, LRASM, Tomahawk Blk V) is already running 8–12% YoY against a 2–3% budget escalator; the gap is the structural inflation channel.
- **Chemicals, basic materials (DOW, LYB, NUE, STLD).** Most exposed to electricity in $ terms. Pricing power is regional and cyclical; expect margin compression rather than full pass-through in 2026–2027, then a second-round price reset in 2028 as contracts roll.
- **EV / battery (TSLA, F, GM, LG-ES, Panasonic JVs).** Cell plants signed PPAs in 2022–2023 at $45–55/MWh; renewals from 2026 are clearing $75–95. This is a direct hit to LFP/NMC cost curves and will slow the EV cost-parity timeline by 12–18 months.

## 4. Macro inflation arithmetic

Industrial electricity is roughly 26% of US electricity consumption, and the manufacturing share of core goods PPI input is concentrated in the segments above. Our pass-through model:

- Direct contribution to **core goods PPI**: +70–110 bps annualized through 2027.
- Indirect contribution to **core PCE** (via durable goods, autos, appliances, and defense-adjacent procurement deflators): +25–45 bps cumulative by end-2027.
- **Fiscal channel**: incremental CHIPS / DPA / IRA appropriations required to keep reshored projects on their original IRR — we estimate $40–70 bn of additional Federal cost across FY2026–2028, financed at >4% — a small but real addition to deficit-driven term-premium pressure.

This does not derail the Fed's 2% target on a 24-month horizon, but it does mean **the last mile of disinflation in core goods is materially harder than the Bloomberg consensus path**, and the neutral rate consistent with reshored-manufacturing-led capex is 25–50 bps higher than pre-AI estimates.

## 5. The feedback loop

1. Constrained grid → large-load tariffs → higher industrial $/MWh.
2. Higher $/MWh → higher reshored capex breakeven → larger subsidy ask → bigger fiscal deficit + higher long-end yields.
3. Higher yields → higher utility WACC → utilities defer or de-scope discretionary T&D → grid stays constrained.
4. Constrained grid → AI hyperscalers accelerate BTM gas (cards 03–04) → pulls GE Vernova / Siemens slots away from utility queue → constraint deepens.
5. Loop closes; the structural inflation differential vs. EU/Japan industrial power costs **narrows the reshoring labor-arb advantage** that justified the policy in the first place.

## 6. What would break the thesis
- A genuine AI-efficiency step-change (e.g. sub-100W/effective-H100 inference) that flattens the load forecast and lets queues clear without rate-base buildout.
- A FERC / DOE intervention that socializes large-load transmission cost across all ratepayers (politically very hard in 2026 — election year residential affordability politics).
- A faster GOES/transformer capacity response than card 05 modeled (CLF + Hyundai Steel JV pulled forward; ATI converter capacity).

## 7. Trade implications
- **Long structurally**: industrial-power-cost beneficiaries with pricing power and contracted feedstock — GEV, ENR.DE, ETN, VRT (already endorsed in card 04); add **ATI, CLF** from card 05; **D, SO** from card 06.
- **Short / underweight**: energy-intensive reshored manufacturing without committed long-dated PPAs — selective EV/battery names, mid-tier chemicals, and any defense prime that has not renegotiated escalators (HII most exposed via Newport News / Pascagoula electrified yards).
- **Rates**: bias toward steeper 2s10s than the consensus terminal-rate path implies, on incremental term-premium from fiscal channel.
