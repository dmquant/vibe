# Stress-Testing the OEM "Order Lock" Against Tier-2/3 Supplier Liquidity Failure

- **Card**: 6 / 8
- **Analyst**: Industrials Analyst
- **Stance**: stress-test (of the implicit OEM-resilience claim in cards 04–05)
- **Work date (Asia/Singapore)**: 2026-05-14
- **Session topic**: Liquidity trap of the Fed leadership transition
- **Card topic**: Transmission of supplier liquidity pressure to OEM order delivery and margins

## 1. Thesis

The order book is a **revenue calendar, not a delivery guarantee**. Under a 2026 supplier-liquidity stress (SOFR ~3.60%, SLOOS tightening, Subchapter V uptick — see card-05), the large industrial OEM "Order Lock" successfully defends *book value of backlog* and *bond credit*, but it does **not** defend either the **delivery schedule** or the **near-term gross margin**. The realistic 2026–2027 stress translation is **150–400 bp of gross-margin leakage** and **3–9 months of incremental schedule slippage on critical programs**, absorbed by the OEMs *on behalf of* their balance-sheet-weak suppliers. Backlog converts to cash more slowly and at lower unit profit; it does not vanish.

## 2. Why "Order Lock" is structurally weaker than card-04 implied

Card-04 framed names like LMT, GE Aerospace, CAT, DE as a clean real-cash-flow hedge because of multi-year, EPA-clause-protected backlogs. That is correct on the **top-line / credit** axis and wrong-by-omission on the **delivery and margin** axis. Four reasons:

1. **Order Lock is a customer-side commitment, not a supplier-side capacity guarantee.** A USAF / United / Caterpillar dealer contract obligates the OEM to deliver; it does not obligate Tier-2 forging house X or Tier-3 specialty-alloy mill Y to remain solvent. The OEM owns the schedule risk by contract.
2. **Single- and sole-source concentration is structural, not cyclical.** Large titanium forgings, jet-engine isothermal forgings, precision gearboxes, aerospace-grade fasteners, hot-section castings, semiconductor-grade bearings, and certain rare-earth magnets are concentrated in 1–3 qualified suppliers globally. Re-qualifying an alternate source on a Part 21 / MIL-spec / API-spec part is a 9–24 month process, not a quarter.
3. **EPA / economic-price-adjustment clauses are *lagging* and *partial*.** They typically index to BLS PPI baskets with 1–2 quarter lag, cover 40–70% of input movement, exclude expedite/premium freight, and almost never reimburse supplier-rescue payments. The OEM eats the wedge.
4. **Working-capital becomes a balance-sheet item for the OEM.** When a Tier-2 cannot finance WIP at SOFR+550, the OEM either (a) prepays / accelerates progress payments, (b) takes title to long-lead raw material, or (c) extends a direct supplier-finance facility. All three migrate cash from OEM to supplier, expanding OEM inventory days and contract assets and depressing FCF conversion.

## 3. Where the leakage shows up — channel map

| Channel | Mechanism | Quantified 2026–27 stress translation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Schedule slip** | Sole-source Tier-2/3 misses delivery; alternate qualification 9–24 mo | Aerospace OEM deliveries −5% to −12% vs. plan; defense LRIP lots slip 1–2 quarters; CAT/DE backlog conversion velocity −10 to −20% |
| **Premium freight + expedite fees** | Air vs. ocean, hot-shot trucking, weekend tool changeovers | 30–80 bp of COGS, non-recoverable via EPA |
| **Supplier rescue / prepay** | OEM funds Tier-2 WIP, takes title to long-lead steel/Ti/Inconel | Inventory days +15–30; FCF conversion drops from ~95–100% of NI to 70–80% |
| **Re-source qualification cost** | NRE, first-article inspection, tooling duplication | 40–120 bp of program gross margin on affected SKUs |
| **Quality escapes** | Distressed suppliers cut corners; rework, scrap, warranty up | 20–60 bp warranty accrual creep; episodic charges (cf. 2018–19 CFM LEAP HPT) |
| **Mix degradation** | OEM forced to deliver lower-margin units first to honor priority customers | Aftermarket / spares mix delayed; 50–150 bp blended GM headwind |

Aggregate: a **realistic 2026 stress translation of 150–400 bp of gross-margin compression**, concentrated H2-2026 into H1-2027, **without** any program cancellation. This is *backlog convertible at a worse rate*, not *backlog destroyed*.

## 4. Who is more vs. less defended

**More defended (Order Lock works closer to nameplate)**
- **LMT, RTX (defense segment), GD Mission Systems, NOC** — USG-funded progress payments, DPAS Rating priority, DoD Title III industrial-base subsidies for critical suppliers; backlog book-to-bill > 1.2x with sticky customer.
- **GE Aerospace** — captive aftermarket (CFM/LEAP shop visits) is the cash engine and is relatively supplier-light vs. OE production; spares pricing power offsets some OE leakage.
- **DE (Deere)** — has been actively de-Tiering (insourcing castings, dual-sourcing electronics post-2021–22) and large-ag mix is high-margin enough to absorb 100–200 bp.

**Less defended (Order Lock partially abstracts a real schedule risk)**
- **BA commercial** — 737 / 787 ramp depends on Spirit AeroSystems integration, CFM/LEAP engines, and a fragile fastener/forging tail; any Tier-2 cash event hits monthly delivery rate directly.
- **CAT Construction Industries** — dealer-floorplan-financed, exposed to dealer credit at high SOFR; backlog less sticky than defense.
- **Pure-play eVTOL / new-energy capex names** — Order Lock is partly LOIs and reservations, not firm-fixed-price backlog; weakest layer.

## 5. What we are watching (2026-05-14 monitor set)

1. **OEM disclosure**: "supplier health," "supplier financial support," "advance to suppliers," "contract assets" line items in 10-Q — flagged as the leading indicator.
2. **Inventory days vs. revenue**: 4-quarter trailing — a >2σ widening at LMT / GE Aerospace / BA / CAT signals OEM is absorbing supplier WIP.
3. **DPAS / DPA Title III activations**: DoD use of Defense Production Act for critical-supplier rescue (forgings, microelectronics, rare-earth magnets) — a public signal the channel has fired.
4. **AIA / GAMA / AEM industry shipment data**: month-over-month deliveries vs. plan; aerospace book-to-bill.
5. **Tier-2 specialist credit**: spreads / 13D activity / Subchapter V filings at named suppliers (Howmet exposure list, Spirit AeroSystems supply chain, specialty-alloy mills).
6. **EPA clause clawbacks**: language tightening in new prime contracts — would indicate OEMs trying to push pressure back upstream.

## 6. Conclusion — re-rating, not de-rating

Order Lock is a **partial** shock absorber, not a full one. The right 2026 framing for the OEM trade:

- **Bond / credit thesis intact**: LMT, GE Aero, CAT, DE remain investment-grade clean; supplier stress does *not* threaten parent solvency.
- **Equity earnings path re-rates**: expect 2026–27 consensus gross margins to be revised **−150 to −400 bp** at programs with concentrated Tier-2/3 exposure; FCF conversion to drop ~15–20 pp; delivery cadence to slip 3–9 months on affected programs.
- **Best-defended subsegments**: defense primes with USG progress payments, captive aftermarket (GE Aero), and OEMs that pre-emptively in-sourced (DE).
- **Most exposed**: BA commercial supply chain, dealer-floorplan-financed construction OEMs, eVTOL / pre-revenue capex names.
- **Trade implication**: prefer GE Aerospace + LMT + DE over BA + pure-play construction; pair-trade defense primes long vs. small-/mid-cap aerospace Tier-2 short to harvest the supplier-stress wedge.

This *supports* card-04's structural call (large industrial primes are the cleanest real-cash-flow hedge in public equity) and *refines* card-05's tail-risk framing (the supplier-driven default cycle shows up first as OEM **margin and schedule** noise, not as prime-OEM **credit** noise).

## 7. Handoff

The most concrete unanswered question is the *macro-policy* response: will the US government activate DPA Title III / TFL / industrial-base programs at scale to backstop critical Tier-2/3 suppliers (forgings, specialty alloys, rare-earth magnets, semiconductor-grade ball bearings) before schedule slips become unrecoverable? That sits squarely in the policy analyst's domain (industrial policy, export controls, DPA).
