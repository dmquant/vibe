# Global Energy Price Pulse and Manufacturing Inflation Transmission - 2026-05-11
**Date:** 2026-05-11 (Asia/Singapore)  
**Analyst:** energy-analyst | Energy Analyst  
**Stance:** support  

As of 2026-05-11, I support the prior card's conclusion that the energy shock strengthens the "higher-for-longer" policy floor, especially for the Fed and the ECB, while leaving Japan most exposed through import costs and JPY weakness. The important change is that the second energy pulse is no longer confined to headline fuel prices: it is now passing through diesel, LNG, freight, fertilizers, chemicals and supplier delivery times into manufacturing input prices.

## Executive Summary
The energy shock is behaving like a cost-push tax on global manufacturing. EIA's April 2026 Short-Term Energy Outlook put March Brent at $103/b and expected a 2Q26 peak near $115/b, with retail diesel above $5.80/gal in April and an average of $4.80/gal for 2026. The LNG channel is more asymmetric: EIA reported that, by the week ending April 24, TTF futures were $14.80/MMBtu, 35% above pre-closure levels, while JKM was $16.02/MMBtu, 51% higher; Henry Hub was down 9% because the US cannot quickly export enough gas to transmit the full global scarcity into domestic prices. This means Europe and Asia face a stronger manufacturing cost impulse than the US.

The passthrough is already visible in surveys. S&P Global's May 8 global PMI note said April manufacturing input costs posted the sharpest spike since June 2022, supplier delivery delays were the worst since August 2022, and safety-stock buying rose to the highest level since June 2022. In the US, April ISM manufacturing prices paid reached 84.6, the highest since April 2022, while employment stayed in contraction. In China, official April PMI stayed expansionary at 50.3, but raw-material purchase prices were elevated at 63.7 and factory-gate prices at 55.1. This is the classic stagflationary mix for central banks: weaker labor demand and orders coexist with input-cost persistence.

## Transmission Map
| Channel | Current evidence as of 2026-05-11 | Inflation implication |
|---|---:|---|
| Crude and refined products | Brent averaged $103/b in March; EIA expected $115/b peak in 2Q26; US diesel forecast above $5.80/gal in April | Raises freight, mining, agriculture, petrochemical and distribution costs quickly |
| LNG and power | TTF +35% and JKM +51% versus pre-Hormuz closure; over 10 Bcf/d of LNG supply affected | Hits European chemicals, metals, glass, paper, ceramics and Asian gas-fired power costs |
| Supply chain timing | Global supplier delivery delays worst since August 2022 | Extends the lag from energy shock to goods prices by forcing inventory building |
| Fertilizers and petrochemicals | World Bank noted a more than 50% m/m urea jump in March | Spills into food costs, plastics, packaging and chemical intermediates |
| FX import cost | USD/JPY and USD/CNY pressure amplify local-currency energy import prices | Makes Japan most exposed; China more insulated by coal and managed FX |

## Regional Read-Through
**United States:** The US has a partial energy shield because domestic gas is insulated and the country is a major hydrocarbon producer, but the shield is incomplete. Global crude, diesel, jet fuel, chemicals and freight are priced internationally. The Fed's April 29 statement explicitly said inflation is elevated partly because of global energy prices and held the federal funds target at 3.50%-3.75%. That makes an early easing cycle hard to justify unless labor-market cooling becomes much sharper than the April NFP signal already discussed in card 01.

**Euro area:** Europe faces the cleanest cost-push problem. The ECB held deposit, main refinancing and marginal lending rates at 2.00%, 2.15% and 2.40% on April 30, but its statement said upside inflation risks and downside growth risks had intensified. Euro area inflation rose to 3.0% in April from 2.6% in March and 1.9% in February, with energy inflation jumping to 10.9%. Because European gas and power prices are closer to marginal LNG pricing, energy passthrough into manufacturing is more direct than in the US.

**Japan:** The BoJ is still the most fragile policy mix, consistent with card 03. On April 28, the BoJ kept the uncollateralized overnight call rate around 0.75% by a 6-3 vote, while three dissenters preferred 1.0% because price risks were skewed upward. Japan's problem is not only oil; it is local-currency oil and LNG. If USD/JPY stays elevated, the energy pulse is imported twice: first through dollar commodity prices, then through yen depreciation.

**China:** China is less fragile on energy availability because of coal, state-directed procurement and discounted non-Western flows, but its export manufacturing base cannot fully avoid global prices for oil-linked chemicals, shipping and industrial metals. The April PMI combination - manufacturing PMI at 50.3, raw-material purchase prices at 63.7 and factory-gate prices at 55.1 - points to margin compression first, followed by selective export-price passthrough if demand holds.

## Policy Judgment
The second energy pulse supports the thread's core thesis: labor-market cooling alone is not enough to unlock rapid monetary easing when manufacturing input costs are accelerating. For central banks, the decision rule shifts from "look through energy" to "look through only if indirect effects remain contained." The evidence is moving the other way: supplier delays, diesel, LNG, chemicals and fertilizers are all creating broader cost pressure.

The Fed can still be patient rather than hawkish because US gas insulation and domestic production soften the shock, but the April 29 dissent pattern shows discomfort with easing language. The ECB is closer to a forced pause-or-hike debate because energy is already pushing euro area headline inflation above target. The BoJ faces the worst contradiction: rate hikes would support the yen and damp import inflation, but they also threaten domestic demand and debt-service dynamics.

## Investment and Macro Implications
- **Rates:** Energy passthrough raises the hurdle for Fed and ECB cuts through summer 2026. The near-term risk is fewer cuts, not a smooth disinflation trade.
- **FX:** This supports the prior preference for expressing USD strength through USD/JPY more than broad DXY, because Japan has the most direct energy-import and policy-constraint exposure.
- **Manufacturing margins:** The first-order loser is margin, not necessarily final demand. Firms with pricing power in chemicals, packaging, transport equipment and industrial components can pass through; commodity-like downstream producers face spread compression.
- **China exports:** China's lower coal-based power costs help, but imported oil-linked inputs and freight costs narrow the export deflation channel that previously helped global goods disinflation.
