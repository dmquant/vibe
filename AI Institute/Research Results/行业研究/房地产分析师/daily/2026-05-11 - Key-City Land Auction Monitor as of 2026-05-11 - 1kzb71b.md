# Key-City Land Auction Monitor (as of 2026-05-11)

## Scope

- Date anchor: I first ran `date +%Y-%m-%d`; the result was **2026-05-11**.
- Monitoring window: **2026-05-05 to 2026-05-11**, using public completed-auction information available by the shell date.
- Cities covered: Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Hangzhou, Chengdu, Suzhou, Nanjing and other key tier-1/tier-2 cities.
- Metric definition: completed auctions are counted only where public data are verifiable. The “weekly failed-auction rate” below is an observed rate for this report’s key-city completed sample; it is not the full 300-city weekly failed-auction rate from CIH or CRIC.

## Prioritised Conclusions

1. **Completed key-city residential land auctions were calm this week.** The verifiable completed sample is mainly Shenzhen’s Longgang Longcheng Street G01046-0106 parcel. Shenzhen Runzhen Real Estate Co., Ltd., a China Resources Land vehicle, acquired it at the reserve price of **RMB 7.045 billion**, with a transacted blended floor price of **RMB 14,776/sqm** and a premium rate of **0%**.[^1][^2]
2. **The sample failed-auction rate is 0%, but the statistic has limited weight.** The completed sample has **1 successful parcel and 0 failed parcels**, so the observed failed-auction rate is **0%**. Because 2026-05-11 is a Monday and the post-Labour-Day auction rhythm has not fully resumed, this should be read only as “no public failed-auction case was found in the key-city completed sample,” not as a national market failed-auction rate.
3. **Land buyers remain concentrated among strong-credit developers.** China Resources Land is the buyer in this week’s sample. At the end of April, Beijing’s three residential parcels were taken by China Resources Land and China Jinmao, with China Resources Land acquiring roughly **RMB 7.06 billion** across two parcels. This reinforces that high-ticket core-city sites are still mainly absorbed by central SOEs, local SOEs and strong-credit platforms.[^3]
4. **This week’s low-premium completed transaction is not enough to call a May cooling trend.** CIH data show the 300-city average premium rate rose to **8.9%** in April, with several residential parcels in Shenzhen, Shanghai and Hangzhou transacting at premiums of **40% or above**. The real May test will come from later auctions in Beijing, Shanghai, Suzhou and Nanjing.[^4]

## Analytical Read-Through

- **Premium rate:** the completed sample shows **0%** for this week, a low short-term reading mainly explained by the tiny sample and auction calendar timing. A genuine cooling call would require later-May core sites in Beijing, Shanghai and Suzhou to keep selling at reserve prices.
- **Failed-auction rate:** the observed failed-auction rate in the completed key-city sample is **0%**. The more important signal will be whether non-core or high-ticket parcels see weak registration, withdrawn listings or reserve-price-only outcomes.
- **Land buyers:** China Resources Land is the only buyer in this week’s completed sample and also continued land banking in Beijing at the end of April. Strong-credit central SOEs and local SOEs remain the main absorbers of core land supply; private-sector participation needs confirmation from upcoming Hongkou, Hangzhou and Suzhou SIP auctions.
- **Investment implication:** the land market is not in a broad recovery. Pricing power is concentrated in “core city, core submarket, small-and-beautiful or low-risk” parcels. Developers are still prioritising sell-through certainty, manageable ticket size and funding safety.
