# Card 04 — Utilities: Can Generation and Interconnection Capacity Monetize the AI Substation Buildout?

- Board: 2e229809-f290-491d-bba7-3b06f10b0d3e | Card 4/8 | Work-date: **2026-05-11** (Asia/Singapore)
- Analyst: Utilities Analyst | Stance: **support**
- Builds on: Card 01 (Jevons effect and inference-led power demand), Card 02 (40% Industrials / 35% TMT / 25% Utilities allocation), Card 03 (2026–2028 transformer/UHV equipment order visibility)

## 1. Verdict

**Support, with medium-high conviction (0.72).** The utilities sleeve is justified, but only if it is framed as a time-staggered AI power monetization basket rather than a generic defensive utility bucket. Equipment can be delivered faster than firm power, so the load realization rate is not 100%. My base case is that by end-2028 utilities can physically serve **65–75% of contracted/vetted AI load** and only **40–50% of gross announced or queue-implied AI load** globally. The bottleneck is not just generation MW; it is deliverable capacity, interconnection studies, local transmission, reactive power, cooling water, gas pipeline capacity, and rate-case approval.

This is still sufficient for a **25% utilities allocation** because the return stream is different from the 40% industrial sleeve. Industrials earn first, through 2026–2028 equipment shipments and margin expansion. Utilities earn later, through 2027–2030 load energization, capacity prices, grid rate base, large-load tariffs, and flexible-generation capacity payments. The position therefore complements the industrial sleeve in time and cash-flow profile rather than simply duplicating the same AI infrastructure beta.

## 2. Load Realization Framework

I define realization as: **MW of AI/data-center load actually energized and supplied at intended load factor by end-2028 divided by MW implied by the substation, transformer, UHV/HVDC, or interconnection pipeline**.

| Region / market | Gross AI-load signal | 2026–2028 bottleneck | End-2028 realization of gross signal | End-2028 realization of vetted/contracted load | Investment read-through |
|---|---:|---|---:|---:|---|
| PJM | Very large data-center additions embedded in the load forecast; 2027/2028 capacity auction short of target | Capacity accreditation, transmission deliverability, queue reform, winter reliability | **50–60%** | **75–85%** | Strongest U.S. capacity-price signal; prioritize nuclear, gas, storage, transmission owners |
| ERCOT | Large-load requests far above realistic near-term supply; ERCOT applies derates/delays in planning | Energy-only scarcity, interconnection, West/Texas transmission, gas dispatchability | **25–35% of raw queue** | **55–65% of adjusted forecast** | High volatility; better for flexible generation, batteries, behind-the-meter deals, wires |
| China East Grid / Central China / Southern Grid | State-directed data-center and industrial electrification load, with UHV imports | Peak-hour tightness, local distribution upgrades, provincial dispatch, hydrology | **60–70% of announced plan** | **75–85% of approved/allocated projects** | Higher physical delivery confidence than U.S. queues; lower explicit ROE upside |
| Global base case | Hyperscaler and sovereign AI plans exceed deliverable grid capacity | Grid interconnection and firm power lag equipment | **40–50%** | **65–75%** | Bullish for utilities, but not a straight-line demand pass-through |

The important point is asymmetry. A 50% realization of the gross data-center queue is not bearish for utilities if prices clear at scarcity levels and connection contracts shift stranded-asset risk to the customer. It is bearish only for developers underwriting every announced AI campus as fully energized by 2028.

## 3. PJM: The Clearest Capacity-Price Transmission

PJM is the cleanest proof that AI load can become utility earnings rather than remaining a demand narrative.

- PJM's updated long-term forecast, published in January 2026, projects materially higher load growth, with data-center demand as the main driver in several zones. PJM explicitly links the forecast to large-load concentration and long-term transmission planning.[^pjm-forecast]
- The 2026/2027 Base Residual Auction cleared **134,311 MW** at **$329.17/MW-day** for the RTO zone, following the 2025/2026 auction clearing price of **$269.92/MW-day**.[^pjm-202627]
- The 2027/2028 auction again cleared near the price cap at **$333.44/MW-day** for the RTO zone, procured **134,479 MW**, and PJM reported a **6,623 MW** shortfall versus the reliability requirement after accounting for Fixed Resource Requirement resources.[^pjm-202728]

The earnings math is straightforward. A 1 GW UCAP resource at **$28.92/MW-day** earned roughly **$10.6 million/year** in capacity revenue. At **$329.17/MW-day**, it earns about **$120.1 million/year**; at **$333.44/MW-day**, about **$121.7 million/year**. The uplift is not a 25–75 bp regulated-ROE story; it is a step change in capacity revenue for cleared generation.

For regulated utilities, the ROE impact is more gradual. Base-case U.S. electric allowed ROE uplift is **25–75 bp** in tight jurisdictions where data-center load forces grid expansion and regulators approve higher risk compensation. The larger effect is rate-base CAGR moving from roughly **6–8%** to **8–11%** for the exposed wires companies, plus special large-load tariffs that require minimum demand charges, collateral, or exit fees. Dominion, AEP, and other data-center-heavy utilities have been pushing tariff structures that make hyperscalers pay for local grid reinforcement if load is delayed or cancelled.[^large-load]

PJM conclusion: realizable load is below gross pipeline, but price realization is high. That is exactly the profile needed for the utilities sleeve.

## 4. ERCOT: Massive Queue, Lower Certainty, Higher Optionality

ERCOT is not PJM with a different map. It has no forward capacity market, so AI load monetization comes through scarcity pricing, bilateral PPAs, tolling contracts, batteries, private-wire structures, and regulated transmission investment.

ERCOT planning documents and public discussions show very large large-load interest from data centers, crypto, industrials, and electrification. The key adjustment is that ERCOT does not treat every request as near-term firm demand. Planning practice applies haircuts and timing delays to large flexible loads, which is why I mark raw-queue realization at only **25–35%** by end-2028 and adjusted-forecast realization at **55–65%**.

This makes ERCOT more volatile but still investable:

- Flexible gas, batteries, and demand response can earn from scarcity hours even if only part of the AI queue materializes.
- Transmission and distribution owners still earn from interconnection work, substations, and local network upgrades.
- Load-serving arrangements with minimum payments or behind-the-meter generation are more important than headline MW announcements.

ERCOT conclusion: do not pay for the raw queue. Pay for flexibility and contracted deliverability.

## 5. China: Higher Physical Delivery, Lower Explicit ROE Upside

China's grid question is different from the U.S. question. The U.S. market has more price volatility but weaker planning coordination. China has stronger state coordination, UHV planning, and dispatch authority, but lower explicit regulated-return upside.

The official 2026 national power-supply outlook says supply-demand balance is broadly secure nationally, while **East China, Central China, Southern China and parts of North China** can still face peak-hour tightness, especially in summer and winter peak periods.[^nea-2026] That matches the investment thesis: China is not short annual energy; it is short deliverable peak capacity and interregional transfer during stressed hours.

The capacity-price mechanism helps flexible generation. China's coal capacity tariff framework moves most provinces from compensation equal to **30% of a CNY330/kW-year benchmark in 2024–2025** toward **50% from 2026**, or about **CNY165/kW-year** for eligible units, with higher levels in tight provinces.[^china-capacity] For a 1 GW eligible coal unit, moving from 30% to 50% of the benchmark is about **CNY65 million/year** of incremental pre-tax capacity revenue. That can add roughly **100–200 bp** to project-level ROE for flexible coal/gas assets in tight provinces, depending on leverage, utilization, and coal spreads.

For grid companies, the formal allowed return does not need to rise sharply. The value comes from asset-base growth, higher utilization, UHV/HVDC imports into East/South load centers, and power-market services. State Grid and China Southern Power Grid investment programs keep the physical-delivery probability higher than in U.S. interconnection queues, but the listed-equity upside is more tied to equipment, grid automation, power generation, and ancillary services than to a clean regulated-ROE re-rating.

China conclusion: **75–85%** realization for approved/allocated AI and data-center projects is achievable by 2028, while gross local-government or corporate announcements should be discounted to **60–70%**.

## 6. Capacity Price / ROE Uplift by Channel

| Channel | Uplift estimate | Confidence | Best beneficiaries |
|---|---:|---|---|
| PJM cleared capacity revenue | From **$28.92/MW-day** to **$329–333/MW-day** for RTO capacity years; about **+$110 million/year per 1 GW UCAP** versus the low-price baseline | High | CEG, VST, TLN, NRG, nuclear/gas-heavy portfolios |
| U.S. regulated electric allowed ROE | **+25–75 bp** in data-center-heavy jurisdictions; more often achieved through rate base and tariffs than formal ROE step-up | Medium | D, AEP, FE, PEG, EXC wires, selected transmission owners |
| U.S. rate-base growth | Exposed utilities move from **6–8%** to **8–11%** rate-base CAGR where large-load tariffs protect cost recovery | Medium-high | Regulated wires, transmission, local distribution |
| ERCOT scarcity / flexibility | Not a capacity price; upside through scarcity spreads, tolling, batteries, PPAs and co-located generation | Medium | Flexible gas, batteries, wires, contracted C&I load suppliers |
| China coal/flexible capacity tariff | From 30% to 50% of **CNY330/kW-year** benchmark from 2026 in most provinces; **~CNY65 million/year per eligible 1 GW** incremental revenue | Medium-high | Huaneng, Huadian, Datang, Guangdong/Shanghai/Anhui flexible generators |
| China regulated grid return | Formal ROE uplift only **0–50 bp**; upside mainly from asset-base growth and utilization | Medium | State-grid value chain, NARI 600406.SH, grid automation, provincial generators |

This is enough to support the 25% sleeve, but the source of return differs by market. In the U.S., price and tariffs do more of the work. In China, volume delivery, capacity compensation, and regulated capex do more of the work.

## 7. Portfolio Implication for the 25% Utilities Sleeve

The right utilities basket is not a broad defensive ETF. It should be an AI-load monetization basket:

- **40% regulated wires and transmission:** D, AEP, EXC, PEG, FE, selected China grid-automation names such as NARI 600406.SH.
- **35% capacity and flexible generation:** CEG, VST, TLN, NRG, Huaneng / Huadian / Datang, plus regional flexible generators with capacity-tariff exposure.
- **15% batteries, demand response, and contracted flexibility:** storage developers, aggregators, tolling-backed portfolios, and behind-the-meter generation partners.
- **10% water / cooling / environmental infrastructure:** regulated water utilities and wastewater/cooling suppliers near data-center clusters. This is smaller, but it matters because cooling-water permits can delay AI campuses even when electricity is available.

Expected 2026–2028 annualized return contribution for the sleeve is **8–11%** in the base case: lower than the 40% industrial sleeve's upside, but with later realization, stronger cash yield, and better protection if AI equipment multiples compress before the load is fully energized. In the bull case, where PJM scarcity remains capped and China peak-load tightness persists, the utilities sleeve can compound at **12–16%**. In the bear case, where hyperscaler demand pauses and regulators reject cost recovery, the sleeve still has a **4–6%** dividend/rate-base floor, provided single-name merchant exposure is controlled.

## 8. Risks

1. **Regulatory pushback:** Regulators may force hyperscalers, not ordinary ratepayers, to absorb grid-upgrade costs. That is correct policy, but it can delay utility earnings recognition.
2. **Load deferral:** AI inference demand may still rise, but a specific data-center campus can be delayed by water, gas, zoning, or transmission studies.
3. **Overpaying for merchant generators:** Capacity-price scarcity is visible and already partly capitalized in CEG, VST, TLN, and NRG.
4. **China tariff discipline:** China can prioritize power-system stability and industrial policy over listed-equity margin expansion.
5. **Fuel and weather:** Gas deliverability, coal spreads, hydrology, and heat waves can change realized earnings more than headline AI load.

## 9. Bottom Line and Handoff

Card 04 supports the Card 02 allocation. The utilities sleeve should stay at **25%**, not because every AI substation becomes fully loaded by 2028, but because partial realization at scarcity-clearing prices is already enough to lift capacity revenue, rate base, and flexible-generation returns. The correct framing is:

**Industrials monetize the buildout first; utilities monetize the energized load later.** That sequencing makes utilities a time-staggered complement to the 40% industrial sleeve.

The next useful card should move from sector validation to portfolio construction: how to rebalance the 40% Industrials / 35% TMT / 25% Utilities basket as equipment margins, TMT capex expectations, and capacity-price earnings peak at different times.
