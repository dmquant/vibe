# Utilities Analyst Research Report: AI Load Contractualization & Grid Reliability

**Analyst:** Utilities Analyst [specialist]

**Status:** Synthesize

## 1. Executive Summary: From "Growth Option" to "Contractual Liability"
As of May 11, 2026, the utility sector has bifurcated. The NERC Level 3 Alert issued earlier this quarter has ended the era of "growth-at-any-cost" for data centers (DCs). We are seeing a transition from a volume-based valuation model to a **quality-of-contract** model. The market is now penalizing utilities that have massive DC interconnection queues but lack robust "Take-or-Pay" clauses or credit-collateralized infrastructure recovery.

The primary investment theme is **"Regulatory Insulation"**: the ability to grow the rate base through DC-driven transmission/generation capex while shielding residential ratepayers from "affordability blowback" (cross-subsidization risk).

## 2. Tier 1: The Contractual Fortresses (Dominion & Southern Co)
These companies have successfully implemented "Large Load Riders" that de-risk the investment cycle.

### Dominion Energy (D)
*   **Contract Quality:** Dominion's 2025-approved "Data Center Infrastructure Rider" requires DC customers to provide 10-year "Minimum Take-or-Pay" (MToP) commitments. Crucially, they must now post liquid collateral for 50% of the substation capex upfront.
*   **Capex Recovery:** Dominion’s $10B+ transmission plan is nearly 100% recoverable under Virginia's friendly regulatory framework. 
*   **Affordability Shield:** By front-loading DC capital contributions, Dominion has kept residential rate increases below 3% CAGR, minimizing political risk in Richmond.

### Southern Company (SO)
*   **Contract Quality:** Georgia Power’s latest Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) updates include a "High-Load Growth" tariff that ties DC pricing to marginal generation costs rather than average system costs.
*   **Nuclear Premium:** With Vogtle 3 & 4 fully operational, SO is the only Southeast utility offering "Firm Zero-Carbon Baseload." This allows them to command a 15-20% reliability premium in their PPA contracts with hyperscalers.

## 3. Tier 2: The Scarcity Rent Plays (Constellation & Vistra)
Independent Power Producers (IPPs) with nuclear assets are capturing "Scarcity Rent" through co-location.

### Constellation Energy (CEG)
*   **Model:** The "Crane Clean Energy Center" (TMI Unit 1) restart remains the industry benchmark. By May 2026, CEG has signed three additional "Behind-the-Meter" co-location deals, totaling 2.4 GW of firm power.
*   **Risk:** The primary risk is the "Grid Exit Fee" debate in PJM. However, CEG’s contracts are structured to pass regulatory "transmission-use" costs directly to the DC tenant, preserving their 50%+ EBITDA margin on these deals.

## 4. The Vulnerability Zone: Affordability Blowback & Stranded Asset Risk
Companies in jurisdictions with weak "Large Load" protections are facing a "Perfect Storm" of rising capacity costs and residential ratepayer revolts.

### American Electric Power (AEP) & Entergy (ETR)
*   **AEP (Ohio/Indiana):** Facing severe regulatory pushback. The Ohio PUC is investigating "Residential Cost Shifting," threatening to disallow recovery of DC-specific transmission upgrades unless AEP can prove direct benefit to local residents.
*   **Entergy (Mississippi/Louisiana):** While having a large DC pipeline, ETR lacks the credit-collateral requirements of Dominion. A market downturn or AI-scaling slowdown could leave ETR with "Stranded Assets" (overbuilt transmission for DCs that never materialized).

## 6. Conclusion
The "Winners" of the 2026 AI load cycle are not those with the most megawatts in the queue, but those with the most **legal protection** in the contract. We favor **Dominion (D)** and **Southern (SO)** for their superior regulatory shielding. We remain cautious on **AEP** due to escalating political friction in the PJM region regarding residential rate parity.
