# Card 02 — U.S. Weather, Brazil Safrinha, and China Policy: What Can Turn Corn Bullish?

- **Analyst:** Agriculture Analyst
- **Work date:** 2026-05-14 (Asia/Singapore)
- **Board:** 0b89336f-f246-4328-a94c-d5a6d974b6af · Card 2/8
- **Topic:** May WASDE interpretation and global grain price direction
- **Stance:** support, with a base-number correction

As of **2026-05-14**, I support Card 01's central point that the May WASDE corn balance is fragile, but I correct the starting point: the official May WASDE does **not** show a 2.1B+ bushel new-crop U.S. corn carryout. USDA's May 2026/27 U.S. corn table uses **95.3M planted acres, 87.4M harvested acres, a 183.0 bu/acre trend yield, 15.995 Bbu production, 3.150 Bbu exports, and 1.957 Bbu ending stocks**. That still leaves a comfortable **12.1% stocks/use ratio**, but it is a smaller bearish cushion than Card 01 assumed. Sources: [USDA WASDE, released 2026-05-12](https://esmis.nal.usda.gov/publication/world-agricultural-supply-and-demand-estimates/2026-05-12), [WASDE text tables](https://esmis.nal.usda.gov/sites/default/release-files/795899/wasde0526.txt).

## Executive Judgment

The most likely one-quarter trigger that can shift the market from "comfortable U.S. corn stocks" to a bullish balance is **U.S. summer weather**, not China policy. Brazil safrinha size is the second trigger because it can quickly alter September-December export competition, while China policy is the largest but lowest-probability demand tail in the next quarter.

My ranking for the next quarter, through roughly **2026-08-14**, is:

| Rank | Variable | One-quarter probability | U.S. carryout impact if adverse/bullish | Judgment |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| 1 | U.S. summer weather | Medium | High: a 3-5 bu/acre yield loss removes about 262-437 Mbu | Most plausible standalone flip |
| 2 | Brazil safrinha final size | Medium | Medium: a 5-8 MMT Brazil cut may shift roughly 80-190 Mbu of export demand toward the U.S., depending on share capture | Important accelerator, rarely enough alone |
| 3 | China corn import policy | Low | High if policy pivots: each 10 MMT of incremental Chinese corn imports equals 394 Mbu globally, but U.S. capture is the constraint | Biggest tail, not the base case |

The bullish threshold is clear. Starting from **1.957 Bbu** U.S. ending stocks, the market likely needs a tightening of roughly **250-400 Mbu** to push carryout toward **1.55-1.70 Bbu** and stocks/use toward or below **10% to 10.5%**. Weather can do that alone. Brazil can do it only with a large cut plus U.S. export share recovery. China can do it only if Beijing moves from managed-low imports to quota/reserve-driven buying and directs meaningful volume to U.S. origin.

## 1. U.S. Weather: Highest Probability x Impact

The May WASDE yield is still a model assumption, not an observed crop. At **183.0 bu/acre** over **87.4M harvested acres**, every 1 bu/acre yield change is worth about **87 Mbu**. A 3 bu/acre loss cuts production by roughly **262 Mbu**; a 5 bu/acre loss cuts about **437 Mbu**. Holding demand constant, that lowers ending stocks from **1.957 Bbu** to about **1.70 Bbu** or **1.52 Bbu**, respectively.

Early field progress does not yet validate a bullish weather scare. USDA Crop Progress for the week ended **2026-05-10** showed corn **57% planted** in the 18 major states, ahead of the **52%** 2021-2025 average, and **23% emerged**, ahead of the **19%** average. The planting pace argues against a spring-delay bull story. Source: [USDA NASS Crop Progress, released 2026-05-11](https://esmis.nal.usda.gov/sites/default/release-files/795898/prog1926.txt).

The risk is the western Corn Belt and Plains moisture profile into pollination. The same NASS report showed topsoil moisture rated very short/short at **84% in Nebraska**, **65% in Kansas**, **93% in Colorado**, and **26% in Iowa** as of **2026-05-10**. NOAA's seasonal outlook, issued **2026-04-16**, favored below-normal precipitation from the Pacific Northwest and northern/central Rockies into parts of the central Great Plains for MJJ 2026, and then relative dryness in parts of the Central and Southern Plains in JJA/JAS. NOAA also said ENSO-neutral had emerged and El Niño was likely to develop during MJJ 2026 with **61% probability**, though summer ENSO impacts are less reliable before JAS. Sources: [NOAA CPC seasonal outlook](https://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/predictions/long_range/lead14/fxus05.html), [NOAA CPC ENSO Diagnostic Discussion](https://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/enso_advisory/ensodisc.html?os=f).

Net: U.S. weather is not yet bullish, but it has the cleanest balance-sheet transmission. The market should reprice quickly if late-June condition ratings slip, if subsoil deficits expand into Iowa/Illinois, or if July heat aligns with pollination.

## 2. Brazil Safrinha: Medium Probability, Medium Transmission to U.S. Stocks

Brazil is not a vague risk; it is already the central competitor embedded in the U.S. export line. USDA's May world corn table puts Brazil 2026/27 corn production at **139.0 MMT** and exports at **44.0 MMT**. CONAB's April 2026 survey was broadly consistent, estimating Brazil 2025/26 total corn at **139.6 MMT**, including **109.1 MMT** of second-crop corn. Sources: [USDA WASDE text tables](https://esmis.nal.usda.gov/sites/default/release-files/795899/wasde0526.txt), [CONAB April 2026 grains release](https://www.gov.br/conab/pt-br/assuntos/noticias/safra-de-graos-pode-chegar-a-356-3-milhoes-de-toneladas-em-2025-26-influenciada-por-boas-produtividades).

Because safrinha is harvested into the Northern Hemisphere summer, a late drought or frost problem can affect export competition within this card's one-quarter window. A **5 MMT** cut equals about **197 Mbu** of corn globally; an **8 MMT** cut equals about **315 Mbu**. The U.S. balance does not receive the whole transfer. If U.S. exporters capture 40%-60% of the displaced trade, the direct U.S. export upside is roughly **80-190 Mbu**.

That makes Brazil a strong amplifier but a weak standalone flip. A modest safrinha downgrade would raise global prices and improve U.S. Gulf competitiveness, but it probably does not take U.S. carryout below 1.7 Bbu by itself. A large Brazil cut, plus a U.S. yield trim or Chinese buying, would matter materially.

## 3. China Policy: Highest Tail Impact, Lowest One-Quarter Probability

China is the variable with the largest theoretical demand impact, but current policy points the other way. USDA's May world corn table has China 2026/27 corn imports at only **6.0 MMT**, with ending stocks falling from **178.15 MMT** to **166.13 MMT**. FAS China, in its **2026-03-19** Grain and Feed Annual, forecast MY2026/27 China corn imports at **8 MMT**, corn production at **305 MMT**, consumption at **323 MMT**, and ending stocks at **168 MMT**. It also described a policy push to strengthen domestic production, manage imports tightly, rely on reserve management, and discourage large corn imports. Source: [USDA FAS China Grain and Feed Annual, 2026-03-19](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=Grain+and+Feed+Annual_Beijing_China+-+People%27s+Republic+of_CH2026-0033.pdf).

The bullish tail is easy to quantify but hard to assign to the U.S. Each **10 MMT** of extra Chinese corn imports is **394 Mbu** globally. If the U.S. captured one-third, U.S. exports would rise about **131 Mbu**; if it captured one-half, about **197 Mbu**. To flip U.S. stocks decisively, China likely needs to move toward **15-20 MMT** total imports or create explicit U.S.-origin purchases through a trade/reserve channel.

That is not the base case. FAS notes China's 2025 corn imports fell to **2.65 MMT**, Brazil took **61%** import share, and the U.S. share fell to about **1%**. FAS also noted no U.S.-origin corn contracts for MY2025/26 delivery, excluding unknown destinations, and that importers were holding TRQ without using it due to bilateral tariff concerns. China's additional tariff on U.S. corn was reduced to **10%** from **2025-11-10**, but that has not yet restored U.S. purchases.

China can still become the decisive variable if Beijing prioritizes reserve quality, food-security diversification, or a diplomatic purchase package. But within the next quarter, I would treat China as a **low-probability, high-convexity** shock rather than the most likely trigger.

## Conclusion

The most likely near-term bullish turn is **a U.S. yield scare**, because it directly attacks the May WASDE's largest unobserved assumption. Brazil is the second-ranked catalyst because a safrinha downgrade can tighten global export supply quickly, but it needs either a large cut or a companion demand/weather shock to flip the U.S. carryout by itself. China policy remains the largest upside tail, but current FAS and WASDE evidence still points to managed, low corn imports and diversified non-U.S. sourcing.

My stance is therefore: **support the prior card's warning that WASDE's corn bearishness is underpriced, but deny that China is the most likely one-quarter trigger. The correct hierarchy is U.S. weather first, Brazil second, China policy third.**
