# Major Grain Price Monitor

**Date anchor:** 2026-05-11, from local `date +%Y-%m-%d`. All references to “today,” “previous trading day,” and “this week” are resolved against this date.  
**Signal:** Neutral to cautious. Global grain futures are recovering modestly after last week’s selloff, but there is not yet evidence of a broad supply squeeze. Chinese cash prices are mixed: corn is slightly firmer, wheat and soybeans are softer, and rice is stable.

## Priority Conclusions

1. **CBOT grain futures are rebounding mildly today, with wheat carrying the highest risk beta.** The 2026-05-11 Farmbucks front-month snapshot shows corn at $4.6825/bu (+0.75%), soybeans at $11.8775/bu (+0.49%), and Chicago SRW wheat at $6.2450/bu (+0.12%). This looks like a repair move after last week’s fund liquidation rather than a confirmed uptrend.[1][2]
2. **Wheat is the first grain-risk monitor.** USDA NASS crop progress for the week ending 2026-05-03 shows U.S. winter wheat rated only 31% good/excellent, far below 51% a year earlier; poor/very poor totals 37%. FAO also reported a 0.8% month-on-month rise in world wheat prices in April, supported by drought in parts of the United States and risk of below-average rainfall in Australia.[4][5]
3. **Corn and soybeans still lack a clear trend-up catalyst.** U.S. corn planting is 38% complete versus a five-year average of 34%; soybean planting is 33% complete versus a five-year average of 23%. April WASDE also shows U.S. corn ending stocks at 2.127 billion bushels and soybean ending stocks at 350 million bushels, leaving supply cushions in place.[4][6]
4. **Rice is split between a firmer international futures market and a stable Chinese domestic market.** Investing.com shows the latest rough rice futures close, on 2026-05-08, at $12.160/cwt, up 1.25% on the day and up 8.2% from $11.235 on 2026-05-01. China’s NBS reported Japonica rice at RMB 4013.0/tonne in late April, unchanged from the prior ten-day period.[3][7]
5. **Chinese grain prices remain capped by policy grain sales and substitute feed grains.** NBS late-April data show corn at RMB 2324.0/tonne (+0.2%), wheat at RMB 2560.8/tonne (-0.5%), and soybeans at RMB 4446.8/tonne (-0.3%). Beijing’s Q1 corn market review highlights minimum-purchase-price wheat auctions, expectations for old rice sales, and incoming barley/sorghum/DDGS substitute supply as pressure points for domestic corn.[7][8]

## Follow-Up Items

- **May WASDE at 12:00 ET on 2026-05-12:** Watch 2026/27 new-crop acreage, yield assumptions, U.S. corn and soybean stocks, and whether global wheat stocks are revised lower.[9]
- **U.S. winter wheat rainfall and ratings:** If good/excellent stays below 35% while Kansas/Oklahoma/Texas dryness persists, wheat could shift from a repair rally to a real risk-premium move.
- **China’s new wheat quality and policy-auction clearing prices:** Poor new-crop wheat quality or firmer auction prices would affect wheat feed substitution and corn demand.
- **Rough rice futures versus Asian demand:** Rough rice is up 8.2% week over week, while Chinese Japonica rice is stable; confirm whether this is futures liquidity/cost pressure or a renewed trade-demand signal.
- **Feed-margin chain:** Soymeal is down and corn is slightly up; if hog and poultry margins remain weak, feed-grain demand elasticity will stay constrained.

## Bottom Line

The working framework should be **wheat first, corn and soybeans on watch, and rice split between domestic and offshore signals**. Near term, global grain futures are repairing, but global stocks and U.S. planting progress do not support a broad grain-price surge. The real upside triggers would be U.S. winter wheat crop loss, lower new-crop stocks in the May WASDE, or another leg higher in energy and transport costs that feeds into rice and grain trade costs. In China, food-security pricing remains stable; the main risks are wheat quality, corn substitution demand, and the pace of policy grain releases.
