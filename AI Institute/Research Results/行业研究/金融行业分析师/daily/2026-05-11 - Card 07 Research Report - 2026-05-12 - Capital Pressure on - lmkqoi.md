# Card 07 Research Report - 2026-05-12 - Capital Pressure on Second-Line European Energy Commodity PB

As of 2026-05-12, I stress-test and partly support Card 06: the weak link is not a public, all-at-once exit from energy commodity finance, but a Q3-Q4 2026 sequence of selective PB/FCM and commodity trade-finance retrenchment from semi-transparent mid-sized energy traders. The most vulnerable providers are UBS Switzerland, Natixis/BPCE, and Societe Generale; DNB is exposed but more strategically committed to Nordic ocean energy; Credit Agricole CIB, SEB, Nordea, and Danske are lower-probability exit candidates under the same shock.

**Stance**

Stress-test. Card 06 is right that the cross-sector contagion bridge runs through bank PB/FCM and commodity finance lines. I narrow the claim: full franchise exits are unlikely in 2026 because these banks still earn strategic client revenue in energy, real assets, and trade finance. The realistic capital event is a client-list and product-list withdrawal: non-renewing uncommitted facilities, raising initial-margin and LC haircuts, novating clearing to larger PBs, and moving medium-risk traders into run-off.

**Capital Trigger**

The capital pressure is credible even though system-wide capital ratios look comfortable. CRR3 has applied in the EU since 2025, with the output floor phased in from 50% in 2025 toward 72.5% by 2030; EBA's 2025 stress-test release says fully loaded CRR3 would create materially larger total risk exposure amounts for several banks even when transitional binding is limited. The FRTB own-funds date in the EU has been postponed to 2027, but banks still face reporting, model, and desk-allocation pressure through 2026. ECB 2026-28 supervision also emphasizes risk-based intensity, geopolitical shocks, ICAAP/ILAAP, and institution-specific stress testing.

For energy commodity PB, this matters because the same client can consume capital through four channels: short-term trade loans and borrowing-base facilities, LC and guarantee CCFs, commodity derivatives CVA/CCR exposure, and securities-financing or clearing balances. If TRP lifts documentation risk and source-verification risk, the bank's apparent short-tenor secured exposure becomes a higher-RWA, higher-operational-risk, lower-RoE client.

**Withdrawal Sequence**

1. Q3 2026: UBS screens first because its disclosed commodity trade-finance book is short-term and mostly uncommitted. The decision can be made without announcing a franchise exit: reduce country and client eligibility, add source-verification conditions, and stop rolling mid-tier traders with mixed cargo history.

2. Late Q3 2026: Natixis follows through portfolio optimization. The most likely move is to move from "energy and natural resources growth" to "clean-client growth only": retain investment-grade majors, infrastructure, renewables, and structured real-assets mandates, while shrinking semi-transparent physical traders.

3. Q4 2026: Societe Generale selectively tightens. SG's brand strength in commodity trade finance argues against a full exit, but capital return discipline and GBIS RoE targets make the low-RoE tail vulnerable. SG is likely to preserve large global traders and push medium-risk clients toward higher collateral and shorter tenors.

4. Q4 2026 into 2027: DNB and the Nordic banks tighten terms rather than withdraw. DNB has the largest strategic reason to stay with oil, gas, offshore, shipping, and power clients. Nordea, Danske, and SEB are more likely to run climate, sanctions, and documentation screens than to make a headline energy PB exit.

**Credit Shock to Mid-Sized Energy Traders**

The amplification mechanism is mechanical. Mid-sized energy traders do not need every bank to exit; they need one PB to stop rolling and a second bank to reprice. Under the stress case, a USD 1bn turnover trader with USD 250-350m working-capital needs loses USD 75-120m of bank capacity if one of four relationship banks exits, and loses USD 150-220m if it was effectively single-PB dependent. Advance rates on mixed-origin inventories fall from 80-85% to 65-75%, LC cash collateral moves from 0-10% toward 15-25%, and commodity-derivative IM add-ons rise 5-10pp.

That shifts the trader from a liquidity problem to a solvency signal. The immediate market price is a 200-350bp funding-cost increase and a 2-4% forced discount on cargoes that cannot be financed by a clean bank syndicate. For the weakest semi-transparent traders, Card 06's 12M PD shock from 1.5-3% to 6-10% looks conservative; if two PBs cut at the same time, 8-12% becomes plausible. The forced migration of flow to Vitol/Trafigura/Mercuria-style integrated majors also widens the commercial spread between clean, auditable molecules and opaque cargoes.

**Capital Release Map**

The fastest capital release comes from uncommitted facilities, not asset sales. UBS has the cleanest run-off lever because its disclosed commodity trade-finance limits are short-term and mostly uncommitted. Societe Generale and Natixis have stronger client-franchise reasons to keep headline capability, so they release capital by client-tiering: investment-grade majors stay, opaque mid-sized traders lose tenor, borrowing-base advance rates, and derivatives clearing support. Nordic banks release capital mainly through sector caps and climate/sanctions screens, not by exiting core energy clients.

For AT1 and senior-credit investors, the highest signal is not a press release titled "exit commodities." It is three smaller disclosures: lower average exposure at default in commodity trade finance, higher Stage 2 or watchlist migration for wholesale trade and transport clients, and management language about "capital-light" client selection in CIB. These are the likely Q3-Q4 2026 tells.

**Implications for the Thread**

This strengthens the TRP thesis. Satellite-data opacity and sanctions risk do not need to block physical oil flows directly. They raise the cost of proof; the proof cost raises bank capital consumption; the capital response removes PB capacity; and the capacity removal forces mid-sized traders to sell inventory, accept wider haircuts, or transfer flow to integrated majors. The blind spot is therefore not only where cargoes are, but which bank is still willing to finance them after the cargo history becomes expensive to verify.
