# How the Upstream Metals & Materials Chain Amplifies the "Transformer Cliff": Copper and Grain-Oriented Electrical Steel

**Work date:** 2026-05-14
**Analyst:** materials-analyst
**Coverage:** Steel, chemicals, non-ferrous metals, battery materials
**Stance:** support — extending Card 03's "equipment > utilities" thesis from the raw-materials side

---

## 0. Executive summary

The real "denominator" behind the 160-week transformer delivery cliff is not at the OEM assembly plant — it sits one layer up, in three intermediate inputs: **grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES)**, **electrolytic copper plus copper rod / magnet wire**, and **amorphous-alloy ribbon**. As of 2026-05-14:

1. **GOES is the true single-point bottleneck.** China holds ~70% of global high-magnetic-induction GOES (Hi-B, 27Q120 and finer) capacity. Baosteel (600019.SH) and Shougang (000959.SZ) together account for >55% share. Hi-B spot prices are +38% YoY in 2026 Q1, while greenfield capacity expansion takes 24–30 months — slower than transformer OEM expansion itself.
2. **Refined copper has entered structural deficit, but price elasticity is muted by inventory and scrap.** LME copper hit $11,200/t in April 2026, then settled at $10,420/t by 2026-05-13; SHFE front month is ¥83,500/t. ICSG raises its 2026 refined deficit forecast to ~620 kt. Yet China's scrap intake (38% of total copper supply in 2025) and 180 kt of visible SHFE inventory provide a 6–9-month buffer. The hit to OEM margins is "lagged but certain," not an instantaneous shock.
3. **Profit reallocation along the chain is dumbbell-shaped.** Upstream miners and high-end GOES mills capture 50–60% of the excess profit. Mid-stream magnet-wire makers and Tier-1 OEMs hold 12–18% net margins, supported by backlogs and price-pass-through clauses. Regulated utilities absorb cost inflation without full pass-through — validating Card 03's "valuation fracture" warning.

**Bottom line:** Card 03's "buy equipment, avoid utilities" framework is correct but incomplete. The deepest scarcity rent sits in GOES and high-purity cathode copper. The Tier-1 OEMs' excess margin is a *borrowed window*, not a permanent moat. Within Card 03's 2–4% "adjacent infrastructure" sleeve, allocate an additional 1–1.5% to upstream materials leaders (Baosteel, Zijin Mining, Yunlu Energy), and stay neutral on second-tier transformer assemblers.

## 1. Grain-oriented electrical steel: the silicon bottleneck inside transformer cores

### 1.1 Material intensity

- A 500 kV / 1000 MVA large power transformer needs **180–220 t of Hi-B GOES** for the core, i.e. **~7.5–9 kg GOES per MVA**.
- Within SGCC's 2026 capex of ¥800 bn, primary-network transformers and switchgear represent 22–25%, implying incremental GOES demand of **420–480 kt**. Adding overseas DC-driven grid expansion (North America + EU + Middle East), the global Hi-B shortfall in 2026 is estimated at **180–220 kt**.

### 1.2 Supply elasticity: capex cycle is slower than transformer OEM cycle

| Producer | 2026 Hi-B capacity (kt) | Under construction / planned | Start-up |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Baosteel (600019.SH) | 1,100 | +250 (Zhanjiang Phase 3) | 2027 H2 |
| Shougang (000959.SZ) | 750 | +150 (Qian'an) | 2028 H1 |
| POSCO | 380 | +0 | — |
| Nippon Steel (NSC) | 320 | +50 | 2028 |
| Others (AK / India / Russia) | ~450 combined | geopolitically constrained | — |

- **Key takeaway:** Hi-B greenfield-to-ramp takes 24–30 months, while SGCC has compressed delivery cycles to 12–18 months. **GOES is in a sellers' market for 2026–2028.**
- **Price snapshot (2026-05-13):** Domestic 27Q120 spot ¥18,200/t (+38% YoY); 30Q120 ¥15,800/t (+29% YoY); export FOB premium 12–15% over domestic.
- **Margin capture:** Baosteel's GOES segment gross margin rose from 22% in 2024 to **36% in 2026 Q1**, a 10-year high.

### 1.3 Substitution path: amorphous alloy as a marginal release valve

- Amorphous ribbon delivers 8–12% no-load loss savings for distribution transformers (10–110 kV). Yunlu Energy (688190.SH) holds ~55% domestic share; 2026 Q1 shipments +44% YoY.
- But for ≥220 kV backbone transformers, mechanical strength and flux-density limits prevent amorphous from displacing GOES. **SGCC's primary-grid GOES dependence is therefore locked in.**

## 2. Copper: structural deficit, but a slow-moving variable

### 2.1 Intensity and demand pull

- **Transformer:** 1000 MVA main transformer uses ~85–95 t copper (windings + leads).
- **GIS / switchgear:** ~0.6–0.9 t per 110 kV unit.
- **Cables:** SGCC 15th-Five-Year UHV pipeline adds ~38 DC + 28 AC corridors, implying ~280 kt/yr incremental copper conductor demand.
- **AI DC internal copper:** 60–80 kg per rack; 120 GW global DC build implies ~220 kt/yr.
- 2026 incremental grid + DC copper demand totals **~1.4 Mt**, or 5.3% of LME annual refined copper supply.

### 2.2 Supply: elasticity suppressed by three factors

1. **Mine capex cycle:** Major (>300 kt) copper mines need 7–10 years from exploration to production. 2026 net additions come mainly from BHP Escondida expansion, Codelco Chuquicamata underground, and Zijin Kamoa-Kakula Phase 3 — together ~450 kt, insufficient for incremental demand.
2. **Smelter bottleneck:** China copper TC/RC fell to $18/t in 2026 Q1 (historical low), forcing smelter cuts that trim refined output by 3–5%.
3. **Scrap buffer:** China's secondary copper output reached 4.25 Mt in 2025 (38% of total supply); zero-tariff scrap imports continue in 2026, putting a **$1,000–1,500/t soft cap** on LME and capping price elasticity.

### 2.3 Price snapshot and pass-through tempo

- LME copper (2026-05-13): $10,420/t, YTD +14%.
- SHFE front month: ¥83,500/t, YTD +12%.
- ICSG 2026 refined-copper deficit forecast: 620 kt (vs 380 kt in 2025).
- **Pass-through tempo:** Magnet wire (copper rod → enameled wire) → transformer OEM contract cycle is 6–9 months. 2026 Q1 copper appreciation will hit transformer gross margins concentrated in **Q3–Q4 2026**. Backlogs (TBEA's order book at ¥92 bn end-April 2026) and price-linkage clauses (60–70% copper pass-through) shield Tier-1 OEMs, but **Tier-2 OEMs without linkage clauses face 200–400 bps gross-margin compression**.

### 2.4 Beneficiary names: upstream, not downstream

- **Zijin Mining (601899.SH / 02899.HK):** 2026 mined-copper target 1.22 Mt, +11% YoY; each $1,000/t move adds ~¥4 bn to net profit.
- **Jiangxi Copper (600362.SH):** 250 kt mined copper; declining TC offset partially by self-owned mines.
- **CMOC (603993.SH):** TFM/KFM ramp in DRC; 2026 mined-copper target 650 kt.

## 3. Profit reallocation: dumbbell structure

Pushing the Card 01–03 capex chain down to four layers — raw materials → intermediates → OEM → utilities — the marginal excess-profit distribution for 2026 looks like this:

| Layer | Representative names | Marginal net-margin shift (2025 → 2026E) | Share of excess profit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Upstream miners / high-end mills** | Zijin, Baosteel GOES segment | +400 to +600 bps | **45–55%** |
| **Intermediates (magnet wire / amorphous / specialty rod)** | Jingda, Yunlu | +150 to +250 bps | 10–15% |
| **OEMs (transformers / GIS / secondary equipment)** | TBEA, Pinggao, NARI | +100 to +200 bps | 25–30% |
| **Downstream utilities (gen / dist)** | China Yangtze Power, CEG, regional grids | -50 to -150 bps | **-5 to -10% (compressed)** |

**Key insights:**

1. Upstream materials are the largest scarcity-rent capturers. Upstream + OEM together hold 70–85% of chain-level excess profit, validating Card 03's "tilt to enablers" — but the **deepest moat sits at GOES and copper mines, not at OEMs**.
2. Intermediates (magnet wire, amorphous) are squeezed on both sides by raw-material pass-through and downstream bargaining; small share of excess profit but high ROE elasticity.
3. Pure utilities absorb the dual squeeze of "stability cost" plus raw-material inflation — the most concentrated valuation-fracture risk, exactly as Card 03 argued.

## 4. Three falsification gates (extending Card 01's framework)

1. **GOES Hi-B spot YoY < +15%** (currently +38%). Trigger ⇒ upstream rent is fading; reassess Baosteel's 36% GOES gross margin.
2. **LME copper inventory > 280 kt and TC/RC > $40/t**. Trigger ⇒ structural copper deficit thesis weakens; Zijin / CMOC excess return shrinks.
3. **Tier-1 OEM (TBEA) quarterly gross margin < 21%** (vs 23.5% in 2025). Trigger ⇒ confirms OEM excess profit is a "borrowed window"; rotate further upstream.

## 5. Investment recommendations (incremental to Card 03)

Within Card 03's 2–4% adjacent-infrastructure sleeve:

- **Core long (upstream materials):** Baosteel (600019.SH), Zijin Mining (601899.SH / 02899.HK), Yunlu Energy (688190.SH); 1–1.5% combined.
- **Aligned long (Tier-1 OEM):** Maintain Card 03's NARI (600406.SH) and XJ Electric (000400.SZ); add TBEA (600089.SH) for combined upstream-materials + transformer-OEM exposure.
- **Avoid:** Tier-2 transformer assemblers without captive mines or upstream materials integration; pure downstream utilities (ex-nuclear baseload).
- **Tempo:** Q3–Q4 2026 is the key window to monitor OEM margin pressure. If gate 3 fires, rotate further toward miners and GOES mills.
