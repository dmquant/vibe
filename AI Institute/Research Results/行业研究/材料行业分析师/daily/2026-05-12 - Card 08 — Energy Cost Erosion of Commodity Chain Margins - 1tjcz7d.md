# Card 08 — Energy Cost Erosion of Commodity Chain Margins

- Date: 2026-05-12 (Asia/Singapore)
- Analyst: Materials Analyst
- Stance: stress-test
- Topic: Does USD 150/bbl oil erode chemicals and steel margins enough to nullify the 20% commodity allocation?

## Thesis
The 20% commodity allocation remains effective, but only because its 8% energy sleeve (upstream-heavy) and 4% gold sleeve carry the load. The remaining ~8% in industrial materials (steel, base metals, bulk chemicals) is **not** an unambiguous winner at USD 150/bbl — for energy-intensive midstream/downstream materials, cost-push exceeds price pass-through and margins compress. Allocators should treat the materials sleeve as **selective long upstream / short downstream**, not as a uniform inflation hedge.

## 1. Energy intensity by sub-industry (sanity-check)
Approximate energy-cost share of COGS at USD 80/bbl baseline:
- Ammonia / urea / methanol: 60–75% (natural-gas / coal feedstock)
- Ethylene (naphtha cracker): 50–65%
- Aluminum (smelting): 30–40% (power)
- Steel (BF-BOF integrated): 20–30% (coking coal + power)
- Steel (EAF): 35–45% (power + scrap)
- Cement: 30–40% (coal + power)
- Copper / zinc smelting: 15–25%
- Mining (upstream ore): 8–15%

At Brent USD 150/bbl with the FX overlay from card 04 (weaker JPY/KRW/INR), local-currency energy cost rises **70–110%** vs. 2024 baseline for Asian processors. Pass-through to product price is empirically capped at **40–60%** in the first two quarters during demand-soft regimes (see chief-economist card 03: central banks holding tight to anchor expectations → demand compression).

## 2. Stress test: USD 150/bbl held for 4 quarters
Estimated EBITDA-margin delta vs. 2024 baseline:

| Sub-segment | Price tailwind | Cost headwind | Net margin Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Upstream E&P (oil/gas) | +++ | + | **+800 to +1500 bps** |
| Iron ore / coking coal miners | ++ | + | +300 to +600 bps |
| Gold miners | ++ | + | +400 to +700 bps |
| Integrated BF-BOF steel (China) | + | ++ | -100 to -300 bps |
| EAF steel (US/EU) | + | +++ | -250 to -500 bps |
| Aluminum smelters (ex-hydro) | + | +++ | -400 to -700 bps |
| Bulk petchem (ethylene, PX) | + | +++ | -500 to -900 bps |
| Nitrogen fertilizer | ++ | +++ | -200 to -400 bps |
| Cement | 0 | ++ | -300 to -500 bps |
| Battery cathode/precursor | 0 | ++ | -150 to -350 bps |

Spread implication: the **upstream vs. downstream materials spread widens by 600–1500 bps**. A flat-weighted materials index would deliver close to zero alpha — the average masks a violent dispersion.

## 3. Does this nullify the 20% commodity weight?
No, but it reframes it. The asset-allocator (card 06) sleeve breaks down as:
- 8% energy → dominantly upstream E&P, USD-denominated. **Validated** by card 07.
- 4% gold → policy-credibility and real-rate hedge. **Validated.**
- ~8% industrial / agri commodities → this is where the stress test bites.

Recommendations to keep the 20% sleeve net-additive:
1. **Tilt the industrial-commodity 8% toward upstream miners and physical/futures exposure** (iron ore, copper concentrate, coking coal, gold), not downstream processors.
2. **Avoid equity exposure to integrated petchem and EAF steel** as a "commodity proxy" — they are short energy, not long commodity.
3. **Pair-trade inside materials**: long upstream miners / short bulk petchem and aluminum smelters captures the 600–1500 bps spread.
4. **China-specific caveat**: BF-BOF steelmakers benefit from administrative price floors and coking-coal domestic supply — less hurt than EAF peers; this is the only downstream long worth carrying, and only in CNY terms.

## 4. Risks to this stress test
- **Demand destruction**: If USD 150/bbl triggers a sharper global recession than card 03 assumes, even upstream miners lose 20–30% on volume — the 8% energy hedge still works (price elasticity > volume), but the 8% industrial sleeve goes negative.
- **Subsidy / price-cap regimes**: EU energy-price caps, China dual-control relaxation, or Indian fertilizer subsidies can transfer the cost shock to sovereigns and partially rescue downstream margins — softens the dispersion thesis.
- **Substitution lag**: Naphtha→ethane switching, EAF→DRI-H2 transitions are multi-year; the stress test holds over 4–8 quarters.

## 5. Verdict on the 20% commodity allocation
**Support, conditional on composition.** The 20% weight is the right number for a stagflation hedge; the composition must lean upstream and metal-physical, not equity-of-processors. Card 06's sleeve design already does this — the stress test confirms it rather than overturns it. The line that *would* fail is a naïve "materials equity index" expression of the same 20%.
