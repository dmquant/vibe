# Card 04 - Raw-Material Stress Test for the Grid-Equipment Order Book

- Board: 3e77be32-74ee-4bcf-9cd4-59a682e84fbf · Card 4/8
- Analyst: Materials Analyst (materials-analyst)
- Stance: **stress-test**
- Work date: **2026-05-12** (Asia/Singapore)
- Root topic: AI infrastructure × fossil-energy "pragmatic marriage"

As of **2026-05-12**, I support Card 03's conclusion that grid hardware is the binding AI-infrastructure constraint, but I narrow the next bottleneck: the raw-material layer is **not uniformly short**. Aluminium and base cable polymers can physically scale. Copper is tight enough to create price rationing and regional allocation. The material most likely to become the next **true physical bottleneck** is **grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES), especially high-permeability grades for large power transformer cores**, because it is small, qualified, hard to substitute, and directly gates HVPT/GSU shipments.

## 1. Executive Judgment

The implied 2025-2030 global equipment order book in Card 03 - HVPT, GSU transformers, HV cables, switchgear and HVDC converter stations - can be delivered only with a **material priority stack**:

1. **GOES / transformer-core steel is the hard bottleneck.** It is the only material on this list where a shortage can stop a transformer shipment even if the buyer pays more. DOE's U.S. grid supply-chain report identifies GOES and LPT manufacturing as the two weakest LPT supply-chain segments, and notes only one U.S. GOES manufacturer with limits in high-quality domestic supply. CRS similarly records that roughly **95% of U.S. transformer cores use GOES**, while new capacity typically needs **3-5 years** to relieve the shortage. This is a physical bottleneck, not just a commodity-cycle problem.
2. **Copper is the broadest price and procurement bottleneck.** IEA's 2025 copper outlook puts total demand at **26.7 Mt in 2024** and **31.3 Mt in 2030**, with clean-tech demand rising from **7.7 Mt** to **10.9 Mt**. Data centres alone could require **250-550 kt Cu in 2030**, about **1-2%** of global demand. Copper will not usually be the single part that stops a transformer factory, but it can raise project costs, force conductor substitution, and tighten CTC copper wire for transformer windings.
3. **HV cable insulation is a system-capacity bottleneck, not a resin bottleneck.** XLPE / thermoplastic insulation compounds are not globally scarce in the way GOES is; the bottleneck is qualified high-voltage extrusion, vertical continuous vulcanisation lines, joints, accessories, test bays and cable-laying capacity. IEA finds cable procurement now takes **2-3 years**, while direct-current cable lead times can exceed **5 years**.
4. **Aluminium can scale physically, but not without energy and regional-policy friction.** IAI/CRU expects aluminium demand to rise almost **40% by 2030**, requiring an additional **33.3 Mt**, with the electrical sector needing **+5.2 Mt**. That is meaningful, but aluminium has a wider production base, higher substitution flexibility in overhead lines, and stronger scrap loops than GOES.
5. **Magnet materials are a geopolitical tail risk, not the base-case grid-equipment bottleneck.** IEA says magnet rare-earth demand has doubled since 2015 and rises another third by 2030, while China had **91%** of refined output and **94%** of sintered permanent-magnet production in 2024. That matters for industrial motors, EVs and wind, but the HVPT/HV cable/HVDC order book is not magnet-intensive enough for magnets to outrank GOES or copper.

**Bottom line:** if the question is "which metal moves price and portfolio factor exposure?", answer **copper**. If the question is "which material actually prevents the order book from converting into shipped transformers?", answer **GOES**.

## 2. Stress-Test Matrix

| Material | 2025-2030 deliverability against the grid-equipment order book | Bottleneck type | Judgment |
|---|---:|---|---|
| GOES / HiB electrical steel | **60-75% ex-China without longer transformer queues; 80-90% globally if China/Japan/Korea supply remains open** | Qualified specialty steel, mill capacity, transformer-core qualification | **True bottleneck** |
| Copper / CTC copper wire | **70-85% without substitution; 85-95% with aluminium/HVDC design changes and price rationing** | Mine supply, refining, CTC wire, cable conductor allocation | **Price bottleneck, secondary physical risk** |
| HV cable insulation systems | **70-85% for HVDC/high-voltage cable systems; >90% for base polymer feedstock** | VCV extrusion, testing, accessories, installation vessels | **System bottleneck, not resin bottleneck** |
| Aluminium conductor / busbar | **85-95%** | Power cost, ex-China smelter economics, conductor-grade rod capacity | **Manageable** |
| Magnet materials | **80-95% for grid equipment, lower if China export licensing tightens** | Rare-earth refining and magnet concentration | **Geopolitical tail risk** |

This ranking differs from a simple tonnage screen. Aluminium and copper dominate tonnage, but **GOES dominates criticality** because the transformer cannot be redesigned quickly around a missing core material.

## 3. Copper: Large, Tight, But Still Price-Rationable

Copper is the most visible beneficiary of the AI-power buildout because it sits in every layer: data-centre busbars and PDUs, substation conductors, transformer windings, underground cables, subsea cables and HVDC lines.

The scale is material. IEA projects total copper demand rising from **26.7 Mt in 2024** to **31.3 Mt in 2030** under STEPS, with clean-tech demand rising from **7.7 Mt** to **10.9 Mt**. IEA's 2025 critical-minerals work also estimates **250-550 kt** of copper demand from conventional and AI data centres in 2030. Against this, Card 03's global grid-equipment order book likely adds **0.7-1.1 Mt/yr** of copper demand by 2030 versus a pre-AI grid baseline, including transformer windings, HV cables and substation copper.

The stress-test result is **tight but not fatal**:

- **Substitution exists.** IEA notes that increasing aluminium's share in underground/subsea cable could reduce 2040 copper demand by **3.6 Mt**, while wider HVDC uptake could cut combined copper and aluminium demand by **4 Mt / 15%** in the SDS.
- **Recycling helps, but cannot solve the timing gap.** IEA's 2025 copper outlook has secondary supply and reuse rising from **4.4 Mt in 2024** to **5.4 Mt in 2030**, useful but smaller than the increase in total demand.
- **The constrained sub-product is CTC copper wire, not generic cathode.** Transformer manufacturers need qualified continuously transposed conductor, insulation systems and winding capacity; cathode availability alone does not equal HVPT availability.

**Investment read:** copper miners, smelters, recyclers and cable makers keep structural pricing power, but copper is more likely to ration marginal projects by price than to become the first hard stop in AI-grid equipment.

## 4. Aluminium: The Relief Valve, With Energy Friction

Aluminium is the natural release valve for copper in overhead lines, selected underground/submarine sections, busbars and some transformer applications. IEA's grid-material work shows aluminium demand for electricity grids rising from **9 Mt in 2020** to **12.8 Mt in 2040** in STEPS and **16 Mt** in SDS. IAI/CRU expects total aluminium demand to rise almost **40% by 2030**, from **86.2 Mt** to **119.5 Mt**, with the electrical sector requiring **+5.2 Mt**.

The physical supply curve is more elastic than copper's because smelter restarts, scrap remelting and conductor redesign can respond faster than new copper mines or GOES mills. The constraint is not geology; it is **electricity cost and carbon policy**. Ex-China smelting remains power-price sensitive, Europe has already lost meaningful primary capacity, and tariff regimes can create local shortages even when global metal exists.

For the order book, aluminium is **deliverable**. It becomes a margin and regional-sourcing issue, not the next true bottleneck.

## 5. GOES: The Most Likely "True Bottleneck"

GOES is the uncomfortable answer because it is less traded and less visible than copper. But it is exactly where the physics of the order book is least forgiving.

Large power transformers, GSUs and distribution transformers require low-loss magnetic cores. For high-voltage and high-MVA units, the relevant product is not generic electrical steel; it is qualified GOES, often high-permeability / HiB grade, with strict loss, thickness, coating and processing requirements. DOE identifies GOES suppliers and LPT manufacturing as the weakest links in the LPT chain. CRS records that roughly **95%** of U.S. transformer cores use GOES and that distribution-transformer capacity relief may require **3-5 years**.

Three features make GOES different from copper:

- **Substitution is limited.** Amorphous metal can help some distribution transformers, but it is not a quick general substitute for large HVPT/GSU units. Non-oriented electrical steel serves motors and EVs; it is not a like-for-like core material for HVPT.
- **Qualification is slow.** A transformer OEM cannot simply switch to an unqualified mill for a 500 kV or 765 kV unit. Loss guarantees, acoustic performance, thermal behaviour and long-life reliability must be validated.
- **Trade barriers amplify the shortage.** The U.S. has one domestic GOES producer, while imported cores, laminations and finished transformers remain politically sensitive. That makes the U.S. shortage worse than the global tonnage balance.

**Stress conclusion:** GOES is the material most likely to convert Card 03's "sold-out transformer backlog" into actual shipment delay. If transformer lead times stay at **120-210 weeks** into 2028, GOES and transformer factory test capacity are the upstream causes to monitor first.

## 6. HV Cable Insulation: Base Polymer Is Fine; Qualified Cable Systems Are Not

High-voltage cable constraints are often described as "XLPE shortage", but that is too crude. The scarce asset is not polyethylene feedstock in general; it is **qualified high-voltage cable-system capacity**: clean-room compound handling, vertical continuous vulcanisation, degassing, testing, joints, terminations and cable-lay logistics.

IEA's 2025 transmission-grid report states that cable procurement takes **2-3 years**, direct-current cables can exceed **5 years**, and cable costs have nearly doubled since 2019. Prysmian's 2025-2026 disclosures show why capacity, not resin, is the constraint: it invested **over EUR 200 million** in the Pikkala HV submarine-cable expansion, capable of producing around **1 km/day**, and its 525 kV HVDC insulation technology lifts a single link from **2.0 GW** to **2.5 GW** at higher operating temperature.

For materials investors, this means the value accrues less to base polymer producers and more to **Prysmian, Nexans, NKT, Sumitomo Electric, LS Cable, ZTT/Hengtong-style cable systems, accessory suppliers and testing capacity**. Insulation compounds are necessary, but the bottleneck rent sits in certified cable systems.

## 7. Magnet Materials: Strategic, But Not This Order Book's First Constraint

Rare-earth magnets are a major strategic fragility. IEA says demand for magnet rare earth elements - Nd, Pr, Dy and Tb - doubled since 2015 and will rise another third by 2030. China held **60%** of magnet rare-earth mining, **91%** of refining and **94%** of sintered permanent-magnet production in 2024. China also imposed export controls on some medium and heavy rare earths on **2025-04-04** and announced broader rare-earth controls on **2025-10-09**, raising licensing risk for strategic sectors.

Still, this card's order book is not as magnet-intensive as EVs, robotics or direct-drive wind turbines. HVPT and cable systems use copper, aluminium, steel, insulation and oil. HVDC converter stations use semiconductors, cooling, transformers, reactors and control systems; magnets are present in actuators, auxiliary motors, sensors and some switchgear, but they are not the dominant material.

**Stress conclusion:** magnets are a geopolitical supply-chain risk for the broader AI-industrial complex, but for the 2025-2030 grid-equipment backlog they rank below GOES, copper and HV cable-system capacity.

## 8. Regional Read-Through

**United States:** the raw-material problem is most acute. The U.S. has limited domestic GOES, heavy dependence on imported transformer content, and tariff uncertainty around steel, aluminium and copper products. Copper can be imported and recycled; GOES/laminations and finished HVPT are harder because qualification, origin rules and utility procurement conservatism interact.

**China:** China has the best chance to physically deliver the order book because transformer OEMs, cable makers, aluminium smelters and electrical-steel producers sit inside the same industrial system. The vulnerability is not domestic shipment; it is export controls and geopolitical access for non-China buyers.

**Europe:** Europe is exposed through HVDC cable capacity, distribution upgrade volume and high power costs for aluminium. JRC's 2026 European grid-material brief estimates 2025-2030 planned additions of **7,269 km** AC transmission routes, **11,155 km** DC cable routes and **429 transformers**, with copper demand for the limited transmission-grid scope rising from **4.3 kt in 2025** to **324 kt in 2030** and polymers for DC cables totaling **392 kt** over 2025-2030. That is a conservative scope and already points to copper and cable-system intensity.

- **Overweight with highest conviction:** GOES / electrical steel and transformer-content beneficiaries: **CLF**, **Nippon Steel (5401.T)**, **JFE Holdings (5411.T)**, **POSCO Holdings (005490.KS)** where product mix is exposed to electrical steel; downstream **GEV**, **Hitachi (6501.T)**, **Siemens Energy (ENR.DE)**, **Hyundai Electric**, **TBEA (600089)**, **XJ Electric (000400)**, **Sieyuan Electric (002028)**.

## 10. Handoff

The materials conclusion sharpens Card 03 rather than overturning it: **the grid-equipment backlog is real, but conversion risk moves upstream into GOES and qualified HV cable / transformer systems**. The next unanswered question is not another raw-material screen; it is whether equipment OEMs can turn this scarcity into margin and cash-flow conversion instead of being squeezed by input costs and liquidated damages.

Recommended next analyst: **industrials-analyst** - stress-test grid-equipment OEM backlog quality, pricing power, margin pass-through and capacity expansion for HVPT, HVDC, switchgear and cable-system suppliers.
