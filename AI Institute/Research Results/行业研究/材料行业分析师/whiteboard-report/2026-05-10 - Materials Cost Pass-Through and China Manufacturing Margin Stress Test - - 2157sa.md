# Materials Cost Pass-Through and China Manufacturing Margin Stress Test - 2026-05-11
**Date:** 2026-05-11 (Asia/Singapore)  
**Analyst:** materials-analyst | Materials Analyst  
**Stance:** stress-test  

As of 2026-05-11, I stress-test the prior card's energy-cost thesis and narrow it: the second energy pulse can protect margins for selected upstream and scarcity-linked intermediate producers, but it is more likely to squeeze China's broad manufacturing margins than to create a clean reflation cycle. The decisive signal is that China's input-price gauges are rising faster than output-price gauges, while late-April spot prices are mixed rather than uniformly rising.

## Executive Summary
The energy pulse is strong enough to lift the raw-material cost floor. EIA's April 7, 2026 STEO expected Brent to peak near $115/b in 2Q26, diesel to exceed $5.80/gal in April, and the Brent-WTI spread to peak at $15/b as Middle East supply and shipping disruptions tightened seaborne markets. EIA's April 28 LNG note showed TTF at $14.80/MMBtu and JKM at $16.02/MMBtu in the week ending April 24, respectively 35% and 51% above pre-closure levels, while Henry Hub fell 9%. World Bank's April 28 Commodity Markets Outlook put the 2026 energy-price surge at 24%, total commodity prices at 16%, fertilizer prices at 31%, and urea at 60%.

For materials, this does not mean every producer can pass through. China's April manufacturing PMI showed the main raw-material purchase price index at 63.7 and the producer price index at 55.1, leaving an 8.6-point diffusion-index gap. That gap was almost unchanged from March's 8.5 points (63.9 versus 55.4), so the evidence points to persistent but incomplete pass-through. NBS late-April circulation prices reinforce the point: LNG rose 9.4%, coke 3.0%, hot-rolled sheet 1.8%, rebar 1.6%, copper 0.7%, zinc 1.2%, lithium iron phosphate 2.8%, urea 0.5%, and monoammonium phosphate 1.3%; but polyethylene fell 2.1%, polypropylene 1.0%, pure benzene 1.2%, polyester filament 4.0%, aluminum 0.7%, cement 1.3%, glass 1.8%, and polysilicon 0.3%.

The base case is margin redistribution, not broad margin preservation. Non-ferrous metals, selected fertilizers, coke and energy-linked intermediates have the best near-term pricing power. Ferrous steel and chemicals are more fragile because China still has excess capacity, property-linked demand remains weak, and export customers can resist price increases. Downstream manufacturers in automobiles, machinery, textiles, plastics, packaging and battery supply chains face the clearest squeeze.

## China Materials View
**Steel:** The late-April steel price bounce looks more like cost-led inventory pricing than demand-led margin recovery. Rebar and hot-rolled sheet were up 1.6% and 1.8% from April 11-20, while coke rose 3.0%. Yet NBS Q1 profits show ferrous smelting and pressing turned from profit to loss, with profit at -RMB 33.4 billion and profit growth at -153.1%. This is the clearest warning that higher intermediate prices can coexist with poor mill margins when demand elasticity and capacity pressure remain high.

**Chemicals:** Chemicals are the most important test for whether energy costs can be passed into global goods prices. March PPI showed raw chemical material purchase prices up 2.9% m/m, and Q1 profits in raw chemical materials and chemical products rose 54.5% y/y. But late-April spot data show PE, PP, pure benzene, butadiene rubber, polyester filament and glacial acetic acid all falling, with acetic acid down 21.8%. The interpretation is spread volatility, not durable pricing power. Upstream or contracted producers can benefit; commodity-like downstream polymers and fibers are exposed.

**Non-ferrous metals:** Non-ferrous is the strongest pass-through pocket. NBS March PPI showed nonferrous metals and cables purchase prices up 22.3% y/y, and Q1 non-ferrous smelting/pressing profits rose 116.7%. World Bank also highlighted record-high prices for several base metals. This is the part of the materials complex where global scarcity, energy intensity and dollar pricing give Chinese producers or processors more ability to protect margins, though aluminum's late-April -0.7% move shows even this pocket is not uniform.

**Battery and new-energy materials:** The signal is split. Lithium iron phosphate rose 2.8% in late April, suggesting battery cathode materials can regain some pricing traction when downstream EV and storage demand holds. Polysilicon still fell 0.3%, pointing to persistent solar-chain overcapacity. Energy inflation alone cannot fix a capacity glut; it raises cash costs and accelerates shutdown pressure for high-cost producers.

## Stress-Test Answer
The energy pulse can maintain industrial margins only under three conditions: the product is globally priced, supply is constrained, and downstream demand is either policy-supported or inventory-driven. Non-ferrous metals, some fertilizers, coke and selected intermediates meet parts of this test. Broad Chinese manufacturing does not.

The strongest evidence is the April PMI pricing wedge. Input-price diffusion at 63.7 against output-price diffusion at 55.1 means firms are raising selling prices, but not enough to offset input inflation. The wedge is especially relevant because production was still expanding at 51.5 and new orders were at 50.6, so the pressure is not yet a recession-only artifact. If orders slip below 50 while the input index stays above 60, margin compression becomes the dominant channel.

The global read-through is also important for the session's root topic. China's export deflation channel weakens when energy, freight, metals and fertilizers lift the floor for intermediate goods. But if Chinese manufacturers cannot pass costs fully, the global effect is not a clean goods-inflation impulse. It is a mix of narrower exporter margins, more selective price increases, and weaker industrial capex appetite. That combination supports the prior "inflation persistence with cooling labor demand" macro thesis without requiring a full China demand boom.

## Investment and Sector Implications
- **Overweight relative pricing power:** non-ferrous metals, selected fertilizers, coke and high-quality chemical intermediates with feedstock access or contracted pass-through.
- **Underweight margin takers:** steel mills without cost advantage, commodity polymers, chemical fibers, glass, cement, polysilicon and downstream manufacturers with export-price resistance.
- **Watch the next data points:** China April PPI after release, May PMI input/output-price wedge, Brent and LNG spot normalization, and whether late-April chemical price declines reverse.
- **Macro implication:** The China channel supports sticky goods costs at the global margin, but the domestic equity implication is dispersion rather than a broad materials beta rally.
