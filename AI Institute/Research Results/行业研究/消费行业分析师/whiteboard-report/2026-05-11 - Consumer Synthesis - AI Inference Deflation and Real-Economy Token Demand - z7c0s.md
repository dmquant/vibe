# Consumer Synthesis - AI Inference Deflation and Real-Economy Token Demand
**Work Date:** 2026-05-11  
**Analyst:** consumer-analyst (Consumer Analyst)  
**Stance:** synthesize  
**Scope:** Baijiu, F&B, retail, branded consumer goods

As of **2026-05-11**, I synthesize and support the prior line of reasoning: cheaper inference should be treated as a demand accelerator, not only as a cost-saving event. From a consumer-sector perspective, the missing bridge between DeepSeek V4-style unit-cost deflation and AI infrastructure capex is simple: retailers, restaurants and brand owners operate millions of low-value, high-frequency decisions where AI was previously too expensive or too slow. When inference cost falls, usage does not rise linearly. It spreads from head-office experiments into every basket, shelf, menu, promotion, store shift and customer-service interaction.

## Core Judgment

The consumer read-through **supports the Jevons thesis** in Cards 01 and 07, with one refinement. The strongest downstream demand for inference is not premium baijiu or low-frequency branded-consumer storytelling. It is transaction-layer retail, QSR/F&B operations, local promotion, demand forecasting, replenishment, search, customer service and agentic shopping. These are "token sink" workflows: small value per decision, enormous decision count, and high tolerance for automation once the unit cost falls.

This means the AI physical-infrastructure thesis is less dependent on frontier-model training alone. Consumer adoption creates a broad inference floor under demand, while power availability and time-to-power decide where that demand can be served. The prior 40% industrial / 35% TMT / 25% utilities framework remains intact; consumer equities should be viewed as **second-order adopters** of inference deflation, not as replacements for the AI physical-infrastructure allocation.

## Sector Map: Where the Elasticity Actually Sits

### Retail

Retail is the cleanest consumer-sector validation of nonlinear inference demand. A large retailer has millions of SKU-store-day decisions, plus search, ads, substitutions, customer chats, fraud checks, returns, route planning and markdown optimization. The unit economics do not require AI to create a new category; it only needs to improve basket conversion, stockout rates, gross margin or labor productivity by small increments.

The most credible use cases are:

- Real-time product search, recommendations and agentic shopping.
- Store-level replenishment and shrink detection.
- Dynamic markdowns and local promotion design.
- Customer service automation, returns triage and loyalty segmentation.
- Creative generation for product pages, livestream scripts and ad variants.

For scaled retailers, the realistic 2026-2027 benefit is not a dramatic revenue surge. It is a **30-80 bp** operating-margin improvement for leaders that already have clean data, closed-loop transaction systems and digital traffic. The compute usage, however, can rise faster than the P&L benefit because each customer interaction can trigger multiple model calls.

### F&B and QSR

F&B and QSR are the second-highest-elasticity consumer vertical. The industry has frequent transactions, labor scheduling complexity, volatile demand, delivery-platform data and localized menu economics. AI inference is likely to be used in drive-through voice ordering, kiosk upsell, kitchen forecasting, delivery timing, menu engineering and franchisee compliance.

The important point for the board thesis is that many of these use cases are latency-sensitive and repetitive. A voice-ordering system or delivery-routing agent consumes inference continuously during operating hours, not once per quarter. That is exactly the kind of workload that turns lower token cost into higher token volume.

### Branded Consumer Goods

For packaged food, beverages, cosmetics and household goods, the demand effect is more selective. AI can lower the cost of content generation, promotion testing, customer insight, product claims compliance and demand sensing. But many brand owners lack first-party transaction data, so retailers and platforms may capture more of the AI economics than manufacturers.

The best-positioned brand owners are those with direct-to-consumer data, loyalty membership, high ad spend, and fast innovation cycles. The weaker group is offline-heavy brands that rely on distributors and cannot close the loop from content to sell-through.

### Baijiu

Baijiu is the least direct AI beneficiary among the consumer categories. Premium baijiu demand is driven by income expectations, gifting occasions, channel inventory, pricing discipline and social consumption, not by recommendation-engine efficiency. AI can help with dealer inventory monitoring, counterfeit detection, consumer CRM, banquet-scene targeting and distributor credit risk. It is a margin and channel-control tool, not a demand-cycle reset.

This distinction matters. A weak Chinese consumption cycle can coexist with rising AI inference demand. Cheaper inference does not make consumers buy more high-end baijiu; it helps platforms and brand owners allocate traffic, inventory and promotion with less waste. Therefore, consumer-sector evidence supports the AI capex thesis through usage intensity, not through a blanket bullish call on all consumer stocks.

## Portfolio Implications

I would not reduce the board's AI physical-infrastructure allocation to make room for broad consumer exposure. The stronger conclusion is:

- Keep the core AI infrastructure framework: **40% industrial / 35% TMT / 25% utilities**.
- Add a separate watchlist of inference-adopter consumer names rather than treating consumer as a fourth infrastructure sleeve.
- Prefer retailers, QSRs and platforms with first-party data, high digital traffic, dense SKU or order complexity, and proven automation rollout.
- Be more cautious on baijiu and traditional offline branded goods, where AI improves execution but does not solve end-demand weakness.

In consumer equities, the investable signal should be evidence of closed-loop deployment: AI tied to conversion, replenishment, markdown, labor or customer-service KPIs. AI marketing language without transaction data should get little valuation credit.

## Risks and Disconfirming Evidence

The first risk is adoption friction. Consumer companies often have fragmented POS systems, messy product data, weak privacy controls, and franchisee execution gaps. Lower inference cost cannot fix bad data architecture.

The second risk is value capture. Hyperscalers and software vendors may capture much of the productivity gain through pricing, leaving consumer companies with modest margin benefit even as they increase token consumption.

The third risk is macro demand. In weak consumption categories such as premium baijiu, AI can improve channel discipline but cannot manufacture gifting occasions or income confidence.

The fourth risk is compliance and brand safety. Personalized pricing, generated claims, consumer-data use and automated service decisions can create regulatory or reputational risk, especially in food, alcohol, cosmetics and cross-border retail.

## Final Synthesis

The consumer-sector evidence strengthens the board's conclusion. AI inference efficiency creates a Jevons-style effect because consumer businesses have vast numbers of small, repeated decisions that become economical only when model calls are cheap. The direct winners are not necessarily consumer stocks; the clearer beneficiaries remain hyperscalers, inference silicon, power equipment, grid infrastructure and utilities that enable the workload. Consumer companies are the downstream proof that the workload exists.

**Final stance:** synthesize and support. The 8-15% power-chain TCO penalty is a tax on deployment geography, not a cap on demand. Consumer adoption makes the demand side broader, more repetitive and more inference-heavy, which reinforces the AI physical-infrastructure thesis through 2026-2027.
