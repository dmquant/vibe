# 2026-05-14 Bond Analyst Card 03: U.S. Treasury Curve Dynamics and HY OAS Credit Transmission

As of 2026-05-14 Asia/Singapore, I stress-test the prior macro line and keep the same base case: a 10Y Treasury breakout is first a valuation and duration shock, not yet a material credit shock. The condition that would change the call is not 10Y alone. It is a joint move in 10Y, 2Y, volatility, and HY OAS, with HY OAS moving above 325-350 bp as the warning zone and closer to 400 bp as the point where the market is pricing a genuine default/refinancing shock.

## Core Judgment

The current curve and spread setup is uncomfortable but not yet credit-crisis pricing. The latest daily observations available through the official/FRED rate and ICE spread series show 2Y at 4.00%, 10Y at 4.46%, 30Y at 5.03%, 10Y TIPS real yield at 1.99%, 10Y breakeven at 2.47%, HY OAS at 2.82%, HY effective yield at 6.97%, IG OAS at 0.77%, and VIX at 17.99. That is a rate shock with unusually tight spreads, not a credit shock.

I therefore support the prior cards' stress-test direction but narrow the transmission rule: a bear steepener led by 10Y/30Y should hit equity multiples, long-duration assets, MBS convexity, bank securities marks, and refinancing math before it forces broad HY spread widening. HY becomes the main market-break channel only if the front end participates. A 2Y move through 4.25% would mean the market is pricing renewed Fed tightening, not just term premium. If that happens together with 10Y above 4.75% and HY OAS above 325-350 bp, the credit impulse becomes self-reinforcing.

The important comparison is March 31, not only year-end. HY OAS was 3.28% on 2026-03-31, versus 2.82% on 2026-05-12. Credit has tightened since the spring stress even as 10Y rose from 4.30% to 4.46%. That divergence is the central reason I do not treat the current bear-steepening discussion as an active HY shock.

## Curve Regimes

**Regime 1: Bear steepener led by 10Y/30Y.**  
This is the current base case. If 10Y breaks 4.60%-4.75% while 2Y remains near 4.00%-4.15%, the market is charging more term premium and inflation uncertainty, not necessarily forcing an immediate Fed hike. The transmission is duration first: growth equity multiples, REITs, utilities, MBS duration hedging, bank AFS/HTM marks, and housing affordability. HY spreads can lag because nominal growth and earnings dollars remain resilient.

**Regime 2: Parallel selloff or bear flattening led by 2Y.**  
This is the faster credit-transmission case. If 2Y breaks 4.25% while 10Y is above 4.60%-4.75%, investors are pricing Fed policy risk rather than only term premium. That raises cash yields, tightens financial conditions through the dollar and front-end funding, and weakens the carry cushion in lower-quality credit. In this regime, HY OAS should be watched as a live trigger, not a lagging indicator.

**Regime 3: Long-end disorder with 30Y above 5.10%-5.25%.**  
This would be a balance-sheet stress regime. It may not immediately explode HY OAS, but it would pressure mortgages, MBS convexity, private-credit marks, insurance duration matching, and bank unrealized losses. The credit channel would be slower and more collateral/funding driven.

## HY OAS Transmission

HY OAS is not just a sentiment gauge. It is the market price for downgrade risk, liquidity risk, default risk, and the option value of avoiding refinancing. The reason I use 325-350 bp as the warning zone is that it would represent a clear break from the current 282 bp level while still being early enough to matter before recession pricing is obvious. A move toward 400 bp would be a different message: the market would be saying the rate shock is no longer just a discount-rate problem.

The current all-in HY yield already matters. HY effective yield is 6.97%, up 44 bp from 2025-12-31. If 10Y rises another 30 bp and HY OAS merely holds, the all-in HY yield can move into the low 7s without a spread accident. That is painful for refinancing but not yet a shock. The shock begins when the Treasury leg and the spread leg rise together: for example, 10Y above 4.75%, 2Y above 4.25%, HY OAS above 350 bp, VIX above 22-25, and HY effective yield moving toward 7.50%-8.00%.

## Stress-Test Answer

Bear steepening alone is unlikely to trigger a material credit shock from here. It can damage equities, duration assets, and balance-sheet marks, and it can make refinancing harder for the weakest issuers. But with HY OAS at 2.82%, IG OAS at 0.77%, and VIX below 20 on the latest observation, the credit market is still behaving as if nominal growth and carry are adequate.

The more dangerous path is a two-step sequence:

1. 10Y breaks 4.60%-4.75% and 30Y holds above 5.00%, pressuring valuation and duration balance sheets.
2. 2Y then follows through above 4.25%, confirming the Fed-hike tail and forcing HY OAS through 325-350 bp.

If only step 1 happens, I would call it a valuation shock. If both steps happen, I would call it a credit-transmission warning. If HY OAS approaches 400 bp and HY effective yield moves toward 8.00%, I would upgrade the language to an active credit shock.

## Portfolio Implications

The practical risk control is to avoid using a single 10Y level as the whole signal. For equities, 10Y above 4.60% is enough to reduce tolerance for high-duration multiples. For bonds, the current curve argues against adding long duration aggressively until the 30Y stops cheapening or inflation breakevens stabilize. For credit, the cleaner trigger is spread-based: trim lower-quality HY exposure if OAS closes above 325-350 bp with 2Y above 4.25%; move from carry to capital-preservation mode if OAS moves toward 400 bp.

In sector terms, this is not yet a blanket default cycle. The first pain should sit in issuers with near-term maturities, weak free cash flow, floating-rate debt, and refinancing dependence. The broader index only becomes fragile when spreads, front-end rates, and equity volatility confirm the same tightening story.
